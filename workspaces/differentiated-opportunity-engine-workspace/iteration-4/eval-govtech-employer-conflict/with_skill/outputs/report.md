- Skills and domain experience: Deep familiarity with municipal procurement
  waste points, govtech buying friction, pricing sensitivities, and workflow
  bottlenecks from working inside procurement software.
- Assets and access advantages: Strong pattern recognition about where mid-sized
  cities lose money; ability to spot recurring workflow failures quickly.
- Distribution and network leverage: Weak usable distribution for a side
  business because employer customers are off-limits and the most obvious buyer
  set is conflict-sensitive.
- Constraints: Must stay fully legal, cannot solicit employer customers, cannot
  use confidential templates/pricing/workflows, only 5 hours per week, does not
  want consulting.
- Credibility signals: Direct industry operating experience in govtech
  procurement.
- Unfair advantages: Publicly defensible advantage is domain fluency and problem
  selection judgment. Internal templates, pricing logic, and workflow insights
  do not count as usable edge unless clearly non-confidential and allowed by
  employer agreements.

Feasibility verdict: `conditional`

Binding constraints:

- Do not use employer confidential information, internal templates, pricing
  logic, roadmaps, or customer-specific workflow knowledge.
- Do not market to or solicit current employer customers.
- With 5 hours per week and no consulting, immediate cash generation is
  unlikely.
- Any offer touching procurement process design should be based on public
  information and original work product, not copied internal know-how.

# Best Bet

Best bet: `Public-records procurement benchmark and template library`

Why this beats the obvious alternatives:

- It avoids the highest-risk move: repackaging employer-confidential templates,
  pricing logic, or workflow insights.
- It fits your "no consulting" constraint better than audits or teardown
  services.
- It has a realistic path to compounding into an asset, while a pure newsletter
  is too slow and a software-first build is too speculative.
- It lets you exploit your real legal edge: knowing what to look for in public
  procurement data, not reusing protected internal material.

Pressure test:

- Demand clarity: There is plausible demand, but not enough evidence yet that
  buyers will pay for a benchmark or template library without a narrowly defined
  use case.
- Distribution fit: Weak today. You likely need one narrow audience first, such
  as vendors selling to mid-sized cities, civic watchdogs, or non-customer
  municipalities in one state.
- Execution difficulty: Reasonable within 5 hours per week if the first product
  is a narrow monthly benchmark, not a full software platform.
- Compliance risk: Acceptable only if every input is public or freshly created
  by you and you avoid employer customers plus any restricted segments in your
  employment documents.
- Why software-first loses: It adds build time before proving anyone will pay,
  and your current distribution is not strong enough.
- What would change the recommendation: If your employment agreement blocks
  adjacent products entirely, verdict becomes `no_go`. If you discover a warm,
  non-conflicted channel willing to buy benchmark memos now, the hybrid ladder
  becomes better.

# 7-Day Validation Plan

1. Review your employment agreement and any invention assignment,
   confidentiality, moonlighting, and non-solicit clauses for 60 minutes.
   Target: produce a one-page red/yellow/green list of what is clearly allowed,
   unclear, and prohibited.

2. Define one narrow ICP and one pain metric. Target: choose exactly one of
   these ICPs by Day 1 end: non-customer municipal vendors, civic watchdogs, or
   non-customer municipalities in one state. Target metric: one measurable
   output such as "flag procurement cycles with 3+ waste signals" or "reduce
   time to triage RFP risk by 30 minutes."

3. Collect a seed dataset from public sources. Target: compile 25 publicly
   available procurement records from 5 mid-sized cities by Day 3.

4. Create a from-scratch prototype deliverable. Target: produce 1 benchmark memo
   and 3 original templates by Day 4 with no reused internal language.

5. Run demand interviews without soliciting employer customers. Target: speak
   with 6 people in the chosen ICP by Day 6. Target split: 3 discovery calls, 3
   message tests by email or LinkedIn.

6. Test willingness to pay. Target: ask for 2 explicit pre-sell asks by Day 7
   for either a $99 template pack or a $250 quarterly benchmark subscription.

7. Decide. Target: continue only if at least 4 of 6 conversations confirm the
   pain, at least 2 request the sample, and at least 1 says yes to a paid
   pre-order or pilot.

# Build Spec

- Initial product: a narrow paid library containing one benchmark memo format, a
  small dataset of public procurement signals, and 3 to 5 original templates.
- Inputs allowed: public RFPs, award notices, council minutes, bid tabs, public
  budgets, public audits, your original synthesis, and interview notes from
  non-conflicted contacts.
- Inputs not allowed: employer internal templates, internal pricing logic,
  customer-specific workflow details, private datasets, exported prompts, or any
  non-public employer material.
- Delivery format: Notion, PDF, or simple static site before any app build.
- AI role: summarize public records, normalize fields, cluster repeated waste
  patterns, and draft first-pass benchmark commentary for your review.
- Narrow launch wedge: one state, one city-size band, one use case.
- Productization trigger: only build software if 3 paying buyers ask for
  recurring updates or searchable comparisons.

# Kill Criteria

- Stop immediately if your agreement review shows the side product is prohibited
  or requires approvals you cannot get.
- Stop if you cannot define a version of the product that uses only public
  information and original work product.
- Stop or pivot if fewer than 4 of 6 target conversations confirm the same pain.
- Stop or pivot if 0 of 2 pre-sell asks convert within 14 days.
- Stop software plans if no buyer asks for recurring access after reviewing the
  sample.

Rejected path:

- Directly repackaging employer templates, pricing logic, or workflow insights
  into a side product. Reason: highest legal and confidentiality risk, weakly
  defensible if challenged, and directly conflicts with your stated requirement
  to stay fully legal.

- Broad procurement consulting. Reason: violates your preference, will consume
  more than 5 hours per week, and is the wrong business model for your
  constraints.

- Software-first govtech micro-SaaS. Reason: too much build before proof, weak
  initial distribution, and unnecessary given the legal need to validate a
  clean-room scope first.
