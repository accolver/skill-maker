## Best Bet

The best path is the hybrid benchmark product: a public-records-based
procurement waste benchmark for mid-sized cities, sold to adjacent buyers rather
than your employer's city customers.

Why this is the best fit:

- It uses your real edge, which is interpretation and judgment, not employer IP.
- It can start as a low-touch product instead of consulting.
- It respects the 5-hour-per-week constraint because the first version can be a
  narrow benchmark for one spend category, one region, or 25-50 cities.
- It creates an asset that can compound into a dataset, recurring report, or
  simple software.
- It reduces employer-conflict risk if you avoid current customers, avoid direct
  city sales initially, and build only from public information plus your own
  independently created scoring framework.

Pressure test:

- Demand clarity: real if you pick a buyer with money and urgency, such as
  vendors deciding where to sell, insurers evaluating municipal operations, or
  local media/watchdog groups needing evidence.
- Distribution fit: moderate, not automatic. You likely need adjacent channels,
  not city-buyer outreach.
- Execution difficulty: manageable if you start narrow and manual.
- Compliance and platform risk: acceptable only if you set strict red lines. Do
  not use internal templates, customer lists, pricing logic, private workflow
  details, or anything learned under confidentiality in a way that is not
  reconstructable from public sources and general experience.
- Service-first question: a full service wedge is not necessary here. A manual
  report or benchmark issue can serve as the validation wedge without becoming
  consulting.

## 7-Day Validation Plan

1. Review your employment agreement, confidentiality agreement, invention
   assignment terms, moonlighting policy, and non-solicit language. Write a
   one-page red-line memo covering forbidden customer segments, forbidden
   materials, and forbidden claims.
2. Choose one tiny wedge: for example, "mid-sized city procurement friction in
   software purchases" or "change-order leakage in cooperative purchasing." Keep
   it small enough to analyze 25 cities.
3. Define 5-7 benchmark signals that can be derived from public records only,
   such as cycle time, rebid frequency, single-bid rates, change-order
   frequency, award concentration, and post-award amendment count.
4. Manually assemble a sample dataset for 10 cities using only public agendas,
   RFPs, award notices, contract amendments, and budget documents. Use AI only
   to summarize and structure public documents.
5. Create a two-page sample report and a simple landing page offering either a
   paid founding subscription or a paid pilot report for adjacent buyers. Make
   the positioning explicit: independent public-record analysis, not affiliated
   with your employer.
6. Send 15 targeted messages to non-overlapping buyers such as municipal vendors
   outside your employer's customer set, insurers, local-investment analysts,
   procurement journalists, or civic watchdog groups. Ask for a 15-minute
   reaction or a prepaid founding spot; do not pitch custom services.
7. Decide using evidence: continue only if you get at least 3 strong
   problem-validation conversations and either 2 prepaid buyers, or 5 qualified
   waitlist signups from your exact buyer type.

## Build Spec

- Product shape: monthly or quarterly benchmark brief plus downloadable CSV and
  short city profiles.
- Initial user: adjacent commercial or research buyer, not your employer's city
  customers.
- v1 workflow: collect public documents -> extract structured fields -> score
  with your original rubric -> publish benchmark PDF/CSV.
- Tech stack: spreadsheet or Airtable, a simple script for document extraction,
  LLM-assisted summarization for public records, static landing page, Stripe or
  Gumroad for preorders.
- Data boundaries: only public records, user-submitted data with permission, and
  your independently created categorization logic. No internal employer assets.
- Core fields: city, population band, spend category, procurement cycle length,
  number of bids, award concentration, amendment frequency, cooperative contract
  usage, notable friction flags.
- Deliverables: one benchmark issue, one downloadable dataset, one explanation
  page for methodology, one disclaimer page stating independence and source
  boundaries.
- Expansion path: benchmark report -> recurring subscription -> searchable
  database -> alerting or simple SaaS workflow.

## Kill Criteria

- Stop immediately if employment documents or counsel indicate the concept
  conflicts with confidentiality, moonlighting, invention-assignment, or
  non-solicit restrictions.
- Stop if you cannot define a product that is clearly built from public sources
  and original frameworks rather than employer know-how.
- Stop if adjacent buyers show interest in the insight but only want bespoke
  consulting.
- Stop if you cannot get meaningful validation within the 5-hour-per-week
  constraint.
- Stop if after 30 days you have no prepayment, no repeat engagement, and no
  evidence that one buyer segment will reliably pay for recurring benchmarks.
