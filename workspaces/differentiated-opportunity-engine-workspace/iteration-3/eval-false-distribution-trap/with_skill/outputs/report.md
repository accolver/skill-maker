# Differentiation Profile

- Skills and domain experience: strong audience-building around AI productivity,
  message testing at scale, understanding of beginner-to-intermediate AI
  workflow pain, likely product intuition around prompts, automations, and tool
  stacks.
- Assets and access advantages: 150,000 followers on X in the AI productivity
  category; low-cost access to feedback, launches, surveys, and activation
  experiments.
- Distribution and network leverage: strong reach to students, job-seekers,
  hobbyists, and ambitious individual users; weak direct access to
  budget-holding business buyers.
- Constraints: does not want service work, does not want a course-led business,
  wants durability, lacks current trust with business buyers.
- Credibility signals: visible audience size and category association in AI
  productivity.
- Unfair advantages: can get fast top-of-funnel attention for self-serve
  products in AI productivity much more easily than a random builder; can
  collect behavioral and survey data from a large relevant user base.

Feasibility verdict: `conditional`

Binding constraints:

- The current audience is real distribution, but mostly to the wrong buyer for
  direct B2B sales.
- A business-buyer-first SaaS is not credible today because there is no trust,
  no buyer access, and no service wedge to manufacture credibility quickly.
- A durable product business is still credible if the first customer is an
  ambitious individual user whose usage can later create team pull and proof.

# Best Bet

The credible path is not `sell business buyers now`. The credible path is
`bottom-up product now, business trust later`.

Best bet: build a self-serve AI workflow operating system for ambitious
individual users that creates team pull and a proprietary benchmark layer.

What the product should do:

- Help users save and reuse high-performing AI workflows, not just prompts.
- Capture before/after metrics such as time saved, output quality, success rate,
  and workflow frequency.
- Let users publish or share proof-backed workflow templates.
- Generate portfolio-grade evidence of performance, which is useful to ambitious
  individuals today and useful to managers later.
- Evolve into team features only after repeated usage patterns appear.

Why this beats the obvious alternatives:

- It beats enterprise-first SaaS because the current audience can actually
  activate here.
- It beats services because the user explicitly does not want service work.
- It beats courses because the main asset becomes product usage data, templates,
  retention loops, and eventually team analytics.
- It beats pure media because the audience is used as demand capture and data
  acquisition, not as the whole business.

Pressure test:

- Demand clarity: strong for ambitious users who want visible productivity gains
  and reusable workflows; weak for abstract “AI assistant” positioning.
- Distribution fit: credible if the launch is self-serve and narrow; not
  credible if the first motion requires enterprise procurement.
- Execution difficulty: manageable if the first version focuses on one repeated
  job and a simple proof loop.
- Compliance and platform risk: acceptable if the initial scope avoids sensitive
  enterprise data and starts with user-owned workflows.
- Why no service-first wedge: service-first would probably improve enterprise
  learning speed, but the user explicitly rejects service work. That makes
  bottom-up software the least-bad credible route.
- What would change the recommendation: if the user gains 10 to 15 warm
  relationships with operators or managers who own budgets, a narrower B2B
  workflow tool or benchmark subscription could become the best near-term path.

Recommended narrow starting point:

Pick one repeated job where the current audience overlaps with real workplace
behavior. Good examples:

- research synthesis for analysts, marketers, or founders
- meeting-to-memo workflows for operators
- outbound personalization and account research for SDRs or recruiters
- content repurposing for creators who run small teams

Do not launch as a general AI productivity app. Launch as a workflow product for
one repeated, measurable job.

# 7-Day Validation Plan

Day 1:

- Define one narrow ICP and one repeated job.
- Write one landing page with one promise and one primary metric. Example: “Turn
  45 minutes of account research into 8 minutes with reusable AI workflows.”

Day 2:

- Draft 3 positioning angles and 10 concrete workflow examples.
- Build a clickable prototype or very thin product stub with these core actions:
  save workflow, run workflow, log result, share template.

Day 3:

- Post 3 distribution tests to X aimed at one ICP, not the whole audience.
- Target numeric goal: 150 landing page visits, 30 email captures, and at least
  10 respondents who identify as using AI for real work at least 3 times per
  week.

Day 4:

- Run onboarding calls or DM interviews with 10 qualified users from the
  signups.
- Target numeric goal: at least 6 say they would use a product weekly for one
  specific workflow, and at least 4 share an existing messy workflow.

Day 5:

- Give 10 users prototype access.
- Target numeric goal: at least 5 create one workflow, at least 3 run it twice,
  and at least 2 share a saved workflow or result with someone else.

Day 6:

- Test willingness to pay with a founder plan or waitlist deposit.
- Target numeric goal: either 5 preorders at $15 to $30 per month equivalent, or
  15 users who agree to a live beta with explicit weekly usage intent.

Day 7:

- Review retention and specificity.
- Decide whether one job is sticky enough to build around.
- Publish one benchmark-style insight from the first users to test whether data
  can become a second asset later.

# Build Spec

Product shape for v1:

- User creates a workflow made of steps, prompts, tools used, and expected
  output.
- User runs the workflow and records a result.
- Product asks for one hard metric and one soft metric after each run.
- User can clone, improve, and share a workflow template.
- Product generates a simple proof card: task, time saved, result quality,
  repeat count.

Required screens:

- landing page for one job
- workflow builder
- workflow run log
- metrics/proof card view
- shared template gallery

Data to capture:

- workflow type
- job role
- time-before
- time-after
- run frequency
- tools used
- share events
- repeat usage

Why this build matters:

- The workflow library creates user value.
- The run data creates the benchmark asset.
- The proof cards create social distribution and later business credibility.
- Team features can be added only after repeated single-user behavior exists.

# Kill Criteria

- Kill or pivot if fewer than 30 qualified work-context signups arrive from the
  first 3 focused posts.
- Kill or pivot if fewer than 6 of 10 interviewed users describe the same
  repeated job with measurable pain.
- Kill or pivot if fewer than 3 of the first 10 testers run a workflow more than
  once in 72 hours.
- Kill or pivot if zero users will prepay or commit strongly to weekly usage
  after prototype access.
- Kill the broad positioning if signups come mostly from curiosity users with no
  repeated work context.
- Do not add team or enterprise features unless at least 5 individual users
  independently ask for collaboration, shared templates, or manager visibility.
