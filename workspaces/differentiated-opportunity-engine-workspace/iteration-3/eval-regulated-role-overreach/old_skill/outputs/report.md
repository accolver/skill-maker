# Differentiation Profile

- Skills and domain experience: fintech compliance support, regulator-facing
  writing, policy/process translation, investigation and response coordination,
  AI-assisted drafting workflow design.
- Assets and access advantages: warm contacts in compliance, firsthand knowledge
  of how small firms struggle with documentation and regulator response
  turnaround, practical sense for what evidence and tone regulators expect.
- Distribution and network leverage: initial customer path through warm intros
  to small regulated firms, consultants, and operators who cannot afford full
  internal compliance teams.
- Constraints: must stay on the right side of unauthorized practice,
  regulated-role substitution, and overpromising; likely limited engineering and
  brand assets at the start.
- Credibility signals: prior fintech compliance experience and lived
  understanding of audit/exam pressure.
- Unfair advantages: stronger workflow and language intuition than a generic AI
  builder; ability to package compliance-adjacent execution without pretending
  to be legal counsel or the named compliance officer.

# Best Bet

The best near-term path is the hybrid ladder:
`compliance response concierge -> internal drafting/copilot system -> narrow workflow product`.

Why this is the best fit:

- It monetizes fastest through a service sold via warm intros.
- It respects the legal constraint better than positioning yourself as a
  fractional or outsourced compliance officer.
- It creates proprietary workflow knowledge, templates, and review structures
  that can later become software.
- It avoids the weakest version of the idea: a generic AI tool that appears to
  replace regulated judgment.

Recommended positioning:

- Sell `AI-assisted regulatory response operations` or
  `exam-response drafting and evidence coordination`.
- Do not sell `outsourced CCO`, `automated compliance decisions`,
  `legal advice`, or `regulator representation` unless you are actually
  licensed/authorized and the engagement structure supports it.
- Put human review, client approval, and legal/compliance sign-off at the center
  of the offer.

Pressure test:

- Demand clarity: strong. Small firms feel real pain around exam responses,
  evidence gathering, and drafting under time pressure.
- Distribution fit: strong enough. Warm contacts are a credible wedge for first
  interviews and pilots.
- Execution difficulty: manageable if you keep scope narrow and start manually.
- Compliance risk: manageable only if you define the product as drafting
  support, workflow coordination, and documentation assistance rather than
  delegated regulatory judgment.
- Service-first before software: yes. You need real artifacts, objections, and
  sign-off patterns before building product.

Practical legal guardrails:

- Use engagement language that says drafts are for client review and approval.
- Require a designated client owner for every submission.
- Avoid making final materiality, interpretation, escalation, or disclosure
  decisions.
- Route legal questions to external counsel or the client's compliance lead.
- Maintain audit trails showing source documents, draft generation, edits, and
  approvals.
- Be careful with confidentiality, retention, model providers, and cross-border
  data handling.

# 7-Day Validation Plan

## Day 1

- Write a one-page offer for
  `AI-assisted regulator response drafting and evidence coordination`.
- Include scope boundaries, disclaimer language, and explicit
  non-legal/non-officer positioning.

## Day 2

- Make a target list of 10-15 warm contacts and adjacent introducers.
- Send short outreach asking for 20-minute discovery calls about exam-response
  pain and current workflow.

## Day 3

- Run 3-5 calls.
- Ask what requests consume the most time, who signs off, what cannot be
  automated, and what would make them trust outside help.

## Day 4

- Build a manual concierge prototype: intake form, evidence checklist,
  draft-response template, reviewer checklist, and approval log.
- Do not build software yet.

## Day 5

- Offer 1-2 pilot engagements with fixed scope, tight turnaround, and mandatory
  client review.
- Price as a premium drafting/coordination service, not cheap AI labor.

## Day 6

- Execute one pilot or simulated sample using sanitized or synthetic materials.
- Measure time saved, draft quality, missing-data detection, and reviewer
  friction.

## Day 7

- Decide whether to continue based on pilot signals.
- Capture repeated tasks that should become internal tooling: intake
  normalization, source mapping, draft assembly, issue spotting prompts, and
  approval tracking.

Success signals for week 1:

- At least 5 useful conversations.
- At least 2 contacts say they would trial the service or introduce a buyer.
- At least 1 paid or strongly committed pilot.
- Clear evidence that the painful part is workflow/document assembly, not just
  generic writing.

# Build Spec

Start with an internal operator tool, not customer-facing software.

## Version 1 internal system

- Intake capture: regulator request type, due date, entity, reviewer, and
  required source documents.
- Source management: upload/index policies, procedures, prior responses, issue
  logs, and evidence files.
- Drafting layer: AI produces first drafts tied to cited source snippets and
  highlights missing information.
- Review layer: checklist for human review, red-flag questions, and approval
  status.
- Audit trail: preserve prompt context, source references, revision history, and
  final approver.

## Product boundaries

- Allowed: drafting, summarization, evidence chase lists, response structuring,
  status tracking.
- Not allowed: final compliance determinations, legal interpretation, autonomous
  filing, impersonation of the firm, or claims that the system acts as the
  compliance officer.

## Likely first product module

- `Exam Response Pack Builder`
- Inputs: request letter, policy docs, prior responses, org facts.
- Outputs: first-draft response, evidence request checklist, open questions
  list, and reviewer sign-off sheet.

## Suggested go-to-market sequence

- Step 1: productized service.
- Step 2: internal tooling used by you during pilots.
- Step 3: narrow client-facing workflow portal only after repeatable demand and
  safe boundaries are proven.

# Kill Criteria

- Stop if buyers mainly want you to act as the named compliance officer or make
  final regulatory judgments.
- Stop if trusted contacts consistently say the risk committee or counsel would
  block AI-drafted responses even with human review.
- Stop if you cannot define a narrow service boundary that avoids
  legal/compliance-role overreach.
- Stop if pilots do not produce measurable time savings or quality gains over
  the client's existing manual drafting process.
- Stop if data handling requirements make the delivery model uneconomic for a
  small-firm wedge.
- Stop if willingness to pay is weak and the work stays fully bespoke with no
  path to reusable workflow assets.
