# Recommendation: sell a service first, but design it like the seed of a product

## Short answer

My honest recommendation: **start with a narrow, high-value service before
building a micro-SaaS**.

Given your profile, you have three strong assets:

- deep firsthand knowledge of merchant reporting pain
- credibility with operators who feel that pain
- the ability to spot recurring workflow problems in ecommerce analytics

But you are missing two things that matter a lot for a SaaS-first path:

- direct budget authority in your network
- close access to founders or senior decision-makers who can quickly buy and
  champion a new tool

That combination usually makes **service-first** the better move. It gets you
paid to learn which pain is urgent enough that people will spend money on it,
and it helps you build decision-maker relationships you do not yet have.

## Why service-first fits you better than SaaS-first

### 1. Your current edge is diagnosis, not distribution

You likely understand the pain better than many founders do. But a micro-SaaS
does not win on insight alone. It also needs distribution, rapid feedback from
buyers, and repeated proof that the problem is painful enough to justify
recurring spend.

Right now your network is mostly peers. Peers are excellent for discovery
interviews and referrals, but they are often weaker for fast budget decisions. A
service lets you start with peer-led introductions and still close business by
positioning yourself as a specialist solving a costly reporting problem.

### 2. Service revenue buys clarity faster than speculative product work

If you build first, you risk spending months on a dashboard, connector, or
alerting workflow that merchants say is useful but will not urgently buy.

If you sell a service first, you learn:

- which reporting pain causes enough frustration to trigger spending
- who actually owns the budget
- what data sources and operational edge cases matter most
- what output clients value: audits, executive summaries, anomaly alerts,
  board-ready reporting, margin views, channel attribution fixes, or forecasting

Those are exactly the inputs you need before productizing.

### 3. Ecommerce reporting pain is often messy and cross-functional

A lot of reporting pain sounds like software pain but is partly a services
problem:

- bad source-of-truth definitions
- broken tracking governance
- inconsistent finance vs marketing views
- custom KPI logic by merchant
- one-off data quality issues

Services handle this ambiguity well. SaaS struggles unless the problem is
already standardized.

### 4. Service-first improves your founder network problem

If you want to become a software founder eventually, the most valuable change is
not just code or product ideas. It is getting repeated access to owners,
operators, and budget holders. A focused service can create that access quickly.

## What I would do in your position

Start with a **productized analytics service** aimed at a painful, measurable
ecommerce reporting outcome.

Good examples of offers:

- "Profit Leak Audit for Shopify brands"
- "Executive Reporting Cleanup for 7- and 8-figure merchants"
- "Marketing-to-finance KPI reconciliation in 14 days"
- "Weekly margin and channel performance reporting for brands that outgrew
  spreadsheets"

The right service is not "I do analytics consulting." It is:

- narrow
- outcome-based
- easy to explain
- priced high enough to signal business value
- repeatable enough that patterns emerge

## Best path: service now, SaaS later

Your goal should not be to stay a service business forever unless you end up
liking it. Your goal is to use service work to identify one of these:

- a repeated workflow clients ask for every month
- a repeated data-cleaning or KPI-mapping process you keep rebuilding
- a repeated alert, report, or decision-support artifact clients rely on

That repeated unit becomes the product wedge.

The strongest micro-SaaS ideas often come from seeing the same ugly manual
process 10 to 20 times in real client work.

## When SaaS-first would make more sense

I would only recommend SaaS-first if at least one of these is true:

- you already have warm access to multiple budget owners who are actively
  looking for a tool
- the pain is extremely narrow and standardized
- you can ship very fast and test demand with direct outreach immediately
- you are comfortable with a long period of low-confidence building and selling

Based on your description, that does not sound like your current situation.

## Risks of service-first

This is the main downside: you can get trapped in custom work.

To avoid that, set rules early:

- sell one narrow offer, not general consulting
- use fixed scopes where possible
- document repeated requests and manual steps
- refuse highly bespoke work unless it teaches you something strategic
- after every client, ask: "what part of this should never be done manually
  again?"

## A practical 90-day approach

### Days 1-30

- Interview 15 to 20 ecommerce operators and marketers in your network
- Focus on recent reporting failures, not abstract wishes
- Identify one painful problem tied to revenue, margin, spend efficiency, or
  executive trust
- Write one clear offer and test it in direct outreach

### Days 31-60

- Close 2 to 3 paid clients
- Keep delivery narrow and highly templated
- Track repeated inputs, logic, outputs, and objections

### Days 61-90

- Look for the most repeated, least bespoke part of delivery
- Build only the smallest internal tool or client-facing feature that reduces
  manual work
- Validate whether clients value that component enough to pay recurring fees

## Decision rule

Use this simple rule:

**If you know the pain well but do not yet control distribution to buyers, start
with a service. If you repeatedly deliver the same outcome with the same inputs
and logic, productize that slice.**

## Bottom line

You sound like a strong candidate to build a business in this space, but **not
by jumping straight into a micro-SaaS**.

Your best move is to sell a narrow, productized service first. That path fits
your current assets, gets you paid faster, builds access to real buyers, and
gives you much better odds of discovering a SaaS wedge that merchants will
actually buy.
