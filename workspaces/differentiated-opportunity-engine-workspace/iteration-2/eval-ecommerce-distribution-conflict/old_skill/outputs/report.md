## Differentiation Profile

- Skills and domain experience: Senior data analyst with deep exposure to
  ecommerce SaaS, merchant reporting workflows, KPI confusion, dashboard
  adoption problems, and the gap between what merchants say they need and what
  they actually use.
- Assets and access advantages: Daily proximity to real reporting pain, pattern
  recognition across multiple merchant scenarios, and the ability to translate
  fuzzy reporting complaints into concrete metrics, cuts, and workflows.
- Distribution and network leverage: Strongest access is to peers and operators,
  not founders. That is weaker for direct software sales, but strong for
  discovery, referrals, internal champions, and early concierge pilots.
- Constraints: No direct budget authority, limited founder network, unclear
  willingness to do broad custom consulting, and a stated preference for a
  micro-SaaS outcome.
- Credibility signals: Senior operator inside the category, fluent in merchant
  reporting language, and likely better than a generic indie hacker at
  identifying expensive reporting friction.
- Unfair advantages: You know which reporting pain is real versus performative,
  where dashboards fail in practice, and which requests recur often enough to
  become a repeatable service and later a product.

Assumption: you can speak with peers and friendly operators quickly, even if
they are not the final buyer.

## Best Bet

My honest recommendation: sell a service first, but make it a deliberately
narrow service that is designed to become software.

Do not start with a broad analytics consultancy, and do not start by building a
generic merchant dashboard micro-SaaS. Your current advantage is problem
insight, not distribution. Because your network is mostly peers, the strongest
near-term path is to use those peers as discovery sources and internal
champions, then sell a tightly scoped reporting concierge or audit to the team
that owns retention, merchant success, implementation, or operations.

The offer should sound more like a product than a freelance engagement. Example
positioning:

`We fix one high-friction merchant reporting workflow in 14 days: metric definitions, reporting logic, and the weekly or monthly decision pack your team actually uses.`

Why this beats software-first:

- You do not yet have repeated proof of which reporting pain gets budget
  approval.
- Peers can introduce you to users and champions faster than they can approve
  new SaaS spend.
- The service gives you transcripts, workflow maps, edge cases, and language
  that become the spec for a future micro-SaaS.
- A product later is far more credible if 5 to 10 teams already paid you to
  solve the problem manually.

The software wedge should emerge only after you observe one repeatable unit of
work, such as:

- recurring metric-definition cleanup
- exception detection for broken or drifting KPIs
- merchant-facing reporting packet generation
- cross-source reconciliation for a single painful report

If after a few pilots the work stays highly custom, keep it as a premium niche
service. If the same workflow repeats with minor variations, promote that slice
into software.

## 7-Day Validation Plan

Day 1: Write a one-sentence ICP and offer.

- Pick one buyer context, not all of ecommerce: for example merchant success
  leaders at ecommerce SaaS companies, agencies serving mid-market Shopify
  brands, or rev-ops teams supporting merchant reporting.
- Draft a fixed-scope offer with price framing, deliverable, timeline, and
  outcome.

Day 2: Build a pain interview list.

- List 15 to 20 peers, ex-colleagues, customer-facing operators, and analytics
  leads.
- Ask for 20-minute calls about the ugliest recurring merchant reporting problem
  they see.

Day 3: Run 5 interviews.

- Goal: identify the report or metric workflow that is both frequent and
  budget-worthy.
- Ask what breaks, who feels the pain, how they handle it today, and what a fix
  is worth.

Day 4: Draft the productized service.

- Turn the most repeated pain into a fixed offer with one deliverable, one
  timeline, and one price band.
- Create a simple one-page brief and outreach note.

Day 5: Pitch 5 to 10 warm contacts.

- Ask for either a paid pilot or an intro to the budget owner.
- Do not ask whether they like the idea. Ask whether they will buy the scoped
  outcome.

Day 6: Deliver one mini sample.

- Build a lightweight artifact: mock KPI pack, anomaly review, metric-definition
  teardown, or reporting QA checklist.
- Use AI only to accelerate packaging and pattern extraction, not as the whole
  product story.

Day 7: Decide based on signals.

- Continue only if you get strong pain confirmation plus at least 2 serious
  follow-up conversations and 1 credible path to a paid pilot.
- If the pain is real but budgets are blocked, revise the buyer target before
  building software.

## Build Spec

Initial build target: no-code or low-code internal delivery system for the
service, not a public SaaS.

- Core workflow: intake form -> data source checklist -> metric definition map
  -> issue detection -> standardized output report.
- Inputs: screenshots of current reports, metric definitions, sample exports,
  notes from merchant-facing teams.
- Outputs: corrected KPI pack, exception summary, metric dictionary, and
  recommended reporting cadence.
- Internal tooling: templates, prompt library for report drafting, QA checklist,
  and a repeatable data review worksheet.
- Automation to add first: report generation, anomaly summarization,
  metric-definition comparison, and standardized recommendations.
- Productization trigger: once 70 percent of delivery follows the same steps
  across at least 3 to 5 paid pilots, pull that slice into a narrow micro-SaaS
  or operator-facing tool.
- Micro-SaaS shape later: one-job product such as KPI QA, exception alerting, or
  merchant reporting pack generation for a specific stack or segment.

## Kill Criteria

- Stop or pivot if 10 to 15 conversations do not reveal one reporting pain that
  recurs with similar language.
- Stop or pivot if peers agree the problem is real but cannot connect you to a
  buyer with budget.
- Stop or pivot if prospects only want bespoke analytics help with no repeatable
  workflow.
- Stop or pivot if you cannot get at least 1 paid pilot or a very strong pilot
  commitment after warm outreach.
- Do not build self-serve SaaS if the manual work has not converged into a
  repeatable narrow job.
