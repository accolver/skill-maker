# Offer Thesis

Create a paid benchmark and template product for private-practice therapists who
already send or want to send a newsletter but do not know what "good" looks
like. The offer sells clarity and speed: buyers get niche-specific newsletter
benchmarks plus ready-to-customize templates they can use without hiring a
marketer or buying a broad consulting engagement.

# ICP

- Buyer: solo and small-group private-practice therapists
- Segment: therapists using an email newsletter for client education, referral
  nurturing, and practice visibility, especially those without an in-house
  marketer
- Painful job to be done: publish a credible, consistent newsletter that feels
  professional, fits ethical boundaries, and saves time
- Why now: many therapists know they should nurture referral sources and stay
  top-of-mind with past or prospective clients, but newsletter quality is vague,
  content creation is slow, and most marketing advice feels too generic or too
  sales-heavy for the profession

# Offer Design

- Primary shape: template or benchmark product
- Why this shape: trust is lower, the audience is niche, and the user explicitly
  does not want service work. A product gives therapists a low-friction purchase
  with clear utility and keeps fulfillment scalable.
- Product name hypothesis: `Therapist Newsletter Benchmarks + Issue Kit`

Core deliverables:

1. A benchmark report for therapist newsletters covering practical norms such as
   welcome sequence structure, issue format, subject line patterns, CTA styles,
   send cadence ranges, and referral-friendly content mix.
2. A swipe file of annotated examples showing what strong therapist-friendly
   newsletter issues look like.
3. A template pack with plug-and-play issue templates, subject line formulas,
   welcome email templates, referral-partner email templates, and editorial
   calendar prompts.
4. A short implementation guide explaining how to customize the templates
   without drifting into overpromotional or ethically awkward messaging.

Scope boundaries:

- Product only, not newsletter ghostwriting or editing
- No custom strategy, audits, or done-for-you setup
- No clinical, legal, or compliance advice
- Templates are for educational and marketing communication, not treatment plans
  or crisis communication
- Benchmarks are directional heuristics for this niche, not universal
  performance guarantees

# Pricing Hypothesis

- Primary pricing model: one-time purchase
- Price hypothesis: $149 to $299 for the initial product
- Why this range: the scope is fixed, fulfillment is non-service, and the buyer
  value is real but not enterprise-sized. The product should be affordable for
  solo practitioners while still expensive enough to signal niche relevance and
  save them from piecing together generic advice.
- What would adjust pricing: evidence that buyers most value the benchmark data
  itself, demand for segment-specific versions like trauma therapists or couples
  therapists, or proof that templates directly reduce content creation time
  enough to support a higher price point

# Rejected Framings

1. Broad consulting offer: rejected because it creates custom service work,
   weakens positioning, and conflicts with the user's stated preference.
2. Generic AI newsletter service: rejected because it sounds interchangeable,
   invites low-trust buyers, and does not communicate therapist-specific nuance.
3. Software-first newsletter platform: rejected because the commercial wedge
   here is information plus templates, not building a tool before proving
   demand.
4. Clinical or compliance guidance product: rejected because it risks legal and
   regulated-role overreach. The offer should stay firmly in marketing education
   and content structure.
