## Part 1: Initial Financial Analysis

### 1.1 Income Gap Analysis

**Current Situation (Ages 62–66, if Robert delays SS):**

- Carol's part-time income: $25,000/year (~$2,083/month) — only until age 62 (3
  more years)
- Social Security: $0 if both delay
- **Monthly income: $2,083**
- **Monthly shortfall: $4,717/month = $56,604/year**

Over the next 4 years (until Robert reaches 66), they need to bridge
approximately **$226,000–$270,000** from savings/investments just to cover basic
expenses, depending on when Carol stops working and whether Robert claims early.

**If Robert claims SS now at 62 ($1,900/month):**

- Monthly income: $1,900 + $2,083 (Carol's work) = $3,983
- **Monthly shortfall: $2,817 = $33,804/year**
- But this permanently reduces his benefit by ~$500/month for life
- Over a 25-year retirement, that's $150,000+ in forfeited income

**At Robert's FRA (age 66):**

- Robert SS: $2,400/month
- Carol (if at FRA, age 63 at that point — she'd need to wait until 66 for her
  $1,100): $0 yet
- **Monthly shortfall at Robert's 66: $4,400/month**

**At both FRA (Robert 66, Carol 66 = 7 years from now):**

- Combined SS: $2,400 + $1,100 = $3,500/month
- **Monthly shortfall: $3,300/month = $39,600/year**

### Key Takeaway: Even with full Social Security, they have a $39,600/year income gap that must be filled from their $1,030,000 in assets.

### 1.2 Withdrawal Rate Analysis

- Annual gap to fill (both at FRA): $39,600
- $39,600 / $1,030,000 = **3.84% withdrawal rate**
- This is close to the traditional 4% rule, but:
  - They are drawing down assets NOW before SS kicks in
  - The bridge period (4–7 years) could consume $200K–$300K
  - After the bridge, remaining assets might be $730K–$830K
  - $39,600 / $780,000 (mid-estimate remaining) = **5.1% withdrawal rate** —
    this is dangerously high
  - Doesn't account for inflation, healthcare costs, or LTC needs

### 1.3 Longevity Risk

- Robert at 62: average male life expectancy ~83, but could live to 90+
- Carol at 59: average female life expectancy ~86, could live to 95+
- They need income for potentially **30+ years**
- If Carol lives to 92, she needs income for **33 years**
- Running out of money at age 82–85 is a real risk at 5%+ withdrawal rates

### 1.4 Long-Term Care Exposure

- Robert's mother required 4 years of nursing home care — strong family history
- National average nursing home cost: ~$108,000/year (private room)
- 4 years = $432,000 — this would consume nearly half their liquid assets
- If both Robert and Carol need care, the exposure could be $500K–$800K+
- This is the biggest single risk to their financial plan

### 1.5 Market Risk Assessment

- Robert lost 30% in 2008 — on $780K today that would be a $234,000 loss
- At 62 with no pension, he cannot afford to lose principal
- A major downturn in the first 5 years of retirement (sequence-of-returns risk)
  could be catastrophic
- His emotional reaction to 2008 suggests he would likely sell at the bottom,
  locking in losses

### 1.6 Tax Considerations

- $780K in 401(k) + $120K in IRA = **$900,000 in tax-deferred money**
- Every dollar withdrawn is taxed as ordinary income
- Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) start at age 73 — on a growing balance,
  these could push them into higher tax brackets
- Strategic Roth conversions between now and 73 could save significant taxes
- Joint brokerage ($85K) gets preferential capital gains treatment
- Savings ($45K) has no tax consequences

### 3.2 Product Details

#### A. Fixed Index Annuity (FIA) with Guaranteed Lifetime Withdrawal Benefit (GLWB)

**Why this product:**

- Robert said he's "nervous about the market" and "doesn't want to go through
  that again" — an FIA with a floor provides **zero downside market risk to
  principal**
- The GLWB rider provides **guaranteed lifetime income** regardless of market
  performance
- Income can begin immediately or be deferred to maximize the benefit base
- Many FIAs offer guaranteed rollup rates of 5–7% on the income benefit base
  during deferral
- **Addresses: market risk, longevity risk, income gap, emotional peace of
  mind**

**Specific Approach:**

- Roll $400,000–$500,000 from the 401(k) into an IRA, then fund the FIA
- Choose a product with a **joint-life GLWB** so payments continue for the
  surviving spouse
- If deferred for 4 years (until Robert's 66), the income benefit base could
  grow to $520K–$600K+ depending on the rollup rate
- At a 5% joint payout rate, that's **$2,167–$2,500/month guaranteed for life**
- Combined with Social Security
  ($3,500/month at both FRA), total guaranteed income could be **$5,667–$6,000/month**
  — very close to their $6,800 need

**Bridge Strategy:**

- During the first 4 years, draw from the brokerage account ($85K) and a portion
  of savings
- Carol's $25K/year income covers 3 years
- Total bridge need: ~$180K–$220K over 4 years
- This is feasible with brokerage + savings + limited IRA withdrawals

**Products to research (examples):**

- Athene Agility series (competitive rollup rates, joint GLWB)
- Nationwide Peak 10 (strong index options, income rider)
- Allianz Benefit Control Annuity (flexible income options)
- Lincoln Financial MYGuarantee Plus (for a fixed-rate tranche)

**Key selling points for Robert:**

- "Your money is never exposed to market losses — when the index goes down, your
  account value stays flat, it doesn't decline"
- "You'll receive a check every month for the rest of your lives — both of your
  lives"
- "This is the closest thing to the pension you never had"

#### B. Hybrid Life Insurance / Long-Term Care Product

**Why this product:**

- Robert has a **direct family experience** with LTC costs devastating finances
- Traditional LTC insurance is expensive at their ages and has rate increase
  risk
- A hybrid (asset-based) product provides: LTC benefits if needed, death benefit
  if not, return of premium if they change their mind
- **Addresses: LTC risk, death benefit need, premium recovery concern**

**Specific Approach:**

- Fund with a single premium of $100,000–$150,000 from the 401(k) rollover or
  brokerage account
- This can leverage the premium 2–3x for LTC benefits (e.g., $100K premium →
  $200K–$300K LTC pool)
- Monthly LTC benefit of $4,000–$6,000/month for 3–5 years
- If they never need LTC, a death benefit passes to heirs tax-free
- If they change their mind, most products offer full return of premium in the
  first 10+ years

**Products to research:**

- Lincoln MoneyGuard (single premium hybrid, strong LTC benefits)
- OneAmerica Asset-Care (asset-based LTC, flexible funding)
- Nationwide CareMatters II (hybrid LTC with chronic illness rider)
- Securian SecureCare (linked benefit LTC)

**Key selling points for Robert:**

- "You told me your mother's care wiped out everything — this product is
  specifically designed to prevent that from happening to you and Carol"
- "If you never need the care, the money isn't lost — it goes to your
  beneficiaries as a tax-free death benefit"
- "You can get your money back if you change your mind — there's a
  return-of-premium feature"

#### C. Short-Term / Bridge Income Strategy

**Purpose:** Cover expenses during the gap years before Social Security and
annuity income fully kick in.

**Options:**

- **Multi-Year Guaranteed Annuity (MYGA):** $80K–$100K for a 3–5 year guarantee
  period at 4.5–5.5% (current rates). Provides predictable short-term income.
- **CD Ladder:** $45K savings into 1-year, 2-year, 3-year CDs for liquidity at
  intervals.
- **Systematic withdrawals** from brokerage account (taxed at capital gains
  rates, more favorable than IRA withdrawals).

#### D. Growth Bucket — Remaining Assets

**Purpose:** Inflation hedge, future large expenses, legacy.

**Approach:**

- Remaining assets ($200K–$300K depending on allocations above) stay in
  diversified portfolio
- Carol's IRA ($120K) could remain invested for growth — she's only 59 and may
  not touch this for 10+ years
- Moderate allocation: 50% equities / 40% bonds / 10% alternatives
- This money has time to recover from downturns since the income floor is
  guaranteed
- **Psychological benefit:** Robert can tolerate market volatility in this
  bucket because his income doesn't depend on it

#### E. Life Insurance — Survivor Income Protection

**Why consider it:**

- When the first spouse dies, the survivor loses one Social Security check (the
  lower one)
- That's $1,100–$2,400/month in lost income, but expenses may only drop 20–30%
- A $150K–$250K term-20 or guaranteed universal life policy could bridge this
  gap

**Assessment needed:**

- Robert's health/insurability at 62
- Whether the annuity GLWB joint payout adequately covers the survivor
- Cost-benefit at their ages (premiums will be significant)

### 3.4 Tax Strategy

1. **Roth Conversion Opportunity (Now through age 73):**
   - Robert has no earned income; Carol earns only $25K
   - They're in a historically low tax bracket right now
   - Convert $30K–$50K/year from traditional IRA to Roth
   - Pay taxes now at low rates vs. higher rates when RMDs force withdrawals
   - Roth money grows tax-free and has no RMDs

2. **Withdrawal Sequencing:**
   - First: Taxable brokerage account (favorable capital gains rates)
   - Second: Tax-deferred accounts (401k/IRA — ordinary income rates)
   - Last: Roth (tax-free, let this grow longest)

3. **IRMAA Planning:**
   - Medicare premiums increase if income exceeds $206K (2024, married filing
     jointly)
   - Large IRA withdrawals or Roth conversions could trigger higher Medicare
     premiums
   - Plan conversions to stay below thresholds

## Part 5: Objection Handling Preparation

### "I don't want to lock up my money in an annuity"

**Response:** "That's a great concern, and I wouldn't want you to lock up ALL
your money. What we're talking about is putting a portion — maybe 40–50% — into
a guaranteed income vehicle. The rest stays fully liquid and invested. Think of
it like building a floor under your retirement — the floor is guaranteed, and
everything above it is flexible."

### "Annuities have high fees"

**Response:** "Some annuities do, and those are the ones I'd steer you away
from. The products I'm looking at have no annual fees on the base contract. The
income guarantee rider does have a fee — typically 0.95%–1.10% — but you're
getting a guaranteed income for life in return. Compare that to the alternative:
if the market drops 30% again like 2008, you'd lose $230,000. The rider fee on
$450,000 would be about $4,500/year — that's your insurance against a
catastrophic loss."

### "What about inflation?"

**Response:** "Excellent question. That's exactly why we don't put everything in
the annuity. The growth bucket is specifically designed to keep pace with
inflation. And some annuity products offer increasing income options — the
payout starts lower but increases over time. We'll look at both options."

### "Long-term care insurance is too expensive / I'll never need it"

**Response:** "Traditional LTC insurance can be expensive, and I agree it's not
always the best fit. What I'm recommending is different — it's a life insurance
policy that also covers long-term care. If you need care, it pays for care. If
you never need care, it pays a death benefit to your kids. If you change your
mind, you can get your money back. There's no scenario where you lose. And
Robert, given your mother's experience, how would it feel knowing that you and
Carol are protected no matter what?"

### "I want to wait and see / I need to think about it"

**Response:** "Absolutely, this is a big decision and I'd never want you to
rush. But I do want to mention two things: First, the longer we wait to start,
the more of your savings you'll draw down during this gap period — every month
costs roughly $4,700 from your nest egg. Second, for the long-term care
protection, your health is the best it's going to be right now. If something
changes health-wise, we could lose the ability to get coverage at all. Can we at
least schedule our follow-up to review the specific numbers?"

### "I just want something safe — can't I just put it all in CDs?"

**Response:** "You could, and CDs are very safe. The challenge is that CDs
currently yield about 4–5%, which on $1,000,000 gives you $40,000–$50,000/year
before taxes. After taxes, that's maybe $32,000–$40,000 — and when rates drop
(which they historically do), that income drops too. The annuity guarantees your
income even when interest rates are low. More importantly, CDs have no long-term
care protection, no death benefit, and no longevity guarantee. You could outlive
your CDs."

## Summary: The Big Picture

Robert and Carol are in a common but precarious situation: adequate assets but
no guaranteed income beyond Social Security, significant market fear, real LTC
exposure, and a gap period that will erode their nest egg. Without intervention,
their risk profile looks like this:

- **Best case:** Markets cooperate, neither needs LTC, both live to ~85. They're
  okay but tight.
- **Likely case:** Some market volatility, one needs some care, one lives
  past 90. They struggle in their 80s.
- **Worst case:** Early market downturn + LTC event. They're broke by 80.

The strategy flips this: guaranteed lifetime income replaces the pension he
never had, hybrid LTC protection neutralizes the nursing home risk, and a growth
bucket provides flexibility and inflation protection. Robert gets to sleep at
night knowing his income is secure, and Carol is protected if something happens
to him.

**Your role in this meeting:** You're not selling products. You're solving the
three problems Robert already told you about — he's afraid of the market, he's
afraid of LTC costs, and he needs income. Listen first, educate second, solve
third. The products follow naturally from the problems.
