# Annuity Objection Handling: Post-Meeting Analysis & Recovery Plan

## What Went Wrong in the Meeting

Before diving into specific responses, let's diagnose what happened structurally
in this meeting. Several things went wrong simultaneously:

### 1. You Were Ambushed by Stacked Objections

The couple came to the second meeting with pre-loaded objections from external
sources (a coworker, a TV personality). This means they did research between
meetings—which actually signals interest, not rejection. People who aren't
interested don't bother looking into it. However, when objections stack up
rapidly, it creates an overwhelming feeling for the agent and momentum for the
client to say no.

### 2. You Likely Went Into "Defense Mode"

When hit with multiple objections, most agents instinctively try to defend the
product. This shifts the dynamic from advisor-client to salesperson-prospect.
The moment you start defending, you've lost the consultative frame.

### 3. You Didn't Isolate and Address Objections One at a Time

When a couple fires off four objections in rapid succession, you need to slow
the conversation down, acknowledge each concern, and address them individually.
Trying to answer everything at once makes you sound scripted and defensive.

### 4. You May Not Have Anchored the Recommendation to Their Specific Problem

If the couple can't connect the annuity recommendation to the specific
retirement income gap you identified in the first meeting, every objection
sounds reasonable. The product needs to be positioned as the solution to _their_
problem, not as a product you're selling.

### 5. The "Think About It" Ending Was Predictable

"We need to think about it" is the most common way clients exit a meeting
they've mentally checked out of. By the time the wife said this, the sale was
already lost. The daughter in finance is a convenient delay tactic—but also a
potential ally if handled correctly.

## Objection-by-Objection Breakdown with Word-for-Word Responses

### Objection #1: "My buddy at work told me annuities are a scam and the insurance company keeps your money when you die."

**What's really going on:** The husband is repeating something he heard from a
non-expert. He doesn't actually understand the product mechanics—he's parroting
a common misconception. This objection is rooted in fear and third-party
influence, not personal analysis.

**What NOT to say:**

- "Your buddy doesn't know what he's talking about."
- "That's not true at all."
- "Well, that depends on the type of annuity."

These responses are dismissive and will make him defensive.

**Word-for-word response:**

> "John, I appreciate you bringing that up, and I'm glad you're doing your
> homework. Can I ask—does your buddy own an annuity, or is he speaking from
> what he's heard?"
>
> _(Pause and let him answer. He'll almost certainly say no.)_
>
> "That's what I figured. There's a lot of misinformation out there about
> annuities because people lump all annuities together—and honestly, some of the
> old-style annuities from decades ago did have some unfavorable features. So
> he's not completely wrong to be cautious.
>
> But let me address the specific concern about the insurance company keeping
> your money. With the Fixed Indexed Annuity I'm recommending, here's exactly
> what happens: If you were to pass away during the accumulation phase, your
> full account value—every dollar you put in plus any growth—goes directly to
> your beneficiary. [Wife's name], that would be you, or your children, or
> whoever you designate. The insurance company doesn't keep a penny.
>
> Now, if you've started taking income through the rider and you pass away, any
> remaining account value still goes to your beneficiaries. The only scenario
> where the insurance company retains funds is if you've taken out more in
> income over your lifetime than you originally put in—which would actually mean
> the annuity did exactly what it was supposed to do: it paid you income you
> couldn't outlive.
>
> Does that make sense? The insurance company is contractually obligated to pay
> your beneficiaries. It's written right in the contract, not a verbal promise."

**Why this works:**

- You validate his concern without agreeing with the misinformation
- You question the source's credibility gently (does his buddy actually own
  one?)
- You acknowledge that some annuity criticism has a kernel of truth (builds
  trust)
- You explain the actual mechanics simply
- You bring the wife into it (she's the beneficiary—she should care about this)
- You end with a confirmation question to re-engage dialogue

### Objection #2: "I saw Suze Orman say never buy an annuity."

**What's really going on:** The wife is citing a celebrity financial personality
as an authority. Suze Orman's blanket anti-annuity stance is well-known, but
it's a generalization that doesn't account for individual situations. This
objection is an appeal to authority.

**What NOT to say:**

- "Suze Orman doesn't know your specific situation."
- "She's a TV personality, not a real financial advisor."
- "She actually changed her stance on annuities."

**Word-for-word response:**

> "Susan, I'm a fan of Suze Orman too—she's gotten a lot of people to take their
> finances seriously, and that's a good thing. And you know what? She's right
> that _some_ annuities aren't great for _some_ people. Where I'd push back a
> little is on the word 'never.'
>
> Here's what I mean: Suze Orman is giving advice to millions of people she's
> never met. She doesn't know that you and John have a $2,400 per month gap
> between your Social Security income and your actual monthly expenses in
> retirement. She doesn't know that you're concerned about running out of money
> if one of you lives into your 90s. She doesn't know that John's company
> pension disappeared when they switched to a 401(k) plan.
>
> _(Adjust the specifics above to match the actual details from your discovery
> meeting.)_
>
> What I'm doing is different from what she does. I sat down with you, we went
> through your specific numbers, and I identified a gap. The annuity isn't
> something I'm recommending because I sell annuities—it's the tool that solves
> the specific problem we found in your retirement plan.
>
> Let me ask you this: if Suze Orman sat down with you, looked at your numbers,
> and saw that same $2,400/month gap, do you think she'd tell you to just hope
> for the best? Or do you think she'd want you to have a plan to fill that gap?"

**Why this works:**

- You don't attack Suze Orman (the wife likes her—attacking her creates
  defensiveness)
- You acknowledge the general advice is partially valid
- You contrast general TV advice with personalized analysis
- You bring it back to THEIR specific numbers from the discovery meeting
- The closing question reframes the issue from "should we buy an annuity" to
  "should we have a plan for this gap"

### Objection #3: "Why would I lock my money up for 10 years when I can just keep it in my brokerage account and withdraw 4% per year?"

**What's really going on:** This is the most sophisticated objection and
suggests the husband has some financial literacy. He's referencing the 4%
withdrawal rule (the "Trinity Study" rule). This needs a substantive,
data-driven response.

**What NOT to say:**

- "The 4% rule doesn't work anymore."
- "The stock market is too risky."
- "You could lose everything in a crash."

**Word-for-word response:**

> "John, that's actually a great question, and the fact that you know about the
> 4% rule tells me you've done some reading on this. Let me share a few things
> about that strategy that most people don't consider.
>
> First, the 4% rule was developed in the 1990s based on historical data going
> back to the 1920s. The original researcher, Bill Bengen, recently said that in
> today's interest rate and market environment, the safe withdrawal rate might
> actually be closer to 3% or even lower. So right off the bat, the number might
> not be 4% anymore.
>
> Second—and this is the big one—the 4% rule has a fatal flaw called _sequence
> of returns risk._ Here's what that means in plain English: if the market drops
> 30% in the first two years of your retirement, and you're pulling out 4% per
> year during that drop, your portfolio may never recover. You're selling shares
> at a low price to generate income, and there aren't enough shares left to
> participate in the recovery.
>
> Let me give you a real example. If you retired in January 2000 with $500,000
> and withdrew 4% per year adjusted for inflation, you would have run out of
> money by 2016. That's 16 years—not 30. The dot-com crash and the 2008
> financial crisis back-to-back destroyed that strategy.
>
> Now here's what makes the annuity different: your principal is protected from
> market downturns. You participate in a portion of the upside through the index
> crediting, but you never lose money in a down year. And the income rider
> guarantees you a specific income amount for life, regardless of what the
> market does. You literally cannot run out of money.
>
> So here's the real question: it's not about locking up your money versus
> keeping it in a brokerage account. It's about which portion of your retirement
> savings should have a guarantee attached to it. I'm not suggesting you put
> everything in this annuity. I'm suggesting that the portion you need to cover
> your essential monthly expenses—your floor—should be in something that can't
> go to zero. The rest can stay in your brokerage account for growth and
> discretionary spending.
>
> Does that distinction make sense?"

**Why this works:**

- You compliment his knowledge (validates him)
- You present factual, data-driven counterpoints
- You introduce "sequence of returns risk" as an educational concept, not a
  scare tactic
- You use a real-world example (2000 retiree) that's verifiable
- You reframe from all-or-nothing to a "floor" strategy
- You acknowledge his brokerage account has value (you're not against it)

### Objection #4: "We need to think about it and talk to our daughter who works in finance."

**What's really going on:** This is a stall/exit objection. The daughter may or
may not actually work in finance in a relevant capacity (a bank teller, an
accountant, and a CFP all "work in finance" but have very different levels of
relevant knowledge). However, dismissing the daughter is a terrible idea—she's
family and her opinion will carry more weight than yours.

**What NOT to say:**

- "What does your daughter do specifically in finance?"
- "I wouldn't want you to miss out on this opportunity."
- "What is it you need to think about specifically?"

These all sound pushy and transparent.

**Word-for-word response:**

> "That makes complete sense, and I'd actually encourage you to talk to your
> daughter. Family should absolutely be part of big financial decisions.
>
> Here's what I'd like to do, if you're open to it: Can I put together a
> one-page summary of what we discussed—the income gap we identified, the
> solution I'm recommending, and how the numbers work? That way when you talk to
> your daughter, she has the actual details in front of her rather than working
> from memory. People in finance appreciate having the data.
>
> And I'll include my contact information on it. If your daughter has questions
> or wants to get on a call with us, I'd welcome that. I have nothing to hide
> here, and frankly, if she looks at this and finds a better solution for you,
> I'll be the first to congratulate her. My job is to make sure you have a plan,
> not to sell you a specific product.
>
> Can we set up a time in the next week or two to reconnect after you've had
> that conversation? How about [specific date]?"

**Why this works:**

- You don't resist the "think about it"—you embrace it
- You position yourself as transparent (nothing to hide)
- You offer to help facilitate the conversation with the daughter
- The one-page summary ensures your message doesn't get garbled in
  telephone-game retelling
- You welcome the daughter's involvement instead of fearing it
- You lock down a specific follow-up date (critical—without this, "think about
  it" becomes "never")

## What You Should Have Done Differently in the Meeting

### Before the Meeting

1. **Pre-framed objections in the first meeting.** At the end of meeting one,
   you should have said: _"Between now and our next meeting, you're probably
   going to Google annuities or talk to friends about them, and you're going to
   hear some negative things. That's completely normal. All I ask is that you
   write down any concerns you have and bring them to our next meeting so we can
   address them together. Fair?"_ This normalizes objections and positions you
   as the person to resolve them, not the person to defend against them.

2. **Sent a pre-meeting email or text.** Between meetings, send something like:
   _"Looking forward to seeing you Tuesday. I know retirement planning is a big
   decision, so if any questions or concerns have come up since we last talked,
   bring them along. That's what the meeting is for."_

### During the Meeting

3. **Started with a recap of THEIR problem, not your solution.** The second
   meeting should open with: _"Before we dive in, let me make sure I have your
   situation right. Based on our last conversation, here's what I found..."_
   Then re-present the income gap analysis. This re-anchors the conversation in
   their problem, not your product. Every objection should then be answered in
   the context of: "Okay, but we still have this gap—how else do you want to
   solve it?"

4. **Used the "Feel, Felt, Found" framework (adapted).** When hit with the first
   objection, you should have slowed down:
   - _"I understand how you feel..."_
   - _"A lot of my clients felt the same way when they first looked into
     this..."_
   - _"What they found was..."_ This buys you time, validates the client, and
     positions you as experienced.

5. **Isolated each objection.** After the first objection, instead of waiting
   for more: _"John, that's an important concern. Before we move on to anything
   else, let me make sure I address that one fully. Is that okay?"_ This
   prevents the stacking problem.

6. **Used a "concern list" technique.** When the couple started firing
   objections, you could have said: _"It sounds like you've both been doing some
   thinking, which is great. Here's what I'd like to do—let's list out every
   concern you have, all of them, and then we'll go through each one. That way
   nothing gets missed. What's the first concern?"_ Write them down visibly.
   This gives you control of the pacing and shows you're not afraid of their
   concerns.

7. **Never let the meeting end without a next step.** The "we need to think
   about it" should never be the last word. Always have a specific callback date
   and a reason for it.

## Recovery Strategy: Getting Them Back for a Follow-Up

Here's your action plan for re-engaging this couple:

### Step 1: Send a Thank-You Within 24 Hours

Send a handwritten note or a thoughtful email (not a templated one):

> "John and Susan, thank you for your time yesterday and for being so open about
> your concerns. Those are exactly the kinds of questions I'd want my own
> parents to ask before making any financial decision. I'm putting together some
> additional information that I think will be helpful, and I'd love to find a
> time to reconnect. No pressure—just want to make sure all your questions get
> answered."

### Step 2: Prepare the One-Page Summary

Create a simple, clean document that includes:

- Their current retirement income (Social Security, pensions, etc.)
- Their estimated monthly expenses in retirement
- The gap (highlighted)
- How the annuity fills that gap (specific numbers)
- A FAQ section addressing the exact objections they raised

### Step 3: Follow Up Within 5-7 Days

Call (don't just email):

> "Hi John, it's [your name]. I was thinking about our conversation and I put
> together that summary I mentioned. I'd love to drop it off or email it over so
> you and Susan—and your daughter—have everything you need to make a fully
> informed decision. When would be a good time?"

### Step 4: If the Daughter Wants to Talk

If they mention the daughter wants to speak with you, treat it as an
opportunity, not a threat:

> "That's great—I'd welcome that conversation. Would it be easiest to do a
> three-way call, or would she prefer to reach out directly? I'll send over the
> summary in advance so she has all the details."

### Step 5: At the Follow-Up Meeting

Open with: _"Before we start, I want to say something. Last time we met, I don't
think I did a good enough job addressing your concerns, and that's on me. Today
I want to make sure every question gets a complete answer. If at the end of this
conversation you still don't feel this is right for you, I'll respect that
completely. Fair?"_

This disarms them and shifts the dynamic from adversarial to collaborative.

## Key Psychological Principles to Remember

1. **People don't buy products—they buy solutions to problems.** Always anchor
   back to the income gap, never lead with the annuity features.

2. **Third-party objections (buddy, Suze Orman, daughter) are proxies for their
   own uncertainty.** Address the underlying fear, not just the surface
   objection.

3. **Couples sell each other.** If you can get one spouse fully on board,
   they'll persuade the other. Watch for who's more receptive and build your
   case through them.

4. **The person who controls the pace controls the meeting.** When objections
   start stacking, slow down. Don't let the clients dictate the rhythm.

5. **Transparency beats persuasion.** Saying "I welcome your daughter's
   involvement" and "if she finds something better, I'll congratulate her"
   builds more trust than the most polished rebuttal.

6. **Never argue with a client.** You can disagree with their information, but
   never with their right to have concerns. Every response should start with
   validation before correction.

## Quick Reference: Objection Response Framework

For any annuity objection, follow this 4-step pattern:

| Step             | Action                                          | Example                                                                    |
| ---------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1. **Validate**  | Acknowledge the concern is reasonable           | "That's a fair concern, and you're not the first person to bring it up."   |
| 2. **Clarify**   | Ask a question to understand the real objection | "Can you tell me more about what you heard specifically?"                  |
| 3. **Educate**   | Provide factual, specific information           | "Here's how this actually works with this specific product..."             |
| 4. **Reconnect** | Tie it back to their personal situation         | "So with your specific gap of $X/month, here's what that means for you..." |

If you follow this framework consistently, you'll handle any objection without
getting defensive and without losing the consultative positioning that makes
clients trust you.
