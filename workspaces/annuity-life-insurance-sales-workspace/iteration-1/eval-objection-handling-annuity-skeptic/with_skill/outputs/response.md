# Objection Handling Debrief: Fixed Indexed Annuity Presentation — Skeptical Couple

## Situation Analysis

You experienced what we call a **"stacked objection" meeting** — where multiple
objections fire in rapid succession and overwhelm the agent before any single
one can be properly addressed. This is actually common with couples, because
each spouse draws confidence from the other's skepticism, creating an escalation
loop. Let's break down exactly what happened, what went wrong, and how to
recover.

---

## What Went Wrong: Diagnostic

### Mistake #1: You likely tried to answer each objection as a standalone rebuttal

When the husband said "annuities are a scam" and the wife followed with "Suze
Orman says never buy one," those are **the same objection** — media-influenced
distrust — stated by two people. If you tried to fight each one separately, you
spent twice the energy and looked defensive both times. The correct move is to
**group and address the root concern once**, acknowledging both of them said it.

### Mistake #2: You may have argued instead of asking questions

The A-E-R framework (Acknowledge, Empathize, Redirect) requires you to **ask a
question** after empathizing — not launch into a defense. If you jumped straight
to "well, actually, annuities aren't a scam because..." you triggered their
defensive instinct and every subsequent objection came harder.

### Mistake #3: You didn't isolate the "think about it" objection in real time

"We need to think about it and talk to our daughter" is the most dangerous
objection because it sounds reasonable. It's not. It's an exit strategy. If you
let them walk out with that as the last word, they will NOT come back on their
own. The daughter in finance will almost certainly talk them out of it — not
because annuities are bad, but because fee-based advisors have a structural
incentive to keep assets under their management.

### Mistake #4: You may not have scheduled a next meeting before they left

The absolute #1 rule when a client says "I need to think about it" is:
**schedule the follow-up BEFORE they leave.** Not "I'll call you next week." A
specific date and time. "How about we reconnect Thursday at 2:00 so I can answer
any questions that come up?"

decision to someone who wasn't in the meeting and likely has a fee-based
advisory bias.

## Word-for-Word Responses: Follow-Up Meeting Script

If you get them back (and I'll show you how below), here's exactly how to handle
each objection. These are designed to be used **in order**, because the sequence
builds trust incrementally.

### Opening the Follow-Up Meeting

Before addressing any objection, you need to **reset the relationship** and take
responsibility:

> **"John, Mary — thank you for coming back in. I want to start by being honest
> with you. Last time we met, I think I did a poor job of addressing your
> concerns. You raised some really important questions, and instead of slowing
> down and making sure I answered each one fully, I felt like I was rushing.
> That's on me. Today, I'd like to go through each of your concerns one at a
> time. I'm not here to convince you of anything. I'm here to make sure you have
> all the facts so you can make the best decision for your family. Fair
> enough?"**

This does three things: (1) disarms their defenses, (2) positions you as humble
and client-first, (3) gives you permission to re-address each point.

---

### Objection 1: "My buddy at work says annuities are a scam and the insurance company keeps your money when you die"

> **"John, I really appreciate you bringing that up, because your friend's
> concern is one I hear often — and honestly, there's a grain of truth that gets
> twisted into a misunderstanding. Let me ask you this first: did your buddy
> mention which type of annuity he was talking about?"**

[He will almost certainly say no, or won't know.]

> **"That's what I usually find. Here's the thing — saying 'annuities are a
> scam' is like saying 'all medicine is bad.' There are over ten different types
> of annuities, and they work very differently from each other. What your friend
> may be referring to are variable annuities, which can have high fees —
> sometimes 3% or more per year. Those get a lot of negative press and, frankly,
> I don't sell those either. I agree those fees can be excessive.**
>
> **The fixed indexed annuity we discussed has zero annual fees on the base
> contract. The only optional fee is for the income rider, which guarantees you
> income for life.**
>
> **Now, to the really important part — 'the insurance company keeps your money
> when you die.' That is simply not true with the product we discussed. Let me
> show you exactly what happens."**

[Pull out the illustration or a simple one-page summary.]

> **"Here's the beneficiary provision: if you and Mary pass away, every
> remaining dollar in this contract goes to your beneficiaries — your children,
> your grandchildren, whoever you name. The insurance company does NOT keep your
> money. That's written into the contract. Now, there IS one type of annuity
> where the payments stop at death — it's called a 'life only' SPIA, and it's a
> completely different product. We are not discussing that. The FIA with an
> income rider we looked at has a full death benefit equal to the remaining
> account value. Your buddy's concern is understandable, but it applies to a
> product that isn't even on the table here.**
>
> **Would it help if I gave you a one-page summary of how the death benefit
> works so you can show your friend? He might find it interesting too."**

**Why this works:**

- You acknowledged his friend's concern without dismissing it
- You agreed that SOME annuities deserve criticism (variable annuities with high
  fees)
- You specifically refuted the "keeps your money" myth with the contract
  language
- You offered a takeaway he can share, turning his skeptical friend into a
  potential lead

---

### Objection 2: "Suze Orman says never buy an annuity"

> **"Mary, I'm glad you brought that up, because Suze Orman is a very
> influential voice and millions of people respect her opinion. I actually agree
> with some of what she says. May I share what she specifically criticizes, and
> then you can decide if it applies to what we're looking at?"**

[Wait for permission — this is critical. It shifts her from defensive to
curious.]

> **"Suze Orman's criticism is primarily aimed at variable annuities — the ones
> with 2-3% annual fees, complicated sub-accounts, and surrender charges on top
> of all that. She's also spoken against agents who put ALL of a client's money
> into annuities, leaving them with no liquidity. On both of those points, I
> agree with her 100%.**
>
> **Here's what's different about what we discussed: First, the fixed indexed
> annuity has zero annual fees on the base contract. Second, we specifically
> designed this so that only a portion of your assets would go into the annuity
> — you'd keep $_____ fully liquid in your savings and brokerage accounts. And
> third, you can access up to 10% of your annuity value every year without any
> penalty.**
>
> **I'd also point out something interesting: Suze Orman herself has said that
> if you need guaranteed income in retirement and you can't get it any other
> way, a fixed annuity can make sense. That's a direct quote. The product she
> criticizes and the one we discussed are fundamentally different things.**
>
> **Here's a question for you, Mary: setting aside what anyone else says — if I
> could show you a way to guarantee that your essential bills are covered every
> single month for the rest of your life, no matter what the stock market does,
> would that give you peace of mind?"**

**Why this works:**

- You didn't attack Suze Orman — you agreed with her specific criticisms
- You distinguished the product you're recommending from the ones she criticizes
- You used Orman's own nuanced position to support your case
- You ended with a question that refocuses on THEIR need, not anyone else's
  opinion

---

### Objection 3: "Why would I lock my money up for 10 years when I can just keep it in my brokerage account and withdraw 4% per year?"

> **"John, that's actually a really smart question, and it tells me you've done
> some reading about retirement strategies. The 4% rule is one of the most
> well-known guidelines out there. May I share something about that rule that
> most people don't know?"**

[Wait for permission.]

> **"The 4% rule was created by financial planner Bill Bengen in 1994. It was
> based on historical market data and said you could withdraw 4% of your
> portfolio in the first year of retirement, adjust for inflation each year, and
> your money should last 30 years. Here's the problem: even Bengen himself has
> since said the safe withdrawal rate today might be closer to 3% — because of
> lower projected returns and longer life expectancies.**
>
> **But here's the really important part — the 4% rule has a failure rate of
> about 5-10% depending on the study. That means for every 100 retirees who
> follow it, 5-10 of them will run out of money before they die. And you don't
> know in advance if you'll be one of the 5. It depends entirely on what the
> market does in the first few years of your retirement.**
>
> **Let me show you why this matters with real numbers."**

[Use a whiteboard, napkin, or simple handout:]

> **"Let's say you retire with $500,000 in your brokerage account and you
> withdraw $20,000 the first year — that's your 4%. But the market drops 30% in
> year one — which happened in 2008 and came close in 2020. Now you have
> $350,000, minus the $20,000 you withdrew, so you're at $330,000. Even if the
> market bounces back 30% the next year, you're at $429,000, not $500,000. And
> you're still withdrawing. That's called sequence of returns risk, and it's the
> number one destroyer of retirement plans.**
>
> **Now compare that to the annuity: regardless of what the market does —
> whether it drops 30%, 40%, or 50% — your guaranteed income payment stays
> exactly the same. Every single month. For the rest of your life. And Mary's
> life.**
>
> **Here's how I think about it: this isn't annuity versus brokerage account.
> It's BOTH. The annuity covers your essential expenses — your mortgage,
> utilities, groceries, healthcare — that's your paycheck. Your brokerage
> account stays invested for growth, travel, spoiling the grandkids — that's
> your playcheck. You need both. The annuity isn't replacing your investments.
> It's protecting them by making sure you never have to sell stocks in a down
> market just to pay the electric bill.**
>
> **Does that distinction make sense — paychecks for bills, playchecks for
> fun?"**

**Why this works:**

- You complimented his knowledge instead of dismissing the 4% rule outright
- You used the 4% rule's own creator to introduce doubt
- You showed the concrete math of sequence of returns risk
- You reframed the annuity as a COMPLEMENT to, not a REPLACEMENT for, his
  brokerage account
- The "paychecks and playchecks" framework is simple, memorable, and powerful

---

### Objection 4: "We need to think about it and talk to our daughter who works in finance"

This is the most critical objection. It must be handled carefully because you're
dealing with a family dynamic, not just a financial question.

> **"Absolutely — and I think that's really wise. This is a big decision and you
> should involve the people you trust. I think it's great that your daughter
> works in finance. May I ask a couple of questions about that, just so I can
> help?"**

[Wait for permission.]

> **"What area of finance does your daughter work in? Is she a financial
> advisor, or does she work in banking, accounting, or something else?"**

[Listen carefully. This tells you whether she's a fee-based advisor (potential
conflict of interest) or in a different area of finance.]

**If the daughter is a fee-based financial advisor:**

> **"That's wonderful — she'll be a great resource for you. I'd actually love
> the opportunity to speak with her directly, if you'd be open to that. Here's
> why: when your daughter evaluates this, she'll naturally compare it to what
> she knows — which is managing investments. And she's probably very good at
> that. But annuities solve a different problem than investment management. They
> provide guaranteed income — like a personal pension. It's not an either/or. I
> find that when I can have a professional conversation with another advisor, we
> usually end up on the same page because the math is the math.**
>
> **Would you be comfortable giving me her contact information, or having her
> call me? I'd be happy to walk through the illustration with her, answer all
> her questions, and let her give you her honest assessment."**

**If the daughter is NOT a financial advisor (banker, accountant, corporate
finance, etc.):**

> **"That's great. She'll definitely have useful perspective. Here's something
> I'd ask you to consider: finance is a very broad field, and retirement income
> planning is a very specialized area within it. A corporate finance analyst,
> for example, is an expert in business valuation — but that's a very different
> skill set than structuring guaranteed retirement income. It's like asking a
> cardiologist to do your knee surgery. Both are doctors, but the expertise is
> different.**
>
> **I'd love for your daughter to review what we discussed. In fact, let me put
> together a simple summary — the problem we identified, the solution, how it
> works, and the guarantees — that you can share with her. That way she has the
> full picture and not just one piece of it. And if she has questions, I'm happy
> to speak with her directly. Does that sound fair?"**

**Then, CRITICALLY, schedule the follow-up:**

> **"Here's what I'd suggest: take this information home, talk to your daughter,
> write down every question that comes up. I'd like to schedule a time next week
> — say, Tuesday or Thursday — for us to reconnect. That gives you time to
> think, talk to your daughter, and come back with any questions. Does Tuesday
> at 2:00 or Thursday at 10:00 work better for you?"**

**Why this works:**

- You didn't resist the daughter's involvement — you embraced it
- You offered to speak directly with the daughter, which shows confidence
- You addressed the potential bias of a fee-based advisor without being
  adversarial
- You provided a takeaway document so the daughter evaluates the FULL picture,
  not a fragment
- You scheduled the follow-up with a specific date and time — not "I'll call
  you"

## Recovery Plan: Getting Them Back for the Follow-Up

Since you didn't schedule a follow-up before they left, here's your recovery
plan:

### Step 1: Wait 48 hours (not more)

Don't call them the next morning — it seems desperate. But don't wait a week —
they'll have talked to the daughter and made a decision without you.

### Step 2: Send a handwritten note + one-page summary

Mail (not email) a handwritten card that says:

> _"John and Mary — thank you for your time Tuesday. I appreciate your
> thoughtful questions. I realize I could have done a better job addressing each
> of your concerns, and I'd welcome the chance to do that. I've enclosed a
> simple summary of what we discussed. If your daughter has any questions, I'd
> be happy to speak with her directly. I'll give you a call Thursday to see how
> you're feeling. — [Your name]"_

Enclose a **one-page summary** that includes:

- The income gap you identified (in dollars)
- The guaranteed income the FIA would provide
- The death benefit provision (beneficiaries get remaining value)
- The 10% annual free withdrawal provision
- The zero-fee structure on the base contract
- A comparison: "4% rule with market risk" vs. "guaranteed lifetime income"

### Step 3: Call on Thursday

> **"Hi John, this is [name]. I'm just following up — did you get the note I
> sent? I wanted to check in and see if you and Mary had a chance to talk things
> over, and if your daughter had any questions I could help with. Would it make
> sense for the three of us — or four, if your daughter wants to join — to sit
> down for 20 minutes next week? No pressure at all. I just want to make sure
> you have everything you need to make the best decision for your family."**

### Step 4: If the daughter pushes back

If they come back and say "Our daughter said we don't need an annuity," respond:

> **"I respect her opinion. May I ask — did she address the income gap we
> identified? You're currently projecting a $[X]/month shortfall between your
> guaranteed income and your essential expenses. How did she recommend covering
> that gap? If she has a solution that gives you guaranteed income for life
> regardless of the market, I'm all for it. If the plan is to withdraw from
> investments and hope the market cooperates, I'd just ask you to think about
> that word — 'hope.' Is hope a retirement plan?"**

## What You Should Have Done Differently: Preventive Measures

### 1. Addressed the "friend/media" objections as a group before presenting

If you suspected anti-annuity bias going in (which you should have identified
during the first meeting's discovery phase), you should have **inoculated**
before presenting:

> **"Before I show you what I've put together, I want to address something.
> There's a lot of misinformation about annuities out there — TV personalities
> and well-meaning friends often criticize all annuities as if they're one
> product. They're not. What I'm going to show you is a fixed indexed annuity,
> which has zero annual fees and guaranteed principal protection. It's very
> different from the variable annuities that get negative press. I just want to
> put that on the table so you can evaluate what I show you on its own merits.
> Fair?"**

### 2. Asked discovery questions about influencers during the first meeting

During fact-finding, you should always ask:

- "Who else have you spoken with about your retirement plans?"
- "Have you worked with a financial advisor before?"
- "Do you have family members in finance who might weigh in on this decision?"
- "What have you read or heard about annuities?"

This surfaces the objections **before** the presentation, when they're easier to
address.

### 3. Used a pre-commitment question before presenting

After fact-finding but before the presentation:

> **"John, Mary — if I can show you a way to guarantee your income gap is
> covered for life, protect your principal from market loss, AND leave the
> remaining value to your kids when you're gone — and the math works — is there
> any reason you wouldn't want to move forward?"**

This surfaces hidden objections early and gets a micro-commitment. If they say
"well, we'd still want to think about it," you can address that BEFORE spending
45 minutes on a presentation.

### 4. Slowed down and addressed one objection at a time

When multiple objections hit, say:

> **"I can tell you both have some really important concerns, and I want to make
> sure I address every single one. Rather than trying to rush through them, can
> we take them one at a time? John, let's start with what your friend told you
> about annuities. Then Mary, I want to make sure I fully answer your concern
> too. Is that okay?"**

This stops the escalation loop and forces a structured conversation.

### 5. Had a one-page "myth vs. fact" handout ready

Before any presentation involving an FIA, you should have a printed one-page
document:

| Myth                                                  | Fact                                                                                                                           |
| ----------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| "The insurance company keeps your money when you die" | Beneficiaries receive the remaining account value. The death benefit is written into the contract.                             |
| "Annuities have high fees"                            | Fixed indexed annuities have zero annual fees on the base contract. Income riders typically cost 0.95-1.10% per year.          |
| "Annuities lock up your money"                        | Most FIAs allow 10% penalty-free withdrawals annually. After the surrender period, 100% is accessible.                         |
| "You can get better returns in the market"            | Annuities don't compete with market returns — they provide guaranteed income. Different tool, different job.                   |
| "Suze Orman says never buy an annuity"                | Orman criticizes variable annuities with high fees. She has acknowledged fixed annuities can make sense for guaranteed income. |

Hand this to the clients at the START of the meeting. Let them read it. Then
say:

> **"If any of these come up in our conversation, we can discuss them in detail.
> But I wanted you to have the facts upfront."**

## Meeting Structure You Should Use Going Forward

For any second meeting where you're presenting a solution, follow this
structure:

### Phase 1: Recap & Inoculate (5 minutes)

- Recap what you learned in the discovery meeting
- Inoculate against known objections
- Ask: "Did anything come up since we last met that you'd like to discuss?"

### Phase 2: Present the Problem, Not the Product (5 minutes)

- Show the income gap analysis
- Quantify the risk: "If you do nothing, here's what happens"
- Let them feel the problem before you offer the solution

### Phase 3: Present the Solution Conceptually (5 minutes)

- "What if we could create a personal pension that guarantees $X/month for
  life?"
- Don't mention the product name yet — sell the CONCEPT

### Phase 4: Show the Math (10 minutes)

- Now show the illustration
- Lead with GUARANTEED values
- Compare: guaranteed income vs. 4% withdrawal strategy in a down market
- Show the death benefit provision clearly

### Phase 5: Address Concerns (15 minutes)

- "What questions do you have?"
- Use A-E-R for each objection
- Handle one at a time, get confirmation before moving to the next

### Phase 6: Close or Schedule (5 minutes)

- If ready: assumptive close — "Let's go ahead and get the paperwork started"
- If not: schedule the next meeting with a specific date/time before they leave
- Provide a takeaway summary document

objections overwhelmed your ability to respond systematically. The product you

Your recovery plan: handwritten note in 48 hours, call on Thursday, offer to
