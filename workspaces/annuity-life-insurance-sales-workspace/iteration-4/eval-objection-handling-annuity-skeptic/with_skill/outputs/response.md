# Objection Handling Debrief: FIA Presentation to Skeptical Couple

## Diagnosis: What Went Wrong

You experienced what I call an **objection avalanche** — multiple concerns
stacking up without resolution, each feeding the next, until the clients felt
overwhelmed and retreated to the safety of "we need to think about it." This is
recoverable, but it requires understanding why it happened and how to prevent it
next time.

Here's the breakdown of what went wrong:

### 1. You Skipped Trial Closes During Your Presentation

This is the root cause. **Trial closes** are check-in questions you ask
throughout your presentation to build incremental agreement and surface concerns
one at a time — BEFORE they pile up. After each key concept in your
presentation, you should have paused and asked something like:

- "Does that make sense so far?"
- "How does that sound to you?"
- "Can you see how that would work in your situation?"

Without trial closes, you presented for 20-30 minutes uninterrupted, and the
husband and wife sat there accumulating doubts. By the time you opened the
floor, they had 4 objections locked and loaded. No agent can handle 4
simultaneous objections gracefully on the spot. Trial closes prevent that
scenario entirely.

### 2. You Didn't Uncover Their Influencers During Fact-Finding

The husband's "buddy at work" and Suze Orman references, plus the wife's
"daughter who works in finance" — these are **third-party influencers** that
should have been identified in your first meeting during the
discovery/fact-finding phase. Specifically, question #10 in the fact-finding
framework: _"Who else influences your financial decisions?"_ and _"Do you work
with a CPA, attorney, or other advisor?"_

If you had known that (a) the husband has a coworker who opines on financial
products, (b) the wife follows Suze Orman, and (c) they have a daughter in
finance, you could have preemptively addressed these in your presentation by
inoculating against each perspective before they ever became objections.

### 3. You Didn't Use the A-E-R Framework

The **A-E-R (Acknowledge-Empathize-Redirect) framework** is the standard method
for handling every single objection. It sounds like you may have tried to argue
or explain your way through each concern rather than following the structured
approach of (1) Acknowledge the concern, (2) Empathize with why they feel that
way, and (3) Redirect with a question or reframe. When you skip A-E-R and jump
straight to defending, the client feels unheard, and subsequent objections
become more entrenched.

### 4. You Forgot the Most Important Reframe

**Objections are buying signals.** Every one of those objections — every single
one — means the couple is engaged and thinking about this seriously. A couple
that isn't interested doesn't argue. They smile, nod, and never return your
calls. This couple argued because they're interested but scared. That's
workable. That's actually a good sign.

Below are specific, word-for-word scripts using the **A-E-R

Practice these out loud until they feel natural.

### Objection #1: "My buddy at work told me annuities are a scam and the insurance company keeps your money when you die."

This is the **#1 myth** in the annuity business. It's also the easiest to
dismantle with facts.

**Your word-for-word response:**

> **[Acknowledge]** "John, I appreciate you sharing that. Your friend is looking
> out for you, and I respect that."
>
> **[Empathize]** "And honestly, I understand why people think that — there's a
> lot of misinformation out there about annuities. If I thought the insurance
> company kept my money when I died, I wouldn't buy one either."
>
> **[Redirect]** "May I share something that might clear this up? The idea that
> 'the insurance company keeps your money' is simply not how modern annuities
> work. When we set up an annuity with an income rider, there are several
> specific payout options that protect your beneficiaries. Let me walk you
> through them:
>
> First, there's the **period-certain option** — for example, a **10-year
> certain** or **20-year certain** payout. This means even if you pass away in
> year 3, your beneficiaries continue receiving your income payments for the
> remaining 7 or 17 years. The insurance company doesn't keep a dime.
>
> Second, there's the **cash refund option**. This guarantees that if you pass
> away before receiving back your full original premium, your beneficiary gets a
> lump-sum check for the difference. So if you put in $300,000 and have only
> received $100,000 in income payments, your beneficiary gets a check for
> $200,000. Period.
>
> Third, there's the **installment refund option**, which works the same way but
> pays the remaining amount to your beneficiary in installments rather than a
> lump sum.
>
> And fourth — and this is the one most couples choose — there's the
> **joint-life option**. This means the income continues at the same level for
> as long as EITHER of you is alive. If John passes first, Mary keeps getting
> the exact same paycheck. If Mary passes first, John keeps getting the exact
> same paycheck. The income only stops when the last of you passes — and even
> then, any remaining account value goes to your beneficiaries.
>
> So when your friend says the insurance company 'keeps your money,' what he's
> probably thinking of is a straight life-only annuity with no period-certain or
> refund feature — which is just one option that very few people choose. Does
> that help clarify things?"

**Then use a trial close:** "John, if you knew that your full investment was
protected and would either come back to you as income or go to your
beneficiaries, would that change how you feel about this?"

### Objection #2: "I saw Suze Orman say never buy an annuity."

**Your word-for-word response:**

> **[Acknowledge]** "Mary, you're not the first person to bring that up. Suze
> Orman has a huge following and people trust her."
>
> **[Empathize]** "And I think it's smart that you research things before making
> a financial decision. That tells me you take this seriously."
>
> **[Redirect]** "May I share some context on what Suze Orman is actually
> criticizing? When she says 'never buy an annuity,' she's primarily talking
> about **variable annuities** — which are investment products that carry high
> fees, often 2-3% per year, and where you can actually lose money in the
> market. I agree with her on variable annuities. I don't sell them.
>
> What I'm recommending for you is a **Fixed Indexed Annuity**, which is a
> completely different product. It has zero annual fees on the base contract,
> your principal is guaranteed — you can never lose money due to market drops —
> and it's designed specifically to create a guaranteed paycheck for life. Suze
> Orman saying 'all annuities are bad' is like a doctor saying 'all medication
> is bad.' There are over 10 types, and they serve very different purposes.
>
> Here's something interesting: Warren Buffett — one of the most successful
> investors in history — his company, Berkshire Hathaway, is one of the largest
> annuity issuers in the world. He understands that annuities are the only
> financial product that can guarantee income you can't outlive. Would it be
> helpful if I showed you the specific differences between the type Suze
> criticizes and the type I'm recommending?"

**Trial close:** "Mary, if you could see for yourself that the specific product
I'm recommending is fundamentally different from what Suze was criticizing — no
market risk, no high fees — would you be open to evaluating it on its own
merits?"

### Objection #3: "Why would I lock my money up for 10 years when I can just keep it in my brokerage account and withdraw 4% per year?"

This is a comparison/returns objection. The key reframe: annuities aren't
competing with the market — they're replacing the pension his parents had.
**Paychecks vs. Playchecks.**

**Your word-for-word response:**

> **[Acknowledge]** "John, that's a very fair question, and the 4% rule is
> something a lot of people have heard about."
>
> **[Empathize]** "I understand the appeal of keeping full control and
> flexibility with your investments. Nobody wants to feel like their money is
> locked away."
>
> **[Redirect]** "May I share why the 4% rule might actually be the riskier
> option? The 4% withdrawal rule was created by Bill Bengen in 1994, and it
> assumes a specific mix of stocks and bonds over a 30-year retirement. But
> here's what most people don't know: even Bengen himself has said the 4% rule
> doesn't work in all market conditions.
>
> Let me show you the math. Let's say you have $500,000 in your brokerage
> account. The market drops 30% the year after you retire — which happened in
> 2008 and again in early 2020. Now you have $350,000. You still need to
> withdraw $20,000 for living expenses. So now you're pulling from a shrunken
> balance. Even when the market recovers 30% the following year, your $330,000
> only grows to $429,000 — not back to your original $500,000. And you've
> withdrawn another $20,000, so you're at $409,000. That's **sequence of returns
> risk**, and it has destroyed more retirement plans than any other single
> factor.
>
> Now here's the different way to think about it. This isn't your Playcheck
> money — this is your Paycheck money. Your parents probably had a pension from
> their employer. It paid them a guaranteed check every month for life, and they
> never compared it to the stock market. That's exactly what this annuity does —
> it creates a personal pension. The rest of your money — your brokerage
> account, your other investments — that stays invested. That's your Playcheck
> money for travel, hobbies, fun. We don't touch it. In fact, because your
> essential bills are covered by the guaranteed annuity income, you can actually
> afford to be MORE aggressive with your investment portfolio, not less. This is
> what researchers call **Retirement Alpha** — using annuities to create an
> extra 1-2% of additional portfolio return because you've removed the fear of
> running out of money.
>
> And one more thing about 'locking up' money: most FIAs allow you to withdraw
> up to 10% per year penalty-free. And the reason there's a surrender period is
> the same reason CDs have a term — the longer the guarantee period, the better
> the rate you get. You're not locking money away. You're making a commitment
> that pays you more."
>
> Also keep in mind the concept of **mortality credits**. When you self-manage
> withdrawals, you're planning for the worst case — you might live to 100. So
> you withdraw conservatively. But in an annuity, the insurance company pools
> risk across thousands of people. Some live longer, some shorter. This risk
> pooling creates mortality credits — a mathematical bonus — that allows the
> annuity to pay you a higher income than you could ever safely withdraw on your
> own. You literally cannot replicate this with a brokerage account."

**Trial close:** "John, if the guaranteed income from this annuity covered your
essential monthly bills — your mortgage, utilities, groceries, insurance — and
your brokerage account was free to grow without you needing to touch it, would
that give you more peace of mind or less?"

### Objection #4: "We need to think about it and talk to our daughter who works in finance."

This is two objections in one: (a) a stall/delay tactic, and (b) a third-party
influencer referral. Handle both.

**Your word-for-word response:**

> **[Acknowledge]** "Absolutely. I wouldn't want you to make a decision this
> important without feeling 100% comfortable. And I think it's great that you
> want to involve your daughter — having someone you trust review this is
> smart."
>
> **[Empathize]** "I want you to feel completely confident, and I know this is a
> lot of information to process in one sitting."
>
> **[Redirect]** "Before we wrap up, can I ask you two things? First — what
> specifically would you like to think about? Is it the product itself, the
> amount we discussed, or something else? I ask because if there's something I
> haven't explained clearly, I'd rather address it now so you can think about
> this with all the information you need, not just some of it.
>
> Second — I'd actually love to include your daughter in our next conversation.
> In my experience, when a family member works in finance, they're often
> evaluating a different type of product than what we're discussing. If she
> works at a brokerage firm or bank, she may be thinking about variable
> annuities or market-based products, which are very different from a fixed
> indexed annuity. I'd welcome the chance to walk through this with her so she
> can ask me any questions directly. Would you be open to scheduling our next
> meeting and inviting her? I'm happy to do a phone call or video meeting if
> she's not local.
>
> And just so we're all on the same page — here are some materials that explain
> exactly what we discussed today. I'll include the illustration, a summary of
> the income guarantee, and an explanation of the payout options we talked
> about. That way your daughter can review the actual product, not just the
> general concept of 'annuities.' When would be a good time for us to reconnect
> — say, Tuesday or Thursday of next week?"

**Critical:** Get a specific date. Never leave with "we'll call you." The
follow-up date is the lifeline.

## What You Should Have Done Differently in the Meeting

### Before the Meeting

1. **During your first meeting fact-find, you should have asked:** "Who else
   will be involved in this decision? Do you have a financial advisor, CPA, or
   family member who weighs in on your financial decisions?" This would have
   surfaced the daughter in finance, the husband's coworker, and the wife's
   reliance on media figures like Suze Orman. Knowing these in advance lets you
   build your presentation to preemptively address each influence.

2. **Inoculation slides/talking points:** Knowing the wife watches Suze Orman,
   you could have opened your presentation with: "Before I show you this
   solution, I want to address something. You may have heard media personalities
   say 'never buy an annuity.' I think that's important to discuss because
   they're usually talking about a very different product than what I'm going to
   show you. Let me explain the difference first, so you can evaluate this on
   its own merits." This neutralizes the Suze objection before it ever comes up.

### During the Meeting

3. **Use trial closes after every key concept.** Your presentation should have
   followed this cadence:

   - Present the income gap analysis → "Does that number feel right to you? Is
     $2,400/month about what you'd need?"
   - Explain how FIA works → "Does that make sense so far — the idea that your
     money grows when the market goes up but never loses when the market goes
     down?"
   - Show the income rider guarantee → "How does it feel knowing that this
     income is guaranteed for both of your lifetimes, no matter what the market
     does?"
   - Explain the death benefit/payout options → "John, knowing that Mary would
     continue receiving this income if something happened to you — and that your
     kids would get any remaining value — does that address the concern about
     what happens to the money?"

   Each trial close surfaces one concern at a time. You address it, get a
   micro-agreement, and move on. No avalanche.

4. **When the first objection hit (buddy at work), you should have paused
   everything** and used A-E-R fully before allowing the next objection. Instead
   of trying to briefly address it and move on, you should have said: "Let's
   stop right here because I want to make sure this is fully addressed. This is
   important." Resolve one objection completely before the next one surfaces.

5. **When the wife mentioned Suze Orman, you should have connected it to the
   husband's concern:** "It sounds like you've both been hearing some things
   about annuities that concern you. Let me address all of this, because I think
   once you see the difference between what they're criticizing and what I'm
   recommending, you'll feel very differently."

### After the Meeting (Now — Your Recovery Plan)

6. **Send a handwritten note within 48 hours.** Not an email — a physical,
   handwritten note. This is your **48-hour handwritten note recovery strategy**
   and it works because almost nobody does it anymore. Here's what to write:

   > _"John and Mary —_
   >
   > _Thank you for your time on Tuesday and for your honest questions. Those
   > are exactly the kinds of questions I want my clients to ask. I realize I
   > may not have addressed all of your concerns as clearly as I should have,
   > and I'd love the chance to revisit a few things._
   >
   > _I'd also welcome the opportunity to meet with your daughter — I think
   > she'd appreciate seeing the specifics of what we're considering, and I'm
   > happy to answer any questions she has._
   >
   > _I'll follow up later this week to see if we can schedule a brief meeting._
   >
   > _Warm regards,_ _[Your name]_"

7. **Follow up 2-3 days after the note arrives with a phone call.** Don't email
   — call. Say: "Hi John, it's [name]. I wanted to follow up on our meeting and
   see if you've had a chance to think things over. I also wanted to see if your
   daughter might be available to join us for a quick conversation. I have some
   time next Tuesday afternoon or Thursday morning — would either of those
   work?"

8. **Prepare educational materials (not sales materials) for the daughter.** A
   one-page comparison showing:
   - Variable annuity (what Suze criticizes) vs. Fixed Indexed Annuity (what
     you're recommending)
   - The specific payout options (period-certain, cash refund, installment
     refund, joint-life)
   - The income gap math from their specific situation
   - A brief explanation of mortality credits and why annuity payout rates
     exceed self-managed withdrawal rates

## Key Concepts to Remember

- **Objections are buying signals** — every objection this couple raised means
  they were engaged and seriously considering this. A disinterested couple
  smiles, nods, and ghosts you.
- **A-E-R (Acknowledge-Empathize-Redirect)** — use this framework by name for
  every single objection. Never skip a step, especially Acknowledge. Skipping
  straight to Redirect feels like arguing.
- **Trial closes** prevent objection avalanches — check in after every concept
  to surface concerns one at a time.
- **Paychecks and Playchecks** — the annuity is the paycheck (guaranteed
  essential income); the brokerage account is the playcheck
  (growth/discretionary). They aren't competing — they serve different purposes.
- **Mortality credits** — the mathematical advantage of risk pooling that makes
  annuity payouts higher than any safe DIY withdrawal rate.
- **Retirement Alpha** — the 1-2% additional portfolio return created by using
  annuities to reduce sequence risk, enabling more aggressive allocation of
  remaining assets.
- **48-hour handwritten note recovery strategy** — a physical handwritten note
  within 48 hours, followed by a phone call, with an invitation to include the
  third-party influencer (daughter) in the next meeting. Schedule a specific
  date. Never leave it open-ended.
- **Death benefit payout options** you must name: **period-certain** (10-year
  certain, 20-year certain), **cash refund option**, **installment refund
  option**, and **joint-life option**. These are the protections built into most
  annuities that destroy the "company keeps your money" myth.

You haven't lost this couple. You've hit a speed bump. Follow the recovery plan,
bring the daughter in, and use trial closes in the next meeting. The fact that
they argued with you is the best sign you could ask for.
