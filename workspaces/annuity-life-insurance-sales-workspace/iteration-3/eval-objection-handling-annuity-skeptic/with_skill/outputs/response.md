# Objection Handling Debrief: FIA with Income Rider — Couple Meeting Recovery

## Situation Assessment

You experienced what we call an **objection avalanche** — multiple objections
stacking up from both spouses in quick succession. This feels overwhelming in
the moment, but here's the critical reframe: **objections are buying signals.**
Every single objection they raised means they were _engaged_ and _thinking_
about this decision. A truly disinterested couple would have said "no thanks"
and walked you out. The fact that they raised four distinct objections tells me
they are seriously considering this — they just need their concerns resolved.

The real problem wasn't the objections themselves. It was that they came all at
once, which suggests you didn't use **trial closes** during the presentation to
surface and resolve concerns incrementally. More on that below.

Let's break down exactly what happened, how to handle each objection using the
**A-E-R (Acknowledge-Empathize-Redirect)** framework, and what to do differently
going forward.

---

## Objection #1: "My buddy at work told me annuities are a scam and the insurance company keeps your money when you die."

### What's Really Going On

This is a **third-party influence objection** combined with a **misinformation
objection**. The husband didn't arrive at this conclusion through research —
he's repeating something he heard from someone he trusts. The "scam" label and
the "company keeps your money" myth are two of the most common annuity
misconceptions, usually rooted in confusion about SPIAs (single premium
immediate annuities with life-only payout options) being applied to ALL annuity
types.

### A-E-R (Acknowledge-Empathize-Redirect) Response

**Word-for-word script:**

> **[Acknowledge]** "I'm really glad you brought that up, and I appreciate that
> you're looking out for your family by asking tough questions. Your buddy
> clearly cares about you too, and I respect that."
>
> **[Empathize]** "You know, that concern comes up a lot, and I completely
> understand why. There's a ton of misinformation out there about annuities, and
> honestly, some of the older annuity designs deserved criticism."
>
> **[Redirect]** "But may I share something that might change the picture? What
> your buddy is describing sounds like one very specific type of annuity — a
> life-only immediate annuity — where income stops at death. That's NOT what I'm
> recommending for you. The fixed indexed annuity we're looking at works
> completely differently. Here's what actually happens if something were to
> happen to you, [Husband's name]: your wife receives the full remaining account
> value as the beneficiary. The insurance company does NOT keep your money.
> Period. In fact, let me show you exactly where it says that in the contract —
> right here in the beneficiary provision. Would your buddy be surprised to
> learn that?"

**Then add this powerful reframe:**

> "Here's what's interesting — by using this annuity to guarantee your essential
> income, we're actually _protecting_ the rest of your investment portfolio from
> being drawn down. That means your brokerage account has a better chance of
> _growing_ for your heirs because you're not depleting it for living expenses.
> So this plan actually _improves_ your legacy, not reduces it. Can I show you
> the numbers on that?"

### Key Facts to Have Ready

- The FIA with income rider passes remaining account value to named
  beneficiaries upon death
- Many FIA income riders include a "death benefit enhancement" — if the client
  dies during the accumulation phase, beneficiaries may receive the greater of
  account value or income base
- Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway is one of the largest annuity issuers in
  the world — would the greatest investor of all time run a "scam"?
- Saying "all annuities are scams" is like saying "all medicine is bad" — there
  are over 10 distinct types, each serving a different purpose

## Objection #2: "I saw Suze Orman say never buy an annuity."

### What's Really Going On

This is the **media pundit objection** — the wife is deferring to a celebrity
authority figure. Suze Orman (and Dave Ramsey) have painted ALL annuities with
the same broad brush. What they're actually criticizing are **variable
annuities** with 2-3% annual fees and complicated fee structures. They rarely
distinguish between variable annuities and fixed/fixed indexed annuities, which
have fundamentally different economics.

### A-E-R (Acknowledge-Empathize-Redirect) Response

**Word-for-word script:**

> **[Acknowledge]** "[Wife's name], I've seen those segments too, and Suze Orman
> is a smart woman. I actually agree with some of what she says."
>
> **[Empathize]** "It makes total sense that you'd pay attention to someone like
> her — she's given a lot of people good general financial advice, and I respect
> that you're doing your homework."
>
> **[Redirect]** "But may I share something important? When Suze Orman says
> 'never buy an annuity,' she's specifically talking about variable annuities —
> the kind with 2-3% annual fees that are tied to the stock market. And
> honestly? I agree with her on that. I wouldn't recommend a variable annuity to
> you either. But what I'm recommending is a _fixed indexed_ annuity, which has
> _zero_ annual fees on the base contract and guarantees you can never lose a
> penny of your principal to market losses. It's a completely different product.
> That's like saying 'never take medicine' because one specific drug had bad
> side effects. Would you be open to learning how this specific product works so
> you can make your own informed decision?"

**Then add the credibility pivot:**

> "Here's a question to consider: Suze Orman is worth about $75 million. She
> doesn't need guaranteed retirement income — she's already set for life. But
> for families like yours, where we've identified a $[X]/month income gap
> between your guaranteed income sources and your essential expenses, the math
> tells a very different story. Suze's advice is great for Suze. Let's talk
> about what the math says for _your_ situation."

## Objection #3: "Why would I lock my money up for 10 years when I can just keep it in my brokerage account and withdraw 4% per year?"

### What's Really Going On

This is the **market comparison objection** combined with the
**liquidity/surrender period objection**. The husband is making two fundamental
errors: (1) comparing an annuity to an investment portfolio (wrong comparison —
it should be compared to a pension), and (2) assuming the 4% withdrawal rule is
safe and guaranteed (it is neither).

### A-E-R (Acknowledge-Empathize-Redirect) Response

**Word-for-word script:**

> **[Acknowledge]** "That's a really good question, [Husband's name], and you're
> clearly someone who thinks critically about your money. The 4% rule is one of
> the most well-known concepts in retirement planning."
>
> **[Empathize]** "I understand the appeal of maintaining full control and
> flexibility with your brokerage account. Nobody wants to feel like their money
> is trapped."
>
> **[Redirect]** "But let me ask you something: does your brokerage account
> guarantee that 4% withdrawal will last your entire life? What happens if you
> retire and the market drops 30% in year one? Let me show you the math on that
> because it's eye-opening."

**Then walk through the sequence of returns risk scenario:**

> "Let's say you have $500,000 in your brokerage account and you're withdrawing
> $20,000 a year — that's your 4% rule. Now the market drops 30% in your first
> year of retirement, like it did in 2008. Your $500,000 is now $350,000. Even
> if the market recovers 30% the next year, you're only at $455,000 — and that's
> _before_ your withdrawals. After taking your $20,000 income, you're at
> $435,000. You started with $500,000 and after two years — even with a market
> recovery — you've lost $65,000 and you're withdrawing from a shrinking base.
> That's called **sequence of returns risk**, and it has destroyed more
> retirement plans than any other single factor."

**Then pivot to the Paychecks and Playchecks framework:**

> "Here's what I actually recommend: we don't choose one _or_ the other. We use
> _both_. The annuity becomes your **paycheck** — guaranteed income that covers
> your essential bills regardless of what the market does. Your brokerage
> account becomes your **playcheck** — growth money for vacations, gifts,
> extras. And here's the really powerful part: because your essential expenses
> are covered by the annuity, you can actually afford to be _more_ aggressive
> with your brokerage account, not less. You don't have to panic-sell in a
> downturn because your bills are already covered. Financial researchers call
> this **Retirement Alpha** — using annuities optimally can add 1-2% of
> additional return to your overall retirement portfolio. You get the guarantee
> AND the growth. Does that change the picture for you?"

**Address the "locked up" concern directly:**

> "And on the 'locked up' point — I'd never recommend putting all your money
> into an annuity. We're talking about using a portion — specifically the amount
> needed to cover your income gap — and leaving the rest fully liquid. Plus,
> most FIA contracts allow you to withdraw up to 10% of the account value per
> year with zero penalty, even during the surrender period. So you still have
> access. Does that help with the liquidity concern?"

### Key Concepts to Emphasize

- **Mortality credits** — the mathematical advantage annuity owners get from
  risk pooling. This is why an annuity can guarantee a higher payout rate than
  what self-managed 4% withdrawals provide. When you pool risk with thousands of
  other policyholders, the insurance company can pay out more to each individual
  because not everyone will live to 100.
- The original 4% rule (the "Bengen rule") was based on a 30-year retirement
  horizon and a 50/50 portfolio. With today's longer retirements and lower
  expected returns, many researchers now suggest 3% or even 2.8% is the safer
  number.
- The annuity isn't "competing" with the brokerage account — it's replacing the
  pension his parents probably had.

## Objection #4: "We need to think about it and talk to our daughter who works in finance."

### What's Really Going On

This is actually **two objections in one**: (1) "I need to think about it" —
which typically means there are unresolved concerns they haven't voiced yet, and
(2) "we want to check with a trusted third-party influencer." The daughter in
finance is a significant factor. She likely works in wealth management or a
similar field and may have a built-in bias against annuities — either from her
firm's culture, her own training, or the structural incentive that money in
annuities is money not under her (or her firm's) advisory fee umbrella.

### A-E-R (Acknowledge-Empathize-Redirect) Response

**Word-for-word script:**

> **[Acknowledge]** "Of course — I would never want you to make a decision this
> important without being completely comfortable. And it speaks really well of
> your family that you include your daughter in these conversations."
>
> **[Empathize]** "This is your retirement security we're talking about. Taking
> time to think it through and getting input from people you trust is exactly
> the right approach. I encourage it."
>
> **[Redirect]** "May I make a suggestion? Rather than you trying to explain all
> of this to your daughter secondhand, what if we invited her to our next
> meeting? That way she can ask me any question she wants directly, see the
> actual numbers, and you'll have all three perspectives in the room. I welcome
> the scrutiny — if this plan can't hold up to your daughter's questions, then
> it's not the right plan for you. Would next [specific day] or [specific day]
> work for the three of you?"

**Then use a diagnostic question to uncover the real concern:**

> "And before we wrap up today — just so I can be as prepared as possible for
> our next conversation — on a scale of 1 to 10, how comfortable are you with
> what we've discussed so far? [Wait for answer.] And what would it take to move
> that to a 10?"

### Why This Response Works

- You're not fighting the daughter's involvement — you're **embracing** it and
  turning a potential adversary into a participant
- By inviting her to the meeting, you demonstrate total confidence in your
  recommendation
- You're scheduling a **specific follow-up date**, not leaving it open-ended
  ("we'll call you") which almost always means a lost sale
- The 1-10 question uncovers what their _actual_ concern is — it's often
  something they haven't said out loud yet

## What You Should Have Done Differently in the Meeting

### Mistake #1: No Trial Closes During the Presentation

**The root cause of the objection avalanche.** You presented the entire solution
and then opened the floor, allowing all four objections to hit at once. Instead,
you should have used **trial closes** — small check-in questions after each
section of your presentation that build incremental agreement and surface
concerns one at a time.

**What trial closes look like in practice:**

- After explaining the income gap: _"Does it make sense that we'd want to cover
  that $[X]/month gap with guaranteed income? How does that feel so far?"_
- After explaining how the FIA works: _"Does the concept of participating in
  market gains without risking losses make sense to you?"_
- After showing the illustration: _"Looking at these guaranteed income numbers,
  does this solve the problem we identified?"_
- After discussing beneficiaries: _"Does it give you some peace of mind knowing
  this passes directly to your beneficiaries?"_

Each "yes" builds momentum. Each concern surfaces immediately and gets resolved
before the next topic. You never let objections pile up.

### Mistake #2: Not Pre-Qualifying Third-Party Influencers During Fact-Finding

During your fact-finding/discovery meeting (Meeting #1), you should have asked
the **decision-making and influencers** questions from the discovery framework:

- _"Besides the two of you, is there anyone else who influences your financial
  decisions? A CPA, an attorney, a family member?"_
- _"Do you have any family members in the financial industry?"_
- _"Where do you typically get financial information — TV, websites, friends,
  books?"_

If you had uncovered the daughter in finance, the work buddy's opinions, and the
wife's reliance on Suze Orman during Meeting #1, you could have **inoculated
against each of these objections** during your presentation. For example:

> "Now, I know some media personalities say negative things about annuities. And
> I want to be upfront about that — most of that criticism is aimed at variable
> annuities with high fees, which is NOT what we're looking at today. Let me
> show you exactly how this product is different..."

By addressing the objection _before_ they raise it, you take the wind out of its
sails.

### Mistake #3: Getting Overwhelmed Instead of Controlling the Flow

When multiple objections come at once, you need to **slow down and triage**.
Here's the technique:

> "I can tell you both have some really important questions, and I want to give
> each one the attention it deserves. Let me take these one at a time —
> [Husband's name], let's start with your concern about [first objection]. Then,
> [Wife's name], I want to make sure I fully address your point about Suze
> Orman. Sound good?"

This does three things:

1. Shows you're listening and taking them seriously
2. Prevents the objections from compounding emotionally
3. Gives you control of the conversation flow

### Mistake #4: Not Addressing Both Spouses Individually

In a couple meeting, both spouses need to feel heard. Direct your responses to
the person who raised each concern. Make eye contact with them. Use their name.
When addressing the husband's concern, acknowledge the wife too: _"[Wife's
name], I want to make sure this makes sense to you as well — does it?"_

## Recovery Plan: Getting Them Back for Meeting #3

You have NOT lost this sale. The fact that they raised objections means they
were engaged. Here is your step-by-step recovery plan:

### Step 1: Send a Handwritten Note Within 48 Hours

Not an email. Not a text. A **handwritten note** on personal stationery, mailed
to their home. This differentiates you from every other financial professional
they've ever met.

**Sample note:**

> Dear [Husband's name] and [Wife's name],
>
> Thank you for taking the time to meet with me again on [day]. I truly
> appreciate your thoughtful questions — they tell me you take your retirement
> planning seriously, and that's exactly the kind of client I love working with.
>
> I realize I may not have addressed all of your concerns as clearly as I should
> have, and I want to make that right. I've been thinking about your situation,
> and I have some additional information I'd like to share that I think will be
> really helpful.
>
> I'd also love the opportunity to meet your daughter — her financial background
> would add a valuable perspective to our conversation, and I welcome her
> questions.
>
> I'll give you a call on [specific day] to see if we can find a time that works
> for all three of you.
>
> Warm regards, [Your name]

### Step 2: Prepare Educational (Not Sales) Materials

Before the follow-up call, prepare a packet of **educational materials** to send
or bring to the next meeting:

- A one-page comparison: "Variable Annuities vs. Fixed Indexed Annuities"
  (addresses both the Suze Orman and the "scam" objections visually)
- A simple illustration of sequence of returns risk (addresses the 4% withdrawal
  objection)
- A copy of the beneficiary provision from the specific FIA contract (addresses
  the "company keeps your money" myth with actual contractual proof)
- A brief, factual summary of how state guaranty associations work

### Step 3: Call to Schedule Meeting #3 — Include the Daughter

When you call, the framing is:

> "Hi [Husband's name], this is [Your name]. I wanted to follow up on our
> conversation and see how you're feeling about things. I've also put together
> some additional information that I think will really help. I'd love to set up
> a time to sit down again — and I meant what I said about including your
> daughter. I want her to hear directly from me and ask any questions she has.
> What does your schedule look like next [day] or [day]?"

**Key:** Propose **specific dates**, not "sometime." Open-ended follow-ups die.

### Step 4: Prepare for the Daughter

The daughter in finance is either your biggest obstacle or your best ally.
Prepare for her by:

- **Anticipating her objections**: She'll likely raise fee concerns (addressed
  by the zero-fee base contract), liquidity concerns (addressed by the 10% free
  withdrawal and the fact that not all assets are in the annuity), and
  opportunity cost arguments (addressed by Retirement Alpha and the Paychecks &
  Playchecks framework)
- **Acknowledging her expertise immediately**: _"[Daughter's name], I really
  appreciate you being here. Your parents speak highly of you, and I welcome
  your perspective. I'm sure you'll have tough questions — I want you to ask all
  of them."_
- **Framing her as an ally, not an adversary**: _"My goal is the same as yours —
  to make sure your mom and dad are financially secure in retirement. If what
  I'm recommending doesn't hold up to your scrutiny, then it's not the right
  plan. Fair enough?"_
- **If she's a fee-based advisor**: Be prepared for the structural incentive
  conversation (diplomatically). Money in an annuity is money not generating
  advisory fees. You don't need to _say_ this directly, but be ready if the
  topic arises: _"I know that in the wealth management world, the perspective on
  annuities can be different. I'd love to hear your specific concerns so we can
  look at the math together."_

### Step 5: Structure Meeting #3 Differently

**Open with a reset:**

> "Before I dive into anything, I want to start by thanking you for giving me
> another opportunity. Last time, I think I could have done a better job
> addressing your concerns individually, and I want to make sure we do that
> today. I'd like to walk through each of the questions you raised, one at a
> time, and make sure everyone — including [daughter's name] — is completely
> comfortable before we move on to the next one. Sound fair?"

**Use trial closes after every single point** — do NOT let objections pile up
again.

**End with a clear decision point:**

> "So based on everything we've discussed — and [daughter's name], I especially
> want your honest take — does this plan make sense for your parents' situation?
> What questions remain?"

## The Bottom Line

You didn't lose this client — you just got caught off-guard. The objections they
raised are among the most common in annuity sales, and every single one has a
clear, factual answer. The real lesson here is structural:

1. **Uncover influencers during fact-finding** so you can inoculate during
   presentation
2. **Use trial closes throughout** so objections surface one at a time
3. **Use the A-E-R framework by name** in your head during every objection:
   Acknowledge, Empathize, Redirect
4. **Remember that objections are buying signals** — silence is the real danger
   sign
5. **Always have a specific follow-up plan** — handwritten note, educational
   materials, specific date, include the influencer

Send that handwritten note today. Call them in two days. Get the daughter in the
room. You've got this.
