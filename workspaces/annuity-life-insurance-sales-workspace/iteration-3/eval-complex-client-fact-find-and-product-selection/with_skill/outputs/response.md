## Part 2: Income Gap Analysis (Paychecks and Playchecks Framework)

This is the mathematical heart of your presentation. **Every recommendation must
be grounded in specific dollar amounts, not vague concepts.** The Paychecks and
Playchecks framework (Tom Hegna) says every retiree needs guaranteed lifetime
income (Paychecks) to cover essential expenses, plus growth-oriented assets
(Playchecks) for discretionary spending.

### Scenario A: Robert Claims Social Security at 62 (Now)

**Guaranteed monthly income:**

| Source                         | Monthly Amount   |
| ------------------------------ | ---------------- |
| Robert's SS (claimed at 62)    | $1,900           |
| Carol's SS (not yet available) | $0               |
| Pension                        | $0               |
| **Total guaranteed income**    | **$1,900/month** |

**Income gap = $6,800 - $1,900 = $4,900/month ($58,800/year)**

This is a massive gap. For at least the next 3 years (until Carol's part-time
income ends), they have Carol's $2,083/month helping. But once she stops
working:

- **Gap with Carol working:** $6,800 - $1,900 - $2,083 = **$2,717/month**
- **Gap after Carol stops working (age 62):** $6,800 - $1,900 = **$4,900/month**
- **Gap after Carol claims SS (at FRA):** $6,800 - $1,900 -
  $1,100 = **$3,800/month ($45,600/year)**

### Scenario B: Robert Delays Social Security to 66 (Recommended)

**Guaranteed monthly income at Robert's age 66:**

| Source                                          | Monthly Amount   |
| ----------------------------------------------- | ---------------- |
| Robert's SS (at FRA 66)                         | $2,400           |
| Carol's SS (still not available — she'll be 63) | $0               |
| Pension                                         | $0               |
| **Total at Robert's 66**                        | **$2,400/month** |

**Full household guaranteed income once Carol claims SS at her FRA:**

| Source                  | Monthly Amount   |
| ----------------------- | ---------------- |
| Robert's SS (at FRA 66) | $2,400           |
| Carol's SS (at FRA)     | $1,100           |
| **Total**               | **$3,500/month** |

**Permanent income gap (both SS benefits active) = $6,800 - $3,500 =
$3,300/month ($39,600/year)**

### Scenario C: Robert Delays to 70 (Maximum Benefit — Explore This)

If Robert delays to 70, his benefit grows roughly 8% per year (delayed
retirement credits):

- Robert's SS at 70: approximately **$3,168/month** (32% increase over FRA)
- Combined with Carol at FRA: $3,168 + $1,100 = **$4,268/month**
- **Gap: $6,800 - $4,268 = $2,532/month ($30,384/year)**

This dramatically reduces the gap but requires 8 years of living off assets. We
need to determine if they can bridge that gap.

### The "Paycheck" Need — Summary

| Scenario            | Permanent Monthly Gap  | Annual Gap | Notes                                |
| ------------------- | ---------------------- | ---------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Robert claims at 62 | $3,800 (after both SS) | $45,600    | Lowest SS, highest gap               |
| Robert claims at 66 | $3,300 (after both SS) | $39,600    | Moderate — likely best balance       |
| Robert claims at 70 | $2,532 (after both SS) | $30,384    | Lowest gap but longest bridge period |

**Recommendation to explore:** Robert delays SS to at least 66, possibly 67-68.
Use a portion of the 401(k) to bridge the income gap during the delay years.
Every year of delay increases his guaranteed lifetime income by ~8% — this is
effectively a risk-free return and the best "annuity" available. You need to
calculate the breakeven (typically around age 80-82 for delaying from 62 to 66).

### The "Playcheck" Need

After covering the income gap with guaranteed sources (SS + annuity income),
remaining assets are **Playchecks** — growth-oriented assets for discretionary
spending (travel, gifts, hobbies, legacy). These assets can be invested more
aggressively precisely BECAUSE the essential expenses are already covered by
guarantees.

This is the **Retirement Alpha** concept — research shows that by using
annuities to cover essential expenses, the remaining portfolio can be allocated
more aggressively, generating an estimated 1-2% of additional portfolio return.
The annuity doesn't just provide income — it improves the ENTIRE retirement plan
by removing the need to sell equities during downturns.

## Part 4: Product Recommendations — Initial Framework

**Important:** Do NOT present these products at tomorrow's meeting. Tomorrow is
fact-finding ONLY. Present the solution at the second meeting after you've
analyzed everything and run illustrations. The cardinal rule: complete the full
fact-find before mentioning ANY product name.

That said, here is your preliminary product strategy based on what you know:

### Tier 1: The Paycheck — Guaranteed Lifetime Income (Highest Priority)

**Product: Fixed Indexed Annuity (FIA) with Guaranteed Lifetime Withdrawal
Benefit (GLWB) Income Rider**

- **Purpose:** Fill the permanent income gap ($3,300-$3,800/month depending on
  SS timing)
- **Why FIA vs. SPIA:** Robert is 62 — he has time to defer. An FIA with income
  rider allows the income benefit base to grow at a guaranteed roll-up rate
  (typically 7-8% simple) during the deferral period. By age 67, a $400,000 FIA
  could generate approximately $2,000-$2,400/month in guaranteed lifetime income
  (joint life option with Carol).
- **Suggested premium range:** $350,000-$450,000 from the 401(k) rollover
  (approximately 45-58% of his qualified assets — within the 50-60% maximum
  guideline)
- **Why it fits Robert:** Zero downside risk (0% floor) addresses his 2008
  trauma. Guaranteed income for life addresses the no-pension problem. Joint
  life option protects Carol.
- **Mortality credits:** Explain that annuity payout rates exceed what
  self-managed withdrawals can safely provide because of risk pooling — people
  who die earlier effectively subsidize those who live longer. This is the
  mathematical advantage of annuitization that Robert cannot replicate on his
  own, no matter how well he invests.
- **Income rider math example (illustrative):**
  - Premium: $400,000
  - Income benefit base roll-up: 7% simple for 5 years
  - Income base at age 67: $400,000 + ($28,000 × 5) = $540,000
  - GLWB rate at age 67 (joint life): ~5.25%
  - Guaranteed annual income: $540,000 × 5.25% = **$28,350/year ($2,362/month)**
  - This covers roughly 62-72% of the income gap depending on SS timing

**Run illustrations from at least 2-3 carriers** to show Robert you're finding
the best fit, not just selling a product.

### Tier 2: Safe Money / Bridge Strategy

**Product: MYGA (Multi-Year Guaranteed Annuity) — "Annuity CD"**

- **Purpose:** Park a portion of safe money at a guaranteed rate (currently
  4.5-6.0%) while Robert bridges the gap to SS at 66 or later
- **Suggested premium:** $100,000-$150,000 from the 401(k) rollover
- **Term:** 3-5 year MYGA aligned with Robert's SS claiming age
- **Why it fits:** Better than CDs for tax-deferred growth; provides guaranteed
  rate with no market risk; the 10% annual free withdrawal can supplement income
  during the bridge years
- **Laddering strategy:** Consider splitting into a 3-year and 5-year MYGA to
  capture potential rate improvements at maturity

### Tier 3: QLAC — Qualified Longevity Annuity Contract (Longevity Insurance)

**Product: QLAC purchased with IRA/401(k) money**

- **Purpose:** Provide guaranteed income starting at age 80-85 as longevity
  insurance, while excluding the premium from RMD calculations
- **Suggested premium:** $200,000 (the current maximum allowed under SECURE 2.0)
- **Why it fits Robert:**
  - He has $900,000 in qualified money — $200,000 is only 22% of qualified
    assets
  - Reduces his RMD tax burden starting at age 73 (the $200,000 is excluded from
    the RMD calculation)
  - Provides substantial guaranteed income starting at 80 or 85, precisely when
    longevity risk is highest
  - His mother lived long enough to need 4 years of nursing home care —
    longevity runs in his family
  - Return of premium option protects Carol if Robert dies before income starts
- **QLAC math example:**
  - $200,000 premium at age 62, income begins at age 80
  - Estimated income: approximately **$2,800-$3,500/month** (rates vary by
    carrier) — this provides a massive income boost in late retirement when
    healthcare costs peak
  - Without QLAC: the $200,000 generates approximately $7,300/year in forced
    RMDs at age 73 (using factor of ~27.4) that Robert may not need and will be
    taxed on

### Tier 4: Long-Term Care Protection (Critical Given Family History)

**Product: Hybrid Life Insurance with LTC Rider OR Asset-Based LTC**

- **Purpose:** Address Robert's #1 emotional concern — his mother's nursing home
  experience that "wiped out everything"
- **Options to explore:**
  1. **Hybrid life/LTC policy** — Single premium or limited-pay permanent life
     insurance with an LTC acceleration rider. If Robert never needs LTC, it
     pays a death benefit. If he does need care, the death benefit is
     accelerated to pay for it. This solves the "use it or lose it" objection
     that kills standalone LTC sales.
  2. **Annuity with LTC rider** — Some FIAs offer LTC benefit multipliers (e.g.,
     2x or 3x the monthly income if the client is receiving qualified LTC)
  3. **Standalone LTC insurance** — Traditional option but premiums can increase
     over time; less attractive at his age
- **Suggested approach:** Fund a hybrid life/LTC policy with a portion of the
  non-qualified assets ($50,000-$75,000 from the joint brokerage account). This
  preserves the tax-advantaged growth on qualified money while providing LTC
  protection.
- **Why it fits:** His mother's experience is not a hypothetical risk — it's a
  demonstrated family pattern. Address this head-on.

### Tier 5: Remaining Assets — The "Playchecks"

After allocating assets to guaranteed income (FIA + QLAC) and protection (LTC),
the remaining assets become Playchecks — growth money for discretionary
spending, legacy, and inflation protection.

**Estimated remaining assets after annuity and insurance allocations:**

| Starting Asset            | Allocated                                       | Remaining     |
| ------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | ------------- |
| Robert's 401(k): $780,000 | ~$400K FIA + $200K QLAC + $100K MYGA            | ~$80,000      |
| Carol's IRA: $120,000     | Keep fully invested (her money, her retirement) | $120,000      |
| Joint brokerage: $85,000  | ~$60K to hybrid life/LTC                        | ~$25,000      |
| Savings: $45,000          | Emergency fund — DO NOT TOUCH                   | $45,000       |
| **Total Playchecks**      |                                                 | **~$270,000** |

These remaining assets (~$270,000) can be invested in a diversified portfolio
with a growth tilt precisely BECAUSE the essential expenses are covered by
guaranteed income. This is the **Retirement Alpha** at work — by securing the
floor with annuities, the remaining portfolio can tolerate more equity exposure,
potentially generating 1-2% more in annualized returns over time. Robert doesn't
need to panic-sell in the next downturn because his bills are paid regardless.

**The
$45,000 in savings stays liquid** as an emergency fund. This covers approximately 6.6 months of expenses ($45,000
÷ $6,800) — meets the 6+ month guideline. Do NOT put this into any annuity or
illiquid product.

## Part 5: Presentation Strategy — The Story Framework

When you come back for the second meeting (after running illustrations), present
using this structure:

### Step 1: Recap Their Situation

"Robert and Carol, based on everything we discussed, here's what I understand.
Robert, you just retired after a long career, and Carol, you're planning to join
him in about 3 years. You have about $1 million in savings, no pension, and
you're concerned about three things: making your money last, protecting it from
another market crash like 2008, and making sure a long-term care situation
doesn't wipe you out like it did with your mother. Is that a fair summary?"

### Step 2: Identify the Problem (Income Gap)

"Here's what the math tells us. Your essential expenses are $X per month. Once
you're both receiving Social Security, you'll have $3,500 per month in
guaranteed income. That leaves a gap of $3,300 every single month — $39,600 per
year — that has to come from somewhere. Right now, that 'somewhere' is your
savings. At that rate, if the market cooperates, you might be okay. But if we
get another 2008 in the first few years of your retirement, that gap could
devastate your plan. That's called **sequence of returns risk**, and it's the
single biggest destroyer of retirement plans."

### Step 3: Introduce the Concept (Paychecks and Playchecks)

"Here's how I think about retirement income. Everyone needs two things:
**Paychecks** — guaranteed income that covers your bills no matter what the
market does, no matter how long you live — and **Playchecks** — growth money for
the fun stuff, travel, gifts, hobbies. Social Security is your first Paycheck.
What we need to do is create a second Paycheck to fill that $3,300 gap. Once
your bills are covered by guarantees, everything else becomes a Playcheck — and
you can invest that money for growth without ever losing sleep over a market
correction."

### Step 4: Show the Math (Guaranteed Values)

Present illustrations — FIA income rider, QLAC, MYGA — showing **guaranteed
values only**. Hypothetical projections are supplementary. If the guaranteed
values don't solve the problem, the product isn't the right fit.

"This illustration shows that by putting $400,000 into a fixed indexed annuity
with an income rider and waiting 5 years, you and Carol would receive a
guaranteed $2,362 per month for the rest of BOTH of your lives. Combined with
your Social Security, that covers $5,862 of your $6,800 in expenses — roughly
86% covered by guaranteed sources. The remaining gap is just $938 per month,
easily covered by your Playcheck investments."

### Step 5: Explain the Science — Mortality Credits and Retirement Alpha

"You might wonder — why can an insurance company pay me more than I could just
withdraw on my own? The answer is **mortality credits**. When you join an
annuity pool, the people who pass away earlier effectively subsidize those who
live longer. This mathematical advantage means the insurance company can
guarantee you a payout rate that's higher than any safe withdrawal rate you
could manage on your own. A typical 'safe' withdrawal rate from a portfolio is
4%. The annuity payout rate on this illustration is effectively 5.25% —
guaranteed for life. That extra return is what we call **Retirement Alpha** — it
doesn't just add income, it makes your entire retirement plan more efficient."

### Step 6: Address the Happy Factor

"One more thing, and this matters more than the math. Research consistently
shows that retirees who have guaranteed income — who know their bills are
covered no matter what — are measurably happier, less stressed, and more
confident in retirement. It's called the **Happy Factor**. You told me you don't
want to go through 2008 again. Imagine the next time the market drops 30% and
you turn on CNBC. Instead of panic, you feel... nothing. Because your bills are
paid. Your Paychecks keep coming. The market drop only affects your Playcheck
money, and that money has time to recover because you don't need it for
groceries. That peace of mind? That's what we're really building here."

### Step 7: Address Long-Term Care

"Robert, you told me about your mother. I want to make sure we address that
directly. We're going to build a plan that includes LTC protection so that if
either of you ever needs care, it doesn't wipe out everything you've built. I'll
show you a couple of options — including one where if you never need care, the
money goes to your family as a tax-free death benefit. You don't lose either
way."

## Part 6: Anticipated Objections and A-E-R Responses

Based on Robert's profile, prepare for these objections using the **A-E-R
(Acknowledge-Empathize-Redirect)** framework. A-E-R means: (1) Acknowledge —
validate their concern, never dismiss; (2) Empathize — show you understand why
they feel that way; (3) Redirect — use a question, story, or fact to reframe.

### "I've heard annuities are bad"

- **Acknowledge:** "I appreciate you being upfront about that. Many people have
  heard negative things about annuities."
- **Empathize:** "And honestly, some of those criticisms are valid — for certain
  types of annuities, particularly variable annuities with high fees."
- **Redirect:** "May I ask which type you've heard about? Because there are
  actually over 10 different types, and the ones I'm recommending have zero
  annual fees on the base contract, guarantee you can never lose principal, and
  provide income for life. They're quite different from what financial pundits
  criticize on TV. Would you be open to seeing how they actually work so you can
  decide for yourself?"

### "I don't want to lock up my money"

- **Acknowledge:** "That's a completely fair concern, and I would never
  recommend putting money somewhere you can't access it."
- **Empathize:** "Access to your money is important, especially in retirement
  when unexpected expenses come up."
- **Redirect:** "That's actually why the first thing we did was map out your
  complete picture. Your $45,000 in savings stays completely liquid — we're not
  touching it. Your Playcheck investments stay fully accessible. The annuity
  portion allows 10% free withdrawals per year without penalty. AND we're only
  using a portion of your assets — you'll still have over $270,000 in fully
  liquid investments. Does that address your concern?"

### "I can get better returns in the market"

- **Acknowledge:** "You're absolutely right that the stock market has
  historically outperformed over long periods."
- **Empathize:** "And I understand wanting to maximize growth — that's smart."
- **Redirect:** "But Robert, remember 2008. You lost 30%. Let me show you what
  that means in retirement math. If you retired with $780,000 and the market
  dropped 30% in year one, you'd be down to $546,000. Now add the $58,800 you
  withdrew for living expenses — you're at $487,200. Even if the market bounces
  back 30% next year, you're at $633,360, still below where you started and
  still withdrawing. That's sequence of returns risk. The annuity isn't
  competing with the market — it's your Paycheck. It covers your bills. The rest
  of your money — your Playchecks — stays in the market for growth. You get
  both: guarantees AND market participation."

### "I need to think about it"

- **Acknowledge:** "Of course — this is one of the most important financial
  decisions you'll make."
- **Empathize:** "I want you to be 100% comfortable."
- **Redirect:** "Help me understand — what specifically would you like to think
  about? Is it the concept, the amount, or something else? I want to make sure
  I've given you everything you need. And I want Carol to be completely
  comfortable too. Can we schedule a follow-up for next week so I can answer any
  questions that come up?"

### "What if the insurance company goes bankrupt?"

- **Acknowledge:** "That's an excellent question, and one that shows you're
  thinking carefully about this."
- **Empathize:** "After 2008, it's natural to question the stability of any
  financial institution."
- **Redirect:** "Insurance companies are actually among the most heavily
  regulated financial institutions in America — more regulated than banks. Every
  state has a guaranty association that protects policyholders, similar to FDIC.
  The carriers I work with all have A-rated or higher financial strength ratings
  from AM Best. These companies have survived the Great Depression, 2008, the
  pandemic, and every crisis in between without missing a single payment to
  policyholders. In fact, when banks were failing in 2008, insurance companies
  were buying them."

### "My son/daughter/neighbor says annuities are a scam"

- **Acknowledge:** "It sounds like you have someone who cares about you and is
  looking out for your interests. That's great."
- **Empathize:** "I appreciate that they're protective of you."
- **Redirect:** "I'd actually love to include them in our next meeting so they
  can ask me any questions directly. I have nothing to hide — everything I
  recommend is backed by math, and I'll show all the guaranteed values, fees,
  and surrender schedules. Would that be helpful?"

**Important:** Use **trial closes** throughout the presentation — "Does that
make sense so far?" and "How does that sound to you?" — to surface objections
one at a time instead of facing an avalanche at the end.

## Part 8: Meeting Logistics and Tactical Reminders

### Before You Walk In

1. **Confirm Carol will be at the meeting.** Call or text Robert today: "Looking
   forward to tomorrow. Just want to confirm Carol will be joining us — it's
   important that we're all on the same page." If Carol cannot attend, seriously
   consider rescheduling. A missing spouse = a delayed decision.
2. **Bring a blank fact-finder form** — fill it out during the meeting
3. **Bring a legal pad** — take notes in front of them (shows you're listening)
4. **Dress professionally but not intimidatingly** — match their level
5. **Bring nothing product-related** — no brochures, no illustrations. Tomorrow
   is about THEM.

### Opening the Meeting

- Start with rapport: "Before we get into any numbers, tell me about yourselves.
  What are you excited about in retirement?"
- Frame the meeting: "My goal today isn't to sell you anything. It's to
  understand your complete situation so I can go back to my office and determine
  if there's a way I can help. If there is, I'll come back with a specific plan.
  If there isn't, I'll tell you that too. Fair enough?"
- This removes sales pressure and positions you as a consultant, not a
  salesperson.

### Closing the First Meeting

- Summarize what you've learned: "Based on everything we discussed, your three
  biggest priorities are: [1], [2], and [3]. Is that right?"
- Set the next meeting: "What I'd like to do is take all of this information
  back to my office, run some numbers, and come back with a customized plan. Can
  we schedule that for [specific date and time next week]?"
- Leave them with one thought: "Robert, you said you don't want to go through
  2008 again. I'm going to show you a way to make sure you never have to."
- **Never leave without a scheduled next meeting.**

### The Confidence Check

At the end of the fact-find, ask: "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you
that your current plan — as it stands today — will get you both through a
30-year retirement without running out of money?" His answer will almost
certainly be 4-6. Follow up: "What would make it a 10?" His answer IS the sale.
