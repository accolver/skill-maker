# HVAC Go-To-Market Assessment

## Bottom line

Your best near-term path is to start with your brother's HVAC owner network, not
with HVAC distribution relationships.

The owner path is stronger because it gives you direct access to the buyer, the
pain, the budget conversation, and real operational workflows you can automate
quickly. Distribution experience is still valuable, but it is better used as
context and credibility rather than as the first channel.

## Why the owner network wins first

### 1. You have a warm intro path to decision-makers

Your brother can introduce you directly to local HVAC service owners. That
matters more than broad industry familiarity because early sales depend on
trust, fast feedback, and repeated conversations with actual buyers.

### 2. Owner pain is easier to translate into software value

HVAC owners live with obvious, expensive problems:

- missed calls and slow lead response
- dispatch inefficiency
- technician scheduling gaps
- quote follow-up leakage
- financing and membership follow-up
- review generation and referral capture
- after-hours answering and triage
- manual customer communication

These are close to revenue, labor utilization, and customer retention, so owners
can justify spending if the result is clear.

### 3. Distribution relationships are one step removed from the buyer

Your distribution background gives you useful domain knowledge, but distribution
contacts are less likely to be the end buyer for software solving
service-business workflow issues. They may help with insight, referrals, or
partnerships later, but they are usually not the fastest path to closed deals.

### 4. Early product discovery needs direct workflow access

You can learn much faster by talking to owners, office managers, dispatchers,
and techs than by talking to distributors about what contractors might want.
Direct exposure will help you avoid building generic automation that sounds good
but does not survive real jobsite and office constraints.

## Best positioning

Position yourself as someone who understands both HVAC operations and the
software layer.

A strong practical positioning statement:

> I spent 8 years in HVAC distribution ops, I understand how contractors
> actually run, and I build software and automations that reduce admin load,
> speed up response, and help HVAC owners capture more revenue without adding
> headcount.

This is stronger than presenting yourself as a generic software builder.

## Recommended initial offer

Start with a narrow, outcome-tied service instead of a broad software platform.

Best initial wedge:

- missed-call text-back and lead capture
- quote follow-up automation
- maintenance membership renewal reminders
- review request automation
- after-hours AI intake and next-day callback workflows
- dispatch/admin workflow automation between phone, CRM, and messaging

These are attractive because they are easy for owners to understand and close
enough to cash flow to justify a pilot.

## Suggested first market sequence

### Phase 1: Brother-intro owner interviews

Talk to 10 to 15 owners or operators.

Goals:

- identify the most painful repetitive workflow
- learn which software stack they already use
- understand where jobs, leads, and admin work leak
- confirm what they would pay to fix first

### Phase 2: Done-for-you automation pilots

Sell a simple implementation, not a product vision.

Examples:

- "I will reduce missed inbound leads"
- "I will automate unsold estimate follow-up"
- "I will set up after-hours intake and next-day scheduling handoff"

This lowers resistance and gives you real case studies.

### Phase 3: Standardize what repeats

Once 2 to 3 pilots show the same pattern, turn the common pieces into a
repeatable offer or lightweight product.

### Phase 4: Use distribution credibility selectively

After you have proof, use your distribution background to:

- strengthen trust with contractors
- get warm referrals from people who know your HVAC history
- explore distributor/channel partnerships later if a repeatable offer exists

## Risks of leading with distribution

- slower path to actual buyers
- more indirect feedback loops
- higher chance of abstract conversations instead of real buying intent
- potential channel confusion if distributors do not see immediate incentive
- risk of building for perceived industry problems instead of urgent owner
  problems

## What makes you unusually credible

You have a useful combination that many software sellers do not:

- industry context from 8 years in HVAC distribution ops
- family-level access to trusted owner introductions
- ability to personally build the software and automation layer

That means you can likely move faster than both generic agencies and
non-technical HVAC consultants.

## Clear recommendation

Start with the brother-to-owner route as the primary channel.

Use your HVAC distribution background as supporting credibility, discovery
context, and later referral leverage, but not as the main first go-to-market
path.

## Immediate next steps

1. Ask your brother for introductions to 5 owners he trusts.
2. Run short discovery calls focused on one question: "What repetitive office
   workflow costs you the most money or slows you down the most?"
3. Pick one painful workflow that appears repeatedly.
4. Sell one fixed-scope pilot tied to a measurable outcome.
5. Turn the result into a short case study before widening outreach.
