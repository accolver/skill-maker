## Hybrid Ladder

### 5. HVAC Revenue Recovery Concierge -> Automation Layer -> Micro-SaaS

- Classification: `hybrid`
- Thesis: start by manually running one narrow revenue-recovery workflow for
  small HVAC shops, automate the repeated steps, then spin the stable workflow
  into software once the process is proven across multiple accounts.
- Why this user can win: this best matches your actual edge: HVAC context, real
  buyer access, and ability to both deliver the service and later build the
  product.
- Why this could fail: if each shop needs a radically different setup, you may
  never reach a reusable product core.
- Scorecard: edge fit 9, speed to revenue 8, durability 9, distribution fit 8,
  AI leverage 8, defensibility 7, execution difficulty 5, compliance/platform
  risk 3.

Recommended ladder details:

- Concrete first offer: a 30-day `Estimate Follow-Up and Job Closeout Sprint`
  for 5-20 tech residential HVAC businesses.
- Narrow ICP: owner-led or GM-led residential HVAC service companies with 5-20

- Measurable pain / outcome metric: increase percent of estimates followed up
  within 24 hours and reduce days from completed job to invoice closeout.
- Productization trigger: after 3 paid pilots, at least 70 percent of the
  workflow is the same, the same data fields recur, and at least 2 customers ask
  to keep paying monthly for the software-enabled process rather than more
  custom work.

Rejected path:

- Pure content or audience-led HVAC AI business: rejected because you have no

# Best Bet

Best bet: `HVAC Revenue Recovery Concierge -> Automation Layer -> Micro-SaaS`

Why this wins:

- It is the highest-fit hybrid path: fastest credible path to revenue without
  abandoning the longer-term micro-SaaS goal.
- It exploits your actual edge better than a pure SaaS-first build: warm intros,
  pilot access, and domain understanding.
- It avoids the worst failure mode for technical founders in niche markets:
  building a tool before proving that owners will pay for one repeated painful
  workflow.

Pressure test:

- Demand clarity: owners already understand lost revenue from slow estimate
  follow-up and delayed closeout; this is easier to sell than vague "AI
  transformation."
- Distribution fit: your brother's network is strong enough to get discovery
  calls and pilot accounts; this matters more than a public audience.
- Execution difficulty: moderate, because you can start manually with
  lightweight automations before standardizing integrations.
- Compliance and platform risk: acceptable if you avoid regulated advice, get
  customer consent for messaging flows, and keep employer-confidential
  distributor information completely separate.
- Why service-first should come before software: the workflow boundaries, data
  quality issues, and buying triggers will be clearer after 2-3 live accounts.
- Why the obvious alternative loses: a micro-SaaS-first path feels cleaner
  technically, but it has slower learning, weaker proof of demand, and a higher
  chance of shipping the wrong feature set.
- What would change the recommendation: if your brother cannot open doors and
  you cannot get at least 5-10 warm owner conversations quickly, the path
  becomes more conditional and you should reconsider the niche or channel.

Suggested initial packaging:

- Offer:
  `We recover revenue stuck in estimate follow-up and admin lag for small HVAC shops in 30 days.`
- Pricing hypothesis: $1,500-$3,000 setup for the sprint, then $500-$1,500 per
  month once the workflow is running.
- First promise: one measurable improvement in either follow-up speed, booked
  jobs recovered, or invoice-closeout time.

# 7-Day Validation Plan

## Day 1

- Write a one-page offer for the `Estimate Follow-Up and Job Closeout Sprint`.
- Define the 3 metrics you will measure: estimate follow-up within 24 hours,
  jobs recovered from stale estimates, and average job-to-invoice closeout time.
- Build a simple ROI calculator using conservative assumptions.

Target: 1 clear offer page and 1 ROI calculator completed by end of day.

## Day 2

- Ask your brother for a list of 15 local owners or GMs who are plausible fits.
- Segment them by likely urgency and relationship strength.
- Prepare a short intro message that asks for a 20-minute workflow interview,
  not a software demo.

Target: 15 named prospects and at least 8 intro requests sent.

## Day 3

- Run 3-5 owner interviews.
- Ask for current process, where estimates go stale, who follows up, and what
  breaks between tech completion and invoicing.
- Capture baseline numbers if possible.

Target: at least 3 interviews completed; at least 2 owners confirm the problem
is painful and currently under-managed.

## Day 4

- Map your brother's current workflow end to end.
- Manually prototype the service using no-code plus AI tooling where possible.
- Identify what can be done by prompt, what needs rules, and what still needs a
  human.

Target: 1 working pilot workflow on your brother's business with a same-day
closeout or follow-up process in place.

## Day 5

- Turn the pilot into a before/after case narrative.
- Pitch 3 interviewees on a paid 30-day sprint.
- Offer a low-friction pilot with explicit metrics and weekly review.

Target: 3 pilot asks made and at least 1 serious paid-pilot conversation
advanced.

## Day 6

- Tighten the scope to the most repeated workflow steps.
- Draft the data fields and events that a later product would need.
- Price the recurring version of the service.

Target: 1 repeatable workflow spec and 1 draft monthly pricing model.

## Day 7

- Review signals against kill criteria.
- Decide whether to continue, narrow the offer, or switch the workflow focus.

Target: a yes / no decision based on actual conversations and pilot evidence,
not enthusiasm.

# Build Spec

Goal: a service-delivery system that can later become a narrow product, not a
big app.

Core workflow to support first:

- Intake stale estimates or completed jobs from the shop's existing system.
- Summarize technician notes and next action using AI.
- Generate follow-up tasks or customer-ready message drafts.
- Track whether follow-up happened within SLA.
- Show simple owner-facing outcomes: jobs recovered, touchpoints sent, closeout
  lag reduced.

Version 1 stack principles:

- Start with spreadsheet or lightweight database plus Zapier/Make/n8n-style
  automation and an LLM layer.
- Avoid deep integration work until the workflow is proven.
- Keep a human review step for customer-facing messages early on.

Minimal product boundary once validated:

- Customer record
- Job / estimate record
- Follow-up state machine
- AI summary and draft generation
- SLA dashboard for office staff
- Owner ROI summary

What not to build yet:

- Full CRM replacement
- Generic AI receptionist platform
- Multi-vertical workflow engine
- Heavy mobile app for technicians

# Kill Criteria

- If you cannot get at least 5 owner conversations from warm intros within 14
  days, stop assuming this channel is enough and revisit the niche or entry
  point.
- If fewer than 2 of the first 5 owners describe the problem as urgent enough to
  pay for in the next 30 days, pivot to a different HVAC workflow pain.
- If you cannot get 1 paid pilot within 21 days of starting outreach, do not
  build software yet.
- If the first 3 pilots all require materially different workflows with less
  than 70 percent overlap, reject the micro-SaaS thesis and keep it as a
  higher-ticket service only.
- If AI output needs heavy manual cleanup and does not save at least 5 admin
  hours per week or produce obvious revenue recovery, narrow the workflow before
  scaling.
