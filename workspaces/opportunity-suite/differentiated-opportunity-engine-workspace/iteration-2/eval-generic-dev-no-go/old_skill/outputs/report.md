## Differentiation Profile

- Skills and domain experience: junior full-stack developer with enough
  technical ability to ship simple web apps, automations, and integrations, but
  without deep domain expertise or a track record in a valuable niche.
- Assets and access advantages: no niche audience, no proprietary data, no
  special domain access, and no budget for paid acquisition.
- Distribution and network leverage: effectively none. Early customers would
  need to come from cold outreach, marketplaces, communities, or app
  directories.
- Constraints: about 4 hours per week, 30-day timeline, wants money fast, does
  not want sales calls, services, or content creation.
- Credibility signals: can build software personally, but likely lacks buyer
  trust in high-stakes niches.
- Unfair advantages: speed of solo implementation and low cost of
  experimentation. Relative to a random non-technical person, the edge is
  shipping. Relative to a random competent developer with ChatGPT, the edge is
  weak.

Assumptions used because follow-up was not practical:

- The user can build and deploy a basic SaaS, browser extension, or automation
  in evenings/weekends.
- The user is willing to do lightweight async distribution such as direct
  messages, listings, and forum posts, but not live selling.
- The goal is first revenue quickly, not necessarily a durable business in 30
  days.

## Best Bet

Build a tiny self-serve product for an existing buyer pool, using marketplace or
search-driven distribution, and reject the fantasy of a differentiated AI
micro-SaaS in 30 days.

Why this is the best bet:

- The user has no meaningful founder edge in a niche, so generic AI SaaS is a
  bad game.
- The user does not want calls, services, or content, which removes the fastest
  learning loops.
- With only 4 hours a week, the only plausible route is to piggyback on existing
  demand rather than create it.
- A directory, calculator, monitor, template engine, or focused utility can be
  sold asynchronously and later turned into software with better evidence.

Recommended path:

1. Pick a buyer group that already pays for small tools without needing
   trust-heavy sales. Good examples are Shopify merchants, Etsy sellers,
   recruiters, Amazon sellers, indie hackers, or local-business operators.
2. Find one repeated workflow visible in public communities, app reviews, Reddit
   threads, or marketplace complaints.
3. Build the smallest self-serve product that removes one painful step. Prefer
   import, summarize, compare, classify, alert, or generate workflows.
4. Sell it where the buyer already is: app marketplace, Chrome Web Store,
   Gumroad, Lemon Squeezy storefront, or a niche directory.

Pressure test:

- Demand clarity: moderate only if based on repeated public complaints; poor if
  invented from scratch.
- Distribution fit: still weak, but better on a marketplace than from a blank
  website.
- Execution difficulty: manageable if the first version is a one-job tool, not a
  platform.
- Compliance and platform risk: acceptable if the tool avoids spam, scraping
  violations, or regulated decisions.
- Service-first wedge: yes in principle, but the user's constraints rule out a
  classic service wedge. The closest acceptable substitute is an async
  concierge-like setup wizard or manual behind-the-scenes data preparation
  without calls.

Bottom line:

- Do not spend the month building a generic AI app and hoping people appear.
- Do not target broad markets like copywriting, resumes, or general
  productivity.
- Do build a painfully narrow utility attached to an existing buyer pool and
  accept that the first win may be small, like first $50 to $300, not instant
  SaaS escape velocity.

## 7-Day Validation Plan

1. Day 1: choose 3 buyer pools with built-in distribution and list 10 recurring
   pain points from reviews, Reddit, Discord, app marketplaces, and support
   threads.
2. Day 2: score each pain point for urgency, repetition, and ability to solve
   with a tiny async product. Kill anything that requires education, trust-heavy
   onboarding, or complex integrations.
3. Day 3: pick 1 problem and create a landing page with a concrete promise,
   example input/output, price, and waitlist or pre-order button.
4. Day 4: post the offer in places where buyers already look for solutions:
   relevant communities, comments on complaint threads, marketplace listing
   drafts, and direct async outreach to a small set of likely users.
5. Day 5: if at least 5 people click through and 2 people leave an email or
   attempt purchase, build the smallest working version. If not, revise the
   problem statement once and retest.
6. Day 6: ship a bare-bones version that performs one job end to end. Charge
   immediately, even if the UX is ugly.
7. Day 7: review usage and refund friction. If users do not complete the core
   action or nobody pays, stop and pick the next problem instead of polishing.

## Build Spec

- Product shape: one-page utility or lightweight SaaS that solves exactly one
  job.
- Ideal jobs: comparison tool, listing optimizer, review summarizer, policy
  checker, keyword clusterer, title generator with constraints, competitor
  monitor, or simple alerting dashboard.
- Stack: use the stack the user already knows. Avoid learning-heavy choices.
- Required features:
  - landing page with a sharp promise
  - simple auth or magic link if needed
  - one core input -> output workflow
  - payment link or checkout
  - basic analytics for visits, completions, and purchases
  - feedback capture after output delivery
- Nice-to-have features to skip initially:
  - teams
  - credits system
  - complex onboarding
  - polished dashboard
  - multi-model orchestration
  - elaborate prompt management UI
- Distribution spec:
  - launch where demand already exists
  - make the title and screenshots obviously specific
  - use the exact words buyers use in complaints and reviews
- Pricing:
  - start with one-time pricing or a very small monthly plan
  - prefer $9 to $29 impulse buys over enterprise framing

## Kill Criteria

- Kill the idea if the user cannot identify a buyer pool with built-in
  distribution within 3 days.
- Kill the idea if the pain point is not repeated by multiple real people in
  public.
- Kill the idea if a landing page with a concrete promise gets no meaningful
  interest after targeted posting.
- Kill the idea if the product requires calls, custom setup, or ongoing service
  to make the first sale.
- Kill the idea if the tool feels interchangeable with dozens of existing AI
  wrappers.
- Kill the month-long micro-SaaS goal entirely if no one tries to pay for a
  sharply defined utility in the first two validation cycles.

Practical recommendation in one sentence: stop aiming for a generic AI
micro-SaaS, and instead ship a tiny paid utility for an existing buyer pool
using marketplace or search-adjacent distribution, with a willingness to kill
weak ideas fast.
