# Differentiation Profile

- Skills and domain experience: Senior data analyst in ecommerce SaaS with
  strong exposure to merchant reporting pain, KPI ambiguity, dashboard adoption
  issues, and the gap between raw data and operational decisions.
- Assets and access advantages: Pattern recognition across many merchants
  without needing to guess the problem from scratch; likely fluency in ecommerce
  metrics, attribution caveats, and reporting stakeholder conflicts.
- Distribution and network leverage: Limited direct founder access and limited
  budget ownership; network is mostly peers, which is weak for direct software
  sales but usable for referrals, channel partnerships, and discovery
  interviews.
- Constraints: No owned audience, no obvious warm path to many founders, and
  likely some employer confidentiality boundaries around using customer-specific
  knowledge.
- Credibility signals: Senior title, domain fluency, and likely stronger
  diagnostic ability than a generic consultant or indie hacker entering
  ecommerce cold.
- Unfair advantages: You understand where merchant reporting actually breaks in
  practice, not just what dashboards claim to solve.

# Best Bet

Your honest best bet is a **service-first hybrid**, not a software-first
micro-SaaS.

The reason is simple: your problem knowledge is strong, but your distribution is
not. You know the pain, but you do not currently control budget and you do not
have a founder-heavy network. That makes a direct jump into micro-SaaS much less
credible than it feels.

The best near-term path is:

**Reporting triage service for a narrow ecommerce ICP, sold through
operators/agencies, with a clear trigger to productize the repeated QA
workflow.**

## Concrete first offer

- Offer: `Ecommerce Reporting Trust Sprint`
- What it does: Audit one merchant's reporting stack, identify 3-5 metric
  definition or instrumentation failures, and deliver a corrected KPI layer plus
  a weekly exception review template.
- Price target: $2,000-$5,000 fixed fee for the first 2-3 pilots.

## Narrow ICP

- Primary ICP: Small ecommerce agencies, fractional ecommerce operators, or
  analytics freelancers serving Shopify brands in the $1M-$20M revenue range.
- Why this ICP: They already have merchant trust and budget conversations; you
  can use them as a channel instead of needing direct founder access from day
  one.

## Measurable pain or outcome metric

- Core pain: Merchants do not trust reported MER, CAC, contribution margin, or
  channel performance because definitions and source joins drift.
- Success metric: Reduce reporting discrepancies or unresolved KPI exceptions by
  at least 50% within 14 days for the pilot account.

## Productization trigger

- Move toward software only after 4-6 paid engagements where at least 70% of the
  issue-detection workflow is the same.
- Stronger trigger: At least 3 customers ask for ongoing monitoring rather than
  a one-time cleanup.

## Pressure test

- Demand clarity: Strong. Reporting trust is a real pain, but you must narrow it
  to one repeated problem, not general “better analytics.”
- Distribution fit: Better through agencies and operators than through cold
  founder outreach.
- Execution difficulty: Moderate. You can deliver manually first with SQL,
  dbt-like logic, spreadsheets, or lightweight scripts.
- Compliance and platform risk: Low to moderate if you avoid using
  employer-confidential material and only use customer-authorized data.
- Should service come before software: Yes. In your case this is not optional
  theater; it is the fastest way to gain buyer access and validate which
  workflow is worth productizing.
- Why the obvious alternative loses: A software-first micro-SaaS loses because
  the market is crowded, founder access is weak, and you do not yet know which
  exact workflow buyers will pay to automate.
- What would change the recommendation: If you already had 10+ warm founder
  conversations or an existing distribution channel into brands, a narrow
  micro-SaaS wedge could move up materially.

# 7-Day Validation Plan

## Day 1

- Write a one-page offer for `Ecommerce Reporting Trust Sprint` with one
  outcome, one ICP, and one price range.
- Build a prospect list of 30 people: 15 peers who serve merchants and 15
  agency/fractional operator contacts or second-degree targets.

## Day 2

- Send 20 targeted messages asking for a 15-minute call, not a sale.
- Goal: book 6 conversations.

## Day 3

- Run 3 discovery calls.
- Ask for the last time a KPI was trusted late, wrong, or disputed; quantify
  cost, delay, or wasted decision-making.
- Goal: hear the same problem statement at least twice.

## Day 4

- Run 3 more calls.
- Test two offer framings: `metric QA sprint` vs `reporting trust sprint`.
- Goal: at least 2 people say they would introduce you to a merchant or client
  with this pain.

## Day 5

- Pitch 3 pilot offers.
- Goal: get 1 verbal yes or 2 strong follow-ups tied to a specific account.

## Day 6

- Build a lightweight delivery checklist: inputs needed, checks run, outputs
  delivered, exception log.
- Time-box the first pilot so you know whether the economics work in under 6-8
  hours of your own time.

## Day 7

- Review results against thresholds.
- Continue only if you hit at least 8 total conversations, 2 warm intros to real
  buyers, and 1 paid pilot or a highly specific pending pilot with timing.

# Build Spec

## Phase 1: Manual-first service stack

- Intake: simple questionnaire capturing stack, core KPIs, known discrepancies,
  and reporting consumers.
- Diagnostic engine: repeatable checklist for metric definitions,
  source-of-truth conflicts, attribution mismatches, and broken joins.
- Deliverables: issue log, corrected KPI definition sheet, exception tracker,
  and weekly review template.
- AI use: summarize discrepancies, draft issue explanations, and accelerate
  documentation; do not pretend AI is the product.

## Phase 2: Product wedge if trigger is met

- Product concept: lightweight metric QA and anomaly workflow for one ecommerce
  stack, not a full dashboard suite.
- First features:
  - KPI definition registry
  - discrepancy alerts
  - exception queue
  - stakeholder-facing explanation layer
- Non-goal: another generic BI dashboard.

# Kill Criteria

- Stop the current offer if you cannot get 8 relevant conversations in 7 days
  through your available network and light outbound.
- Stop the agency/channel wedge if fewer than 2 operator or agency contacts are
  willing to introduce you after hearing the offer.
- Do not build software if you have fewer than 3 paid pilots or if the workflows
  differ so much that less than 70% of the delivery checklist repeats.
- Kill the merchant-reporting angle if buyers consistently describe the pain as
  low-priority compared with acquisition, inventory, or finance operations.

# Rejected Paths

- Software-first ecommerce analytics micro-SaaS: rejected for now because your
  buyer access is too weak relative to the crowded market, so you would be
  building before learning.
- Broad analytics consulting: rejected because it is too vague, too custom, and
  does not naturally ladder into a defensible product.
