  with direct knowledge of denial management and prior authorization failure
  modes, workflow bottlenecks, and where provider groups lose money.
- Assets and access advantages: Strong domain fluency, likely ability to
  interpret payer policy changes, operational edge in structuring workflows, and
  ability to apply AI to messy administrative processes.

  no consulting is desired, and no sales calls are desired.

  calls, about 4 hours per week, must be fully legal, must avoid
  employer-confidential information and likely avoid any product that looks
  derived from internal docs or customer data.
- Credibility signals: Senior title in a hard operational niche, practical
  understanding of claims economics, and likely high trust with niche operators
  once positioned correctly.
- Unfair advantages: The real edge is problem selection and workflow judgment in
  denials and prior auth. The unusable edge is any employer data, internal
  process docs, customer-specific insight, or proprietary materials.

Feasibility verdict: `conditional`

Binding constraints:

- You do have a niche edge, but the highest-value obvious buyers are legally
  sensitive because they may overlap with employer relationships or require
  PHI-handling workflows.
- `4 hours/week + no consulting + no sales calls` makes fast cash unlikely.
- A fully legal path exists, but it needs to be productized, asynchronous, based
  on public or synthetic inputs, and sold outside your employer's customer set.

# Best Bet

Best bet:
`Denial intelligence library -> benchmark feed -> lightweight ops software`

This is the most credible path that respects your constraints.

Why it beats the obvious alternatives:

- It is more legal and cleaner than provider-facing workflow software because it
  can be built from public payer documentation, synthetic examples, and non-PHI
  operator education.
- It fits your no-consulting preference better than audits or teardowns.
- It has better long-term defensibility than a one-off template pack because the
  asset can compound into a structured taxonomy, change log, and benchmark
  dataset.
- It is more realistic than pure content because the product itself is the
  monetization layer.

Recommended initial offer:

- A paid subscription for independent RCM agencies, medical billing firms,
  outsourced prior-auth teams, and small health-tech vendors.
- Deliverables: weekly payer-change digest, denial reason taxonomy updates,
  specialty-specific documentation checklists, synthetic appeal/evidence packet
  examples, and a monthly benchmark memo.
- Positioning: operations intelligence and workflow preparation, not legal
  advice, not reimbursement guarantees, not medical necessity judgment.

Narrow ICP:

- Independent RCM agencies and billing firms with 5-50 employees serving
  outpatient specialties.

One measurable pain/outcome metric:

- Reduce time spent interpreting payer changes and updating denial/pre-auth SOPs
  from multiple hours per week to under 30 minutes per account manager or team
  lead.

Productization trigger:

- If 3 or more paying customers repeatedly ask for the same searchable fields,
  change alerts, or benchmarking cuts, build the lightweight software layer.

What would change the recommendation:

- If you later become open to async expert services, a paid audit plus
  implementation offer could accelerate learning and revenue.
- If an employment review shows broad moonlighting or IP assignment
  restrictions, the niche-specific version should stop and move to a different
  healthcare-adjacent workflow.

# 7-Day Validation Plan

## Day 1: Legal boundary check

- Review employment agreement, invention assignment, moonlighting policy,
  confidentiality terms, and any non-solicit language.
- Output: one 1-page written boundary memo for yourself.
- Numeric target: identify `0` uses of employer data, `0` employer documents,
  `0` employer customers, and `0` workflows that require PHI.
- Stop condition: if you cannot clearly separate the product from employer IP or
  customer restrictions, pause the niche.

## Day 2: Define one offer and one ICP

- Pick one specialty cluster and one buyer: for example, independent RCM firms
  focused on GI, cardiology, or orthopedics.
- Draft a one-page landing page with pricing.
- Numeric target: one clear paid offer, one ICP, one outcome metric, one
  founding price.
- Suggested founding price: `$199/month` or `$1,500/year` for the first five
  buyers.

## Day 3: Build three proof artifacts

- Create three sample artifacts using only public sources and synthetic
  examples:
- A payer-change digest.
- A denial reason taxonomy map.
- A prior-auth documentation checklist for one specialty.
- Numeric target: `3` sample artifacts, `100%` sourced from public material,
  with source links.

## Day 4: Create the async sales surface

- Publish the landing page, a Stripe or preorder link, and a sample preview PDF
  or Notion page.
- Numeric target: `1` landing page, `1` payment or deposit path, `1` sample
  pack.

## Day 5: Run narrow outreach without calls

- Send short async emails or LinkedIn messages to non-employer buyers only:
  small RCM agencies, billing owners, and relevant health-tech operators.
- Post in `3-5` relevant communities or channels if allowed.
- Numeric target: `30` targeted async outreaches, `3` posts, `10` responses, `5`
  qualified positives.

## Day 6: Ask for money, not compliments

- Offer a founding subscription with a limited charter: weekly updates plus
  monthly benchmark memo.
- Numeric target: `2` paid preorders or `5` refundable deposits of at least
  `$100` each.

## Day 7: Decide

- Review whether demand and legal separation are both real.
- Continue only if you hit at least `2` paid preorders or `5` deposits and had
  no legal red flags.

# Build Spec

## V0 product

- Core product: a searchable denial and prior-auth operations intelligence
  library.
- Source material: public payer bulletins, prior auth lists, policy updates, fee
  schedule notes, CMS/public guidance, and your own clean-room synthesis.
- Delivery format: simple member site or Notion-style portal plus email digest.
- AI role: summarize rule changes, classify them into a stable taxonomy, draft
  checklist updates, and generate synthetic examples for review.
- Human role: final editorial review for every published item.

## Minimal features

- Payer-change tracker.
- Denial reason taxonomy.
- Specialty-specific documentation checklists.
- Synthetic appeal/evidence packet examples.
- Monthly benchmark memo built from aggregated customer-reported or
  survey-reported metrics only if collected with permission and no PHI.

## What not to build initially

- No EHR integration.
- No direct claim ingestion.
- No PHI upload.
- No automated clinical or legal decisioning.
- No provider-specific reimbursement promises.

## Suggested stack

- Lightweight site + payments + email + a simple internal content pipeline.
- Keep the first version manual and editorial. Do not build software before
  buyers repeatedly ask for searchable history, alerts, and filters.

# Kill Criteria

- Legal kill: your employment terms or counsel review suggests the niche product
  is likely covered by IP assignment, non-solicit, or confidentiality
  restrictions.
- Demand kill: after `30` targeted async outreaches and `3` public posts, you
  get fewer than `5` qualified positive responses.
- Monetization kill: within `14` days, you get `0` paid preorders and fewer than
  `5` deposits.
- Source-quality kill: you cannot create clearly valuable artifacts from only
  public sources and synthetic examples.
- Time-budget kill: maintaining the product requires more than `4` hours per
  week before reaching `3` paying customers.

Bottom line: do not start with provider-facing software or consulting. Start
with a clean-room, public-source, asynchronously sold denial intelligence
subscription for non-employer buyers, and only add software if people pay for
the information layer first.
