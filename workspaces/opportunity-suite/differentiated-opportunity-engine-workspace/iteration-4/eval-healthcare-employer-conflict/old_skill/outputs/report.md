  workflow fluency, denial and prior authorization operations knowledge, AI
  automation ability, strong understanding of where margin leaks happen in
  provider revenue cycle.
- Assets and access advantages: Deep problem selection advantage in a narrow,

  access, so outbound enterprise selling is a poor fit. Better channels are
  self-serve distribution, written content, marketplaces, and adjacent buyers

  employer relationships.
- Constraints: No PHI, no use of employer confidential information, no contact
  with employer customers, no consulting or sales calls, roughly 4 hours per
  week.
- Credibility signals: Staff engineer title, direct exposure to denial and prior
  auth loss points, credible builder-operator profile in a painful niche.
- Unfair advantages: Better judgment than a generic AI founder about which
  denial and prior auth problems are worth solving, what language buyers use,
  and what workflows can be standardized without regulated data.

## Best Bet

Build a narrow
`payer policy diff monitor -> action playbook -> self-serve copilot` for one
specialty with high prior-auth and denial pain.

Why this is the best near-term path:

- It uses your real edge: problem selection and workflow judgment, not access to
  PHI.
- It stays on the safe side of employer conflict if you use only public payer
  documents, synthetic examples, and non-customer distribution.
- It works with 4 hours a week because the first version is curated intelligence
  plus lightweight automation, not a full workflow platform.
- It does not require consulting calls. The product can be sold as a
  subscription with sample outputs, landing pages, and email capture.
- It creates an asset: every policy summary, action playbook, and mapped denial
  scenario becomes reusable product inventory.

Pressure test:

- Demand clarity: Strong. Denials and prior auth create immediate financial
  pain, but the wedge must be specific. Pick one specialty and one payer set.
- Distribution fit: Moderate. You lack warm buyer access, so broad enterprise
  selling is weak. Inbound channels, niche SEO, LinkedIn posts, and partnerships
  with RCM consultants are better.
- Execution difficulty: Reasonable. The first version is mostly ingestion,
  summarization, and templated output.
- Compliance risk: Manageable if you avoid PHI, avoid employer data, do not copy
  proprietary internal materials, and clearly state the product is operational
  guidance, not legal advice.
- Service-first question: A heavy service-first wedge is not necessary here. A
  light concierge layer is enough: manually curate the first few updates and
  playbooks before automating.

Recommended wedge:

- Buyer: Independent provider groups outside employer relationships, small MSOs,
  billing services, or RCM consultants.
- Initial use case: "Tell me what changed in payer prior-auth and medical
  necessity rules this week, what it likely breaks, and what my team should
  change on Monday."
- Pricing direction: Start with a low-friction subscription or paid pilot, not
  custom enterprise deals.

## 7-Day Validation Plan

1. Pick one specialty and one payer cluster. Good examples are areas with
   repetitive prior-auth pain, such as imaging, behavioral health, or specialty
   infusion.
2. Create 3 sample outputs from public sources only: one policy diff summary,
   one denial root-cause playbook, and one appeal/checklist template. Use
   synthetic examples where needed.
3. Build a one-page landing page that promises one concrete outcome: weekly
   payer rule changes translated into operational actions. Include screenshots
   of the sample outputs.
4. Add a self-serve waitlist or preorder button. Do not book calls. The CTA
   should be `join waitlist`, `start pilot`, or `subscribe for launch pricing`.
5. Distribute through written channels only: 5-10 LinkedIn posts, 2 short niche
   articles, and outreach to a small set of non-customer RCM consultants via
   email with a sample artifact, not a meeting request.
6. Manually produce one week of updates for the chosen niche and measure whether
   people open, click, reply, or preorder.
7. Decide based on signals. If there is traction, automate document ingestion
   and publish cadence. If not, narrow the niche or shift to the white-label
   consultant channel.

## Build Spec

MVP scope:

- Input sources: Public payer bulletins, prior-auth requirement pages, medical
  policy updates, CMS/public guidance, and specialty society guidance where
  relevant.
- Processing: Fetch documents, detect changes, summarize deltas, classify likely
  operational impact, generate checklists and appeal-framework suggestions.
- Output artifacts: Weekly digest, searchable playbook library,
  specialty-specific issue pages, and a simple copilot chat over the curated
  corpus.
- Data policy: No PHI, no customer data, no employer data, no confidential
  workflow artifacts.

Suggested product shape:

- `Monitor`: Track public policy changes for one specialty and payer set.
- `Explain`: Show what changed in plain operational language.
- `Act`: Generate action lists, front-desk/billing checklists, and appeal prep
  templates.
- `Search`: Let users ask natural-language questions over the curated corpus.

Lean stack:

- Scraper/ingestion for public policy pages.
- LLM summarization plus a deterministic diff layer.
- Small reviewed knowledge base with source links.
- Static site or simple SaaS shell with Stripe and email delivery.

What not to build yet:

- Full denial workflow software.
- EMR/EHR integrations.
- Anything requiring PHI, BAAs, or direct operational access.
- Broad multi-specialty support before one niche shows pull.

## Kill Criteria

- Stop if you cannot identify a niche where public policy changes happen often
  enough to create recurring value.
- Stop if 2 weeks of distribution produce no meaningful signal, defined as no
  waitlist signups, no replies from adjacent buyers, and no willingness to pay.
- Stop if your employer policy clearly bars adjacent moonlighting in this
  category.
- Stop if the product requires customer-specific data or PHI to be useful in the
  first version.
- Stop if buyers only want custom analysis and reject the self-serve
  subscription format.

If this path fails, the best pivot is `white-label tooling for RCM consultants`,
because it keeps you out of direct provider selling while using the same
knowledge assets.
