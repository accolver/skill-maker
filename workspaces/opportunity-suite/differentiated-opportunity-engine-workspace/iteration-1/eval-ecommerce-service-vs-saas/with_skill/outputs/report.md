## Best Bet

Honest recommendation: sell a service first, but do it in a way that is
explicitly designed to become software.

You want a micro-SaaS, and that instinct is directionally good. But your current
situation does not favor software-first:

- You know the pain deeply, which is a real edge.
- You do not directly own buyer budget, which weakens your ability to get
  immediate software commitments.
- Your network is mostly peers, which is much better for discovery, warm
  introductions, and lightweight service pilots than for founder-led SaaS
  selling.

That combination usually means a service-first wedge is not a compromise. It is
the shortest path to a better micro-SaaS.

The right version is not generic consulting. It is a productized reporting
concierge for ecommerce merchants or small agencies serving them.

Recommended offer:

- Fixed deliverable: weekly and monthly KPI pack, channel performance breakdown,
  anomaly explanations, and 1-2 decision memos.
- Narrow ICP: Shopify brands doing roughly $1M-$20M GMV, or agencies managing
  several brands that need cleaner reporting.
- Pricing hypothesis: a one-time audit/cleanup offer followed by a monthly
  reporting retainer.

Why this beats software-first:

- Demand clarity: merchants already pay for solved reporting pain when trust is
  broken or leadership lacks visibility.
- Distribution fit: peers can introduce you to operators, analysts, rev ops
  people, or agency contacts faster than they can close SaaS deals for you.
- Execution difficulty: manual delivery plus lightweight automation is much
  easier than building integrations, onboarding, permissions, and dashboards
  before you know the exact wedge.
- Compliance and platform risk: low, assuming you handle merchant data with
  normal care and use standard contracts/processes.
- Service-first wedge before software: yes, strongly recommended. The service
  will show which 20 percent of the reporting workflow is painful enough,
  frequent enough, and standardized enough to productize.

What to avoid:

- Building a broad merchant dashboard product before you have 3-5 paying service
  customers.
- Selling to "all ecommerce merchants" instead of a narrow segment.
- Positioning around dashboards alone. The buying trigger is usually mistrust,
  slow answers, missed anomalies, or unclear decisions.

## 7-Day Validation Plan

Day 1:

- Write a one-page offer for a reporting audit plus monthly concierge.
- Pick one narrow ICP and one core promise, such as "I help Shopify brands trust
  their weekly numbers and catch what changed before it hurts margin."

Day 2:

- List 25 possible contacts from peers, former coworkers, agencies, consultants,
  and operators.
- Ask for 10 short interviews, not sales calls.

Day 3:

- Run 3-5 interviews focused on recent reporting frustration, current workflow,
  tools, manual workarounds, and what delays decisions.
- Test for willingness to pay for faster answers, not for "a dashboard" in the
  abstract.

Day 4:

- Turn the strongest pain pattern into a productized pilot.
- Define scope, timeline, inputs needed, and a fixed pilot price.

Day 5:

- Send the pilot offer to the warmest 5 prospects.
- Ask for either a paid pilot or a live walkthrough of their current reporting
  mess.

Day 6:

- Build only the internal tooling needed to deliver faster: ingestion helpers,
  anomaly summaries, QA checks, or reusable report templates.
- Document each repeated step and each custom step.

Day 7:

- Review signals: interviews completed, concrete pain repetition, paid interest,
  objections, and workflow repeatability.
- Decide whether to continue the service wedge, narrow the ICP, or kill the
  idea.

## Build Spec

Initial build target: internal operator tooling, not customer-facing SaaS.

Phase 1 internal tool:

- Input connectors: CSV uploads first, then Shopify/export connectors only if
  repeated demand justifies them.
- Standard metric layer: revenue, orders, AOV, repeat rate, refund rate,
  contribution/margin proxies if available.
- Reporting templates: weekly KPI summary, anomaly notes, channel/segment
  drilldown, and management-ready commentary.
- AI assist layer: generate first-draft anomaly explanations, summarize weekly
  changes, and draft stakeholder notes.
- QA layer: flag broken mappings, missing dates, suspicious jumps, and source
  mismatches.

Phase 2 productization trigger:

- Once the same workflow is used across at least 3 customers with minimal
  customization, package the narrowest repeated job.
- Best likely software wedge: anomaly explanation and metric-trust workflow, not
  a general dashboard.

Success condition for software build:

- You can point to a repeated painful task customers want continuously, not just
  a one-time reporting cleanup.

## Kill Criteria

- Fewer than 5 meaningful discovery conversations after a reasonable warm
  outreach push.
- Reporting pain is real, but nobody will pay for outside help because existing
  staff or agencies already cover it.
- Every prospect wants a heavily custom analytics project rather than a
  repeatable service.
- You cannot identify one repeated workflow that appears across at least 3
  prospects.
- The buying motion depends on founder access you do not have and cannot reach
  through warm paths.
- After 2 weeks of outreach and interviews, the strongest signal is curiosity
  about dashboards but no willingness to pay for a pilot.
