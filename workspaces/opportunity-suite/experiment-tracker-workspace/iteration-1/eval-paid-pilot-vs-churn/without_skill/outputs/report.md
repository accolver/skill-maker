## Experiment Summary

- 12 HVAC owner conversations completed.
- 4 of 12 explicitly said missed calls are painful.
- 2 of 12 agreed to a paid pilot.
- 1 of those 2 actually paid.
- 1 churned after setup because the workflow was too custom.

## State Assessment

The signal is promising but still narrow. Missed calls appear to be a real pain
point for a meaningful subset of HVAC owners, and there is early willingness to
pay. At the same time, the post-sale experience is fragile: churn happened not
because the problem was unimportant, but because the solution required too much
custom workflow work.

Current read:

- Problem signal exists: 33% mentioned missed calls as painful.
- Buying signal exists: 17% agreed to a paid pilot.
- Revenue signal exists: at least one customer paid.
- Delivery risk is the main blocker: customization complexity can erase early
  traction.

## Interpretation

This does not yet look like a demand failure. It looks more like a packaging and
implementation failure. The likely issue is that the offer is too bespoke, which
raises setup effort, slows time-to-value, and creates churn risk even after
someone says yes.

## Recommended Next Experiment

Run a tighter paid pilot experiment with a standardized offer.

Proposed experiment:

- Target only HVAC owners who clearly report missed-call pain.
- Sell a fixed-scope pilot with a narrow promise, such as missed-call text-back
  or missed-call capture only.
- Refuse custom workflows for this round.
- Use one setup path, one onboarding flow, and one success metric.

Suggested hypothesis:

"HVAC owners with clear missed-call pain will pay for a simple, standardized
pilot if setup is fast and does not require custom workflow design."

Success criteria:

- At least 3 paid pilots from the next qualified conversations.
- Most pilots go live with the same setup template.
- Lower churn during onboarding and first-use period.

What to learn:

- Whether demand survives after removing custom work.
- Which single use case is easiest to deliver repeatedly.
- Whether churn is caused by weak demand or by implementation friction.
