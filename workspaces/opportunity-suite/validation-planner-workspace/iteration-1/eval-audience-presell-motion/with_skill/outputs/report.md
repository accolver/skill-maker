# Validation Thesis

The best first test is an audience pre-sell for a narrowly defined paid product:
a niche newsletter template bundle paired with a benchmark pack that shows
proven examples, metrics, and positioning patterns from the same niche. The
biggest unknown is not trust or access because the user already has both; it is
willingness to pay for a productized asset instead of continuing to consume free
content.

# Key Unknowns

- Primary unknown: willingness-to-pay risk. Will this trusted audience pay now
  for a specific template + benchmark product without requiring a service layer?
- Secondary unknown: offer-shape risk. Is the higher-value wedge the template
  itself, the benchmark data, or the combination?
- Secondary unknown: price-point risk. Can the product clear a meaningful price
  point for this audience, or does demand only exist at a low-ticket impulse
  price?

Why this is the biggest unknown: distribution and trust are already present, so
the next decision-grade question is whether the audience will exchange money for
a defined product. If the answer is weak, building more assets or adding service
complexity is the wrong move.

# 7-Day Plan

## Day 1

- Pick one narrow audience job-to-be-done that already performs well in
  newsletter replies, clicks, or questions.
- Define one paid offer only: `Template Pack + Benchmark Tear-Down`.
- Draft a one-page checkout or pre-order page with:
  - specific outcome
  - who it is for
  - what is included
  - delivery date within 7 to 14 days
  - founding price
- Recommended starting price: $49 to $99 if the audience is
  prosumer/creator-heavy, or $149 to $299 if the audience is operator/B2B-heavy.

## Day 2

- Send a segmented newsletter pitch to the most relevant audience slice.
- Ask for the strongest feasible commitment signal: completed checkout or
  pre-order payment.
- Include one explicit objection-handling line: this is a product, not a
  service, and buyers get reusable assets plus benchmarks.

## Day 3

- Follow up with high-intent responders and non-buyers who clicked but did not
  purchase.
- Ask one tight question only: what prevented purchase right now?
- Track objections in three buckets: price, scope, and credibility of outcome.

## Day 4

- Run a second distribution pass using a sharper angle based on Day 2 to Day 3
  feedback.
- Change only one variable:
  - headline, or
  - price, or
  - primary promise

## Day 5

- Offer 5 to 10 buyers a short onboarding form to capture the exact benchmark
  dimensions they care about most.
- Use this to confirm that the product can be delivered repeatably without
  slipping into custom service work.

## Day 6

- Review conversion by segment:
  - total recipients
  - clicks
  - checkout starts
  - completed purchases
  - refund requests or hesitation signals
- Decide whether the demand is for:
  - templates only
  - benchmarks only
  - the combined pack

## Day 7

- Make a go/revise/kill decision.
- If `go`, commit to delivering version 1 and lining up a second paid cohort or
  evergreen checkout.
- If `revise`, narrow the niche or change the product framing.
- If `kill`, stop before building a broader product library.

# Success Criteria

- `Go`: at least 2 completed purchases in 7 days from the existing audience.
- `Go`: at least 1 buyer explicitly states the benchmarks/examples were a major
  reason to purchase.
- `Go`: at least 20 percent click-to-interest rate from the segmented send,
  where "interest" is checkout start, reply asking buying questions, or
  completed purchase.
- `Revise`: strong engagement but only 1 purchase, or multiple high-intent
  replies concentrated around a different scope or price point.
- `Revise`: buyers want one component clearly more than the bundled offer,
  suggesting a narrower product wedge.

Why these numbers: the audience is already warm and trust already exists, so the
bar should be stronger than opens or signups. The validation-metrics guide
suggests 2 checkout attempts or 1 to 3 paid pilots as decision-grade signals;
here, 2 real purchases in 7 days is the closest equivalent.

# Kill Criteria

- `Kill`: zero completed purchases after 2 segmented sends to the relevant
  audience.
- `Kill`: fewer than 5 qualified buyer conversations or replies after the sends,
  indicating weak urgency.
- `Kill`: all serious prospects ask for custom review, consulting, or
  done-for-you help instead of wanting the product itself.
- `Kill`: the only way to close buyers is to promise bespoke service, which
  breaks the product wedge.

# Why Not The Lower-Signal Alternative

The lower-signal version would be a poll, waitlist, or "would you buy this?"
survey. That is weaker than necessary because the user already has audience
trust and direct access. In this case, asking for money is feasible, so soft
engagement would create false positives and delay the real decision.
