# Validation Thesis

The best near-term test is not whether a full enterprise procurement analytics
product can be built. It is whether Fortune 500 procurement leaders will spend
budget or reserve a paid slot for a narrowly scoped diagnostic that surfaces
savings leakage, supplier concentration risk, or contract non-compliance from
data they already have.

The single biggest unknown is **buyer-access plus willingness-to-pay for a
no-product wedge**. With only one weak second-degree intro and no product, the
plan must prove that the founder can reach the right buyer and convert at least
one serious conversation into a paid diagnostic or a clearly scheduled
commercial next step.

# Key Unknowns

- **Primary unknown:** Can the founder get a Fortune 500 procurement or
  procurement-operations buyer to commit to a paid diagnostic without a product?
- Demand risk: Is the pain urgent enough to create internal momentum now?
- Buyer-access risk: Can the founder get enough qualified meetings from one weak
  intro plus targeted outbound?
- Willingness-to-pay risk: Will a buyer pay for a diagnostic instead of asking
  for free consulting or a generic deck?
- Workflow-repeatability risk: Do multiple prospects describe a similar enough
  problem to support one wedge?

# 7-Day Plan

## Offer To Test

Offer a fixed-scope service-first wedge:

`Procurement Leakage Diagnostic: in 10 business days we analyze supplier spend, maverick buying, duplicate vendors, and contract leakage, then deliver a quantified savings hypothesis and a pilot recommendation.`

Commercial ask:

- Price: `$3,000` paid diagnostic
- Alternative if procurement process blocks payment this week: signed statement
  of work or scheduled procurement review for the same diagnostic within 30 days
- Credit: 100% of the diagnostic fee credited toward any later pilot

## Day 1

- Define ICP as `Fortune 500` companies with large indirect spend and likely
  fragmented procurement workflows.
- Build a list of `25 target accounts`.
- Identify `40 named buyers`: VP Procurement, Head of Strategic Sourcing,
  Procurement Operations leader, or Finance Transformation leader.
- Write two outreach variants and one intro-forward message.
- Prepare a one-page offer with scope, sample deliverable, price, and clear CTA
  for a `20-minute fit call`.

Numeric targets:

- `25` target accounts
- `40` named buyers
- `1` one-page offer

## Day 2

- Ask for the `1` weak second-degree intro with a concrete forwardable note.
- Send `20` highly personalized outbound emails.
- Send `10` LinkedIn messages to the same or adjacent buyers.
- Ask `5` relevant operators, advisors, or former colleagues for referral paths
  into procurement leaders.

Numeric targets:

- `1` intro request sent
- `30` direct buyer messages sent
- `5` referral asks sent

## Day 3

- Send a second batch of `15` personalized messages to new targets.
- Follow up on all non-responders from Day 2.
- Hold any calls booked and probe for current reporting gaps, unused savings
  opportunities, and budget ownership.
- End each call with a concrete ask: paid diagnostic, sponsor intro, or
  data-sharing next step.

Numeric targets:

- `15` new outbound messages
- `5` live conversations booked by end of Day 3 or Day 4 combined
- `100%` of calls end with a direct commercial ask

## Day 4

- Run `2-3` buyer calls.
- Test the same wedge with each buyer, not a custom pitch.
- Capture exact language around pain, existing tools, and why current reporting
  is insufficient.
- Ask one buyer to review the diagnostic scope and confirm whether `$3,000` is
  low enough to buy quickly.

Numeric targets:

- `2-3` qualified calls completed
- `2` buyers confirm a painful and costly analytics gap
- `1` buyer gives pricing feedback tied to a real buying process

## Day 5

- Run remaining calls to reach `5-6` qualified conversations total.
- Send a written proposal to every prospect that showed urgency.
- Ask the strongest prospect for one of two commitments: `paid diagnostic` or
  `scheduled procurement/legal review for a paid diagnostic`.

Numeric targets:

- `5-6` total qualified buyer conversations
- `2` written proposals sent
- `1` explicit commercial next step requested from top prospect

## Day 6

- Follow up on open proposals.
- Tighten the wedge if feedback clusters around one use case, such as contract
  leakage or tail-spend visibility.
- Disqualify prospects that only want broad transformation consulting or custom
  dashboard work.

Numeric targets:

- `2` proposal follow-ups sent
- `1` narrowed wedge selected if pattern appears
- `0` time spent on fully custom unpaid discovery

## Day 7

- Score results against go, revise, and kill thresholds.
- Decide whether to continue with the current ICP and offer, narrow to a smaller
  segment, or stop.
- Preserve proof: call notes, proposal responses, objections, and any
  buying-process evidence.

Numeric targets:

- `1` final decision memo
- `1` clear next-step choice: go, revise, or kill

# Success Criteria

## Go

- `5+` qualified conversations with real buyers or budget influencers in `7`
  days
- `2+` buyers explicitly confirm the same high-cost procurement analytics
  problem
- `1+` buyer does one strong commitment action:
  - agrees to a `$3,000` paid diagnostic, or
  - schedules a defined internal buying step for that paid diagnostic within
    `30` days with a named sponsor
- At least `1` prospect shares enough context or sample data fields to scope the
  work concretely

## Revise

- `3-4` qualified conversations completed
- Pain is real, but it fragments across use cases or buyer types
- No one accepts the current offer, but at least `1-2` buyers ask for a narrower
  wedge, lower-friction starting point, or different buyer entry point

Revision action:

Narrow to one use case and one buyer, for example
`contract leakage detection for indirect spend` sold to
`procurement operations directors`.

# Kill Criteria

- Fewer than `3` qualified buyer conversations after `45+` targeted outbound
  touches plus the warm intro attempt
- `0` buyers agree to a paid diagnostic, paid pilot slot, or defined internal
  commercial step
- Most interested prospects want free consulting, bespoke analytics projects, or
  long RFP-driven evaluations that break the wedge
- No repeated urgent problem appears across the first `5-6` conversations

# Why Not The Lower-Signal Alternative

Buyer interviews, deck feedback, and landing-page clicks would produce false
positives here. Enterprise procurement analytics has a long sales cycle,
expensive stakeholder time, and many people who will happily discuss the problem
without buying. Because there is no product yet, the strongest realistic signal
is a paid diagnostic or a concrete internal buying step toward one. That tests
both access and willingness to pay without pretending the product already
exists.
