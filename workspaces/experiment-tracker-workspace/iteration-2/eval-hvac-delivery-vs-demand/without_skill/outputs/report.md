# HVAC Experiment Summary

## Current State

The signal is promising but not yet strong enough to claim repeatable demand.

- 12 HVAC owner conversations were completed.
- 4 owners explicitly said missed calls are painful.
- 2 owners agreed to a paid pilot.
- 1 owner actually paid.
- 1 customer churned after setup because the workflow was too custom.

## What This Suggests

- There is a real problem: missed calls matter to at least a meaningful subset
  of owners.
- Interest can convert into revenue, since one customer paid.
- The bigger risk right now appears to be delivery, not just demand.
- The churn reason is especially important: the solution may be too bespoke to
  deploy reliably.

## Interpretation

This looks like early demand with weak repeatability. The core pain seems
credible, but the current offer may depend too much on custom setup. That makes
onboarding slower, increases implementation risk, and can erase value even after
a sale.

At this stage, the main question is not "do HVAC owners care at all?" It is "can
we deliver a standard version of the workflow that solves the missed-call
problem with minimal customization?"

## Recommended Next Experiment

Run a delivery-focused experiment around a standardized pilot.

### Experiment Goal

Test whether HVAC owners will successfully activate and keep using a narrow,
mostly fixed missed-call workflow.

### Proposed Experiment

Offer the next 3 to 5 HVAC prospects the same tightly scoped pilot:

- one target use case: missed inbound calls only
- one standard workflow: missed call triggers immediate text-back and lead
  capture
- one onboarding path with minimal or no custom logic
- one simple success metric: percentage of missed calls receiving an automated
  follow-up

### Success Criteria

- At least 2 customers complete onboarding without custom workflow changes.
- At least 1 additional customer pays for the standardized pilot.
- Customers continue using it for 2 or more weeks without churn caused by setup
  complexity.

### Why This Experiment

This directly tests the current bottleneck. If standardized delivery works, the
business can validate both demand and operational feasibility. If prospects only
convert when the workflow is heavily customized, that is evidence the offer is
not yet productized enough to scale.
