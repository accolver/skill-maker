## HVAC Go-To-Market Assessment

### Bottom line

Your strongest wedge is **selling software/automation through your brother's
owner network**, not trying to monetize your past distribution-side network
first.

The reason is simple: your brother gives you warm access to the actual buyer,
while your distribution experience gives you domain fluency and credibility once
you are in the room. That combination is more valuable than a cold start with
distributor-side contacts who may understand the workflow problems but often do
not control the budget for field-service software purchases.

### Why this path is stronger

1. **You can reach owners faster**

Your brother can introduce you directly to local HVAC service owners. That is
immediate distribution. With no audience, warm introductions matter more than
brand.

2. **Owners feel the pain most directly**

Local HVAC owners live inside dispatch problems, missed follow-up, quote delays,
maintenance agreement leakage, technician communication gaps, and after-hours
admin work. They can buy quickly if the value is obvious.

3. **Your background still compounds the advantage**

Eight years in HVAC distribution ops means you likely understand parts flow,
branch operations, order friction, seasonality, contractor behavior, counter
relationships, and how messy real-world HVAC processes are. That makes you
better at building useful tools for service companies than a generic SaaS
founder.

4. **Smaller sales motion**

Selling to a local owner can often happen through direct conversation, a live
prototype, and a pilot. Selling into distribution networks usually requires more
stakeholders, longer cycles, and a less obvious path from pain to budget.

### Best initial customer profile

Start with HVAC service businesses that have:

- 5 to 30 techs
- an owner still involved in operations
- enough job volume to feel admin pain
- no internal software team
- visible process breakdowns across dispatch, quoting, follow-up, or maintenance
  agreements

This segment is large enough to pay, small enough to decide quickly, and messy
enough to benefit from custom automation.

### Best problem areas to explore first

Focus on painful workflows that are frequent, expensive, and boring.

Strong candidates:

1. **Quote follow-up automation** Missed estimates and weak follow-up lose
   revenue. Owners understand this immediately.

2. **Maintenance agreement retention and reactivation** Many shops leak
   recurring revenue because renewals, reminders, and outreach are inconsistent.

3. **Dispatch/admin workflow automation** Reducing manual texting, call tagging,
   scheduling updates, and technician-to-office handoff creates obvious labor
   savings.

4. **Parts and job-status visibility** Your distribution background may help
   here if shops struggle to track ordered parts, delayed installs, and customer
   communication around status.

5. **After-hours lead capture and triage** Missed calls and weak intake are
   common and easy to quantify.

### Recommended strategy

Use a **service-business-first** approach:

1. Have your brother introduce you to 10 to 15 owners.
2. Run short discovery conversations focused on operational pain, not product
   pitching.
3. Look for repeated problems tied to revenue leakage or office labor.
4. Build one narrow solution for one repeated pain.
5. Pilot with 2 to 3 shops.
6. Turn the pilot into either:
   - a productized service
   - a lightweight SaaS
   - a hybrid done-for-you automation offering

### Positioning that fits your background

You do not need to present yourself as a generic AI founder.

Better positioning:

> I spent years in HVAC distribution ops, understand how contractors actually
> run, and now build practical software and automations that remove admin drag
> and recover lost revenue for HVAC service companies.

That is credible, specific, and grounded in the trade.

### Why not start with distribution contacts first

Your distribution background is still valuable, but it is likely a **supporting
asset**, not the primary channel.

Risks of starting there:

- contacts may be peers, not buyers
- budget ownership may be unclear
- the value proposition may be more indirect
- sales cycles may be slower
- you may get trapped in broad workflow discussions without a fast paid pilot

Distribution may become useful later for:

- referrals into contractor accounts
- reseller/channel partnerships
- data or workflow insights
- adjacent products around purchasing, parts visibility, or branch-contractor
  coordination

### Highest-probability path

The highest-probability path is:

**Use your brother's owner access as the channel, and your distribution
experience as the credibility layer and product insight engine.**

That gives you all three things an early vertical software business needs:

- warm distribution
- domain expertise
- clear operational pain

### Practical next steps

1. Make a target list of 15 owners your brother can introduce you to.
2. Ask each owner the same 8 to 10 questions about admin bottlenecks, missed
   revenue, and repetitive office work.
3. Rank pains by frequency, cost, and urgency.
4. Pick one use case where ROI can be shown inside 30 days.
5. Sell a paid pilot before overbuilding software.

### Final recommendation

If choosing between the two, **bet on the local owner network first**.

It is the faster route to conversations, clearer route to revenue, and better
fit for a founder with strong HVAC operations context but no audience. Your
distribution experience should shape the offer and sharpen your edge, but your
brother's service-owner access should drive the initial go-to-market motion.
