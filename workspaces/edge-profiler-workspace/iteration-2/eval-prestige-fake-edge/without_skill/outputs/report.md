# Fintech SaaS Edge Assessment

## Bottom line

You probably **can** figure out a fintech SaaS, but not because of McKinsey
prestige, smart friends, or broad fintech reading alone. Those inputs are useful
for pattern recognition and taste, yet they are usually a **fake edge** unless
they convert into repeated access to painful, specific workflows and buyers.

## What is real vs. fake edge here

### Real assets you have

- Strong general problem-structuring ability.
- High comfort with complex, regulated, enterprise-like domains.
- Broad fintech map of market structure, players, and trends.
- A likely network of capable operators who can give fast feedback.

### Likely fake edges

- "I know smart people": helpful for ideas and introductions, not enough for
  distribution.
- "I read a lot about fintech": useful context, but reading rarely reveals
  urgent workflow pain.
- McKinsey brand: can open some doors, but does not substitute for customer
  pull.
- General ambition to do "a fintech SaaS": too broad to produce fast learning.

## What this implies

Your best path is not to invent a big fintech thesis from first principles. It
is to use your analytical strengths to run a **tight search process** for
narrow, costly problems in adjacent operational workflows.

Good targets are usually:

- Repetitive and painful.
- Owned by a clear buyer.
- Expensive to do manually.
- Frequent enough to matter.
- Messy because of data, compliance, reconciliation, exceptions, or vendor
  sprawl.

## Where someone like you may actually have an edge

You may outperform on problems that sit between:

- finance and operations,
- compliance and workflow,
- vendors and internal teams,
- fragmented data and decision-making.

Examples of categories worth probing:

- Compliance operations for fintechs or banks.
- B2B onboarding / KYB / periodic review workflows.
- Reconciliation, exception handling, and audit prep.
- Treasury, cash visibility, or payment operations tooling.
- FP&A / finance ops software for fintech-adjacent companies.
- Internal tooling for risk, controls, or vendor management.

## What not to do

- Do not start with a trendy macro theme like embedded finance or stablecoins
  and work backward.
- Do not assume network prestige will create distribution.
- Do not spend months reading instead of getting workflow-level detail.
- Do not target a buyer you cannot reach weekly.

## Recommended search strategy

For the next 4-6 weeks:

1. Pick 2-3 narrow buyer types you can actually reach.
2. Do 20-30 interviews focused on current workflow, not idea pitching.
3. Look for repeated manual work, exception queues, spreadsheet handoffs, and
   compliance burden.
4. Rank opportunities by pain intensity, frequency, willingness to pay, and ease
   of access.
5. Build only after you hear the same painful process from multiple similar
   buyers.

## Practical screening questions

Use these to test whether an opportunity is real:

- Who loses time or money every week because of this?
- Is there a single team with budget authority?
- Is the pain acute enough that they already use spreadsheets, BPOs, or clumsy
  software?
- Can you get 10 more conversations with this persona quickly?
- Can a simple v1 reduce labor, errors, or cycle time in a measurable way?

## Final assessment

Your current profile is **better than average for discovering a fintech SaaS**,
but mainly because it can support disciplined problem selection, not because it
gives you a built-in moat. If you turn your network and knowledge into repeated
exposure to one painful workflow, that becomes a real edge. If not, it remains
smart-person theater.

## Recommendation in one sentence

Treat your prestige and fintech knowledge as a door-opener for customer
discovery, not as proof that you already have a startup edge.
