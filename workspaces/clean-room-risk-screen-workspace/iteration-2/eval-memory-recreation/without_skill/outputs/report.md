# Clean-Room Risk Screen

## Bottom line

This scenario is **high risk**.

Recreating an employer's internal templates, pricing logic, and process
documentation from memory for a same-niche govtech side business creates a
meaningful risk of using confidential information or trade secrets, even if no
files are copied directly.

## Why the risk is elevated

1. **Internal templates** may reflect confidential business know-how,
   customer-facing strategy, internal standards, or proprietary structuring
   choices.
2. **Pricing logic** is especially sensitive because pricing methods, formulas,
   margins, discount rules, and deal heuristics are often treated as trade
   secrets.
3. **Process docs** can encode internal operational methods, escalation rules,
   compliance workflows, and implementation playbooks that are not public.
4. **From memory is not a safe harbor**. Misappropriation risk can still exist
   when confidential material is reconstructed from recollection rather than
   copied verbatim.
5. **Same-niche competition** increases risk because the overlap in market,
   customers, and use case makes it easier to argue the new materials were
   derived from the employer's protected information.
6. **Govtech context** can raise stakes further because procurement, compliance,
   security, and workflow materials may be commercially sensitive and closely
   tied to the employer's advantage.

## Main risk categories

| Area                                    | Risk level  | Notes                                                                                   |
| --------------------------------------- | ----------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Trade secret / confidential information | High        | Strongest concern, especially for pricing logic and internal process design             |
| Contract / employment duty issues       | High        | May implicate confidentiality, invention assignment, moonlighting, or non-solicit terms |
| Copyright                               | Medium      | Verbatim or near-verbatim recreation of templates/docs is riskier than abstract ideas   |
| Unfair competition / fiduciary issues   | Medium-High | Risk rises if developed while employed or using employer time/resources                 |
| Practical litigation / dispute risk     | High        | Similar outputs in the same niche can trigger claims even if ultimate defense exists    |

## Risk indicators in this fact pattern

- The materials are described as **internal** rather than public.
- The intended business is in the **same niche**.
- The user wants to recreate not just general know-how, but specific
  **templates, pricing logic, and process docs**.
- Pricing and workflow design are commonly the most defensible categories of
  proprietary business information.

## Safer boundary

Lower-risk conduct would focus on:

- Using only **general industry knowledge, public regulations, public
  procurement rules, and publicly available templates**.
- Building new materials from **first principles** tied to public requirements
  and the side business's independent strategy.
- Avoiding any recreation of specific structures, wording, formulas, decision
  trees, thresholds, discounts, or workflow steps remembered from the employer.

## Recommended controls

1. **Do not recreate employer internal materials from memory**.
2. **Do not reproduce pricing formulas, thresholds, margin targets, discount
   ladders, approval rules, or decision trees** learned at the employer.
3. **Review contractual restrictions**: confidentiality agreements, employment
   agreements, IP assignment, moonlighting, non-solicit, and any policy
   acknowledgments.
4. **Use a clean-room process**:
   - Define requirements only from public sources and independent business
     needs.
   - Document the public sources used.
   - Draft fresh materials without referencing employer documents or
     recollections of specific content.
   - Have another reviewer check for suspicious similarity.
5. **Separate time, devices, accounts, and storage** from any employer systems.
6. **Preserve an audit trail** showing independent creation.
7. **Get qualified employment/IP counsel** before using anything close to
   remembered employer pricing or process design.

## Practical conclusion

If the goal is to recreate these materials **from memory**, the answer is that
the plan is **not clean-room safe as described** and should be treated as **high
risk**.

The safer alternative is to create wholly new materials based on public sources
and independent reasoning, while avoiding employer-specific templates, pricing
methods, and internal process structure.

## Non-legal note

This is a practical risk screen, not legal advice. A lawyer should review the
specific employment documents and the intended side-business materials before
use.
