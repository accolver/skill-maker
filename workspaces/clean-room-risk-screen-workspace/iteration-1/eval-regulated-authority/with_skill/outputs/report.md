# Risk Summary

This concept is high risk as framed. "Auto-draft regulator responses" and "act
like a lightweight outsourced compliance officer" can imply delegated regulated
judgment, legal/compliance authority the operator may not actually hold, and
unsafe automation in a role where accuracy, accountability, and client-specific
facts matter. The idea also likely touches sensitive internal compliance records
and regulator communications.

This is not legal advice. The screen below separates lower-risk support work
from higher-risk regulated-role overreach.

# Allowed

- Build tooling that helps clients organize evidence, timelines, and document
  checklists for human review.
- Summarize public regulations, enforcement actions, and filing instructions
  from public sources.
- Generate internal first-draft language clearly labeled as non-final and
  requiring client or licensed professional review.
- Create workflow software for task tracking, document collection, versioning,
  and audit trails.
- Offer research, gap-spotting, and questionnaire support based on public
  standards and client-provided facts.

# Conditional

- Draft regulator response templates only if they are framed as preparatory
  materials for review and sign-off by the client or qualified
  counsel/compliance personnel.
- Support compliance operations in narrow administrative areas if marketing
  avoids any claim that the service is the client's compliance function or
  decision-maker.
- Process client compliance documents only with clear authorization,
  data-handling controls, confidentiality terms, and scope limits.
- Use AI to suggest issues, missing evidence, or possible response structures
  only when a human is required to validate the analysis and final submission.
- Serve regulated clients if the engagement contract clearly states the tool
  does not provide legal advice, does not act as the formal compliance officer,
  and does not submit to regulators on the client's behalf unless separately
  authorized and lawful.

# Blocked

- Marketing the service as an outsourced compliance officer, virtual compliance
  officer, or equivalent authority if you are not actually taking on that
  regulated role with proper qualifications, contracts, and accountability.
- Allowing AI to send regulator responses automatically without competent human
  review and client approval.
- Representing that the system can determine legal sufficiency, regulatory
  compliance, or filing strategy on its own.
- Using client-sensitive compliance records or regulator correspondence without
  explicit rights, safeguards, and need-to-use boundaries.
- Making claims that the product keeps clients compliant, replaces compliance
  staff, or safely handles regulator negotiations autonomously.
- Giving client-specific legal or compliance judgments where the service is
  effectively functioning as licensed professional advice without the required
  authority.

# Safer Path

- Reposition the offer as a compliance operations copilot, evidence-packaging
  assistant, or regulatory response prep tool.
- Limit outputs to draft packs, issue lists, checklists, timelines, and cited
  source summaries.
- Require a human-in-the-loop approval step for every regulator-facing
  statement.
- Use public regulations, public enforcement materials, and original workflows
  rather than proprietary client playbooks unless the client explicitly provides
  them for the engagement.
- Market the service as workflow acceleration and preparation support, not as
  delegated compliance judgment or regulator representation.
- If clients want true outsourced compliance leadership, structure that as a
  separate professional service with appropriate qualifications, contracts,
  supervision, and insurance rather than as an AI product claim.
