# Risk Screen: Using Employer Materials for a Side Business

## Scenario

The user wants to use employer internal templates, pricing logic, and process
documents from a govtech job to launch a side business in the same niche.

## Bottom Line

Risk is **very high**. Using employer internal materials for a side business in
the same niche creates serious exposure under confidentiality, trade secret,
intellectual property, duty of loyalty, moonlighting, unfair competition, and
possible public-sector ethics or procurement rules. The safest screen result is
**do not use the employer's internal materials, logic, or documents**.

## Why The Risk Is High

1. **Internal templates and process docs are likely confidential**. Even if they
   do not look novel, internal operating materials are commonly protected by
   confidentiality agreements, handbook rules, and trade secret law if they
   provide business value from not being public.

2. **Pricing logic is especially sensitive**. Internal pricing methods, margins,
   bid strategy, discount thresholds, and qualification logic are often among
   the most protected employer assets. Reusing them in a competing or adjacent
   business creates unusually strong misappropriation arguments.

3. **Same niche increases competitive conflict**. Launching in the same govtech
   niche makes it easier to argue the side business is competitive, disloyal, or
   unfairly advantaged by employer know-how.

4. **Govtech context can add extra constraints**. Public sector work may involve
   procurement integrity rules, restrictions tied to government customer
   relationships, data handling obligations, security requirements, and ethics
   rules that increase the seriousness of reuse.

5. **"I changed the wording" is usually not enough**. Reformatting,
   paraphrasing, or lightly adapting employer materials usually does not
   eliminate the risk if the substance, structure, decision rules, or
   confidential know-how came from internal documents.

## Specific Risk By Material Type

| Material                               | Risk      | Notes                                                                       |
| -------------------------------------- | --------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Internal templates                     | High      | May be copyrighted, confidential, and evidence of copying even if edited.   |
| Pricing logic                          | Very High | Often core trade secret or confidential business information.               |
| Process docs                           | High      | Can embed internal methods, controls, and operational know-how.             |
| Customer-specific knowledge            | Very High | May implicate confidentiality, procurement, and relationship misuse.        |
| General industry know-how in your head | Medium    | Safer only if truly general, non-confidential, and independently expressed. |

## Practical Screen Result

**Red light** for using employer materials directly or indirectly.

Avoid all of the following:

1. Copying files, text, tables, checklists, workflows, formulas, or bid logic.
2. Recreating documents from memory when the memory is of internal employer
   materials.
3. Using employer pricing assumptions, segmentation rules, margin targets, or
   proposal structure.
4. Using employer customer lists, pipeline details, contacts, nonpublic
   requirements, or lessons learned.
5. Building the side business while still employed if company policy restricts
   outside work or competing activity.

## Safer Path

If the user wants to pursue the business with lower risk:

1. Build materials from a **clean room** process using only public sources,
   independently developed frameworks, and original drafting.
2. Avoid the same niche while still employed, especially where offerings
   overlap.
3. Review employment agreements, confidentiality agreements, invention
   assignment clauses, moonlighting policy, code of conduct, and any non-solicit
   or non-compete language if applicable.
4. Avoid using employer devices, accounts, time, data, or software.
5. Get advice from an employment/IP attorney before launch if the user intends
   to operate in the same market.

## Key Uncertainties

The exact risk level could increase further depending on:

1. Contract language signed by the employee.
2. Whether the side business would compete for the same customers.
3. Whether any government data, security-controlled information, or
   procurement-sensitive information is involved.
4. Whether the employer already has side-work disclosure or approval
   requirements.

## Final Screen

- Overall risk: **Very High**
- Recommendation: **Do not use employer internal templates, pricing logic, or
  process docs**
- Preferred alternative: **Independent clean-room creation from public and
  original sources**

## Note

This is a practical risk screen, not legal advice.
