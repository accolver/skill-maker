# Risk Summary

This plan is high risk because it explicitly depends on employer internal
templates, pricing logic, and process documentation to start a side business in
the same govtech niche. Those materials are likely employer-confidential, may be
protected as trade secrets or proprietary know-how, and are the kind of assets
most likely to create immediate IP, confidentiality, and employment-conflict
problems.

This is not formal legal advice, but the practical screen is clear: using those
internal materials directly is not a clean-room path.

# Allowed

- Using generalized industry knowledge and professional judgment that lives in
  your head, without copying employer documents, wording, structure, formulas,
  or internal methods.
- Researching the govtech market using public procurement documents, public
  agency guidance, public RFPs, public budgets, public award data, and public
  competitor materials.
- Creating brand-new templates, workflows, checklists, and playbooks from
  scratch using public sources and independent reasoning.
- Serving a different niche or narrower workflow if the offer can be delivered
  without employer-confidential information or restricted customer access.
- Offering human-in-the-loop support such as research, drafting assistance,
  documentation prep, or implementation support when the work does not require
  misusing confidential know-how or overstating authority.

# Conditional

- Launching a side business in the same broad sector only if your employment
  agreement, invention assignment terms, moonlighting rules, confidentiality
  obligations, and non-solicitation restrictions allow it.
- Building pricing for the new business only if it is independently developed
  from public market inputs, your own testing, and original business assumptions
  rather than employer pricing logic.
- Creating templates in a similar problem area only if they are genuinely
  original and not derived from employer forms, field names, document structure,
  approval logic, or internal process steps.
- Marketing to potential customers in the niche only if you are not soliciting
  accounts, contacts, or pipeline relationships you only know through your
  employer and are not using confidential customer intelligence.
- Offering process guidance only if you avoid regulated-role overreach and do
  not imply official, legal, procurement, or compliance authority you do not
  have.

# Blocked

- Reusing employer internal templates, forms, checklists, proposal structures,
  spreadsheets, or playbooks.
- Reusing employer pricing logic, rate cards, margin rules, bundling strategy,
  discount thresholds, bid strategy, or other internal commercial logic.
- Reusing employer process docs, SOPs, implementation methods, customer-specific
  lessons, internal decision trees, or operational documentation.
- Copying or lightly rewriting employer content, even if you change formatting,
  labels, or examples.
- Using customer information, account history, procurement intelligence, or
  relationship access obtained through your job for the side business.
- Positioning the business as if it has privileged insider methods, official
  standing, or automation that can safely replace regulated or accountable human
  judgment.

# Safer Path

- Start with a clean-room offer based only on public-data research products and
  original assets created from scratch.
- Pick a narrow pain point in govtech that can be addressed through generalized
  workflow support rather than employer-specific methods.
- Build new templates from a blank page after reviewing only public materials,
  and document the sources used to show independent creation.
- Develop pricing from scratch using public competitor signals, customer
  interviews, your own cost model, and live testing.
- Keep the service human-in-the-loop: research, evidence gathering, drafting
  assistance, and workflow preparation are safer than delegated judgment or
  insider-style claims.
- If there is any overlap with your current employer’s market, review your
  employment documents before launch and avoid current customers, restricted
  prospects, and any employer-confidential material.
