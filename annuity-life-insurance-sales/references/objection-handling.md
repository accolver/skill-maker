# Objection Handling Guide for Annuity & Life Insurance Sales

## The A-E-R Framework

Every objection follows the same response pattern:

1. **Acknowledge** — Validate their concern. Never dismiss or argue.
2. **Empathize** — Show you understand why they feel that way.
3. **Redirect** — Use a question, story, or fact to reframe the concern.

**Critical rules:**

- An objection is a buying signal — it means they're engaged
- Never say "but" after acknowledging — it negates everything before it
- Use "and" instead: "I understand your concern, AND here's what I've found..."
- Ask permission before redirecting: "May I share something that might help?"
- If you don't know the answer, say so. "That's a great question. Let me
  research that and get back to you tomorrow."

## Annuity Objections

### "I hate annuities" / "I've heard annuities are bad"

**Why they say it:** Media pundits (Suze Orman, Dave Ramsey) have painted ALL
annuities with the same brush, usually criticizing variable annuities with high
fees.

**Response:** "I appreciate your honesty. May I ask — which type of annuity are
you referring to? There are actually several very different types, and the ones
I work with have zero fees and guarantee you can never lose money. They're quite
different from what you might have heard about on TV. Would you be open to
learning how they work so you can decide for yourself?"

**Key points to make:**

- There are over 10 types of annuities — condemning all of them is like saying
  "I hate all medicine"
- Fixed and fixed indexed annuities have NO annual fees (unlike variable
  annuities)
- Fixed annuities are the ONLY financial product that can guarantee income for
  life
- Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway is one of the largest annuity issuers in
  the world

---

### "I need to think about it"

**Why they say it:** Usually means they have an unresolved concern they haven't
voiced, or they want to check with someone else.

**Response:** "Of course — this is an important decision and I want you to be
completely comfortable. Help me understand: what specifically would you like to
think about? Is it the product itself, the amount, or something else? I want to
make sure I've given you all the information you need to make your decision."

**Follow-up if they can't articulate it:** "Many of my clients have found it
helpful to sleep on it and we revisit next week. But before we wrap up, on a
scale of 1-10, how comfortable are you with what we discussed? What would make
it a 10?"

---

### "I can get better returns in the market"

**Why they say it:** They're comparing annuities to equity returns, which is the
wrong comparison.

**Response:** "You might be right — over a long period, the stock market has
historically returned 8-10%. But let me ask you this: can the stock market
guarantee you'll never lose money? Can it guarantee you income for life
regardless of what happens? An annuity isn't competing with the market — it's
replacing the pension your parents had. You'd never compare your pension to the
stock market. This is your paycheck — guaranteed income to cover your bills.
Your other investments can still chase market returns. That's your playcheck
money."

**The math reframe:** "If you retire with $500,000 in the market and it drops
30% in year one, you now have $350,000. Even if it recovers 30% the next year,
you only have $455,000. And if you've been withdrawing $40,000/year for income,
it's much worse. That's sequence of returns risk, and it's destroyed more
retirement plans than any other factor."

---

### "What if the insurance company goes bankrupt?"

**Why they say it:** Legitimate concern — they want to know their money is safe.

**Response:** "That's a very important question, and I'm glad you asked.
Insurance companies are among the most heavily regulated financial institutions
in America — far more regulated than banks. Each state has a guaranty
association that protects policyholders, similar to FDIC for banks. The limits
vary by state but are typically $250,000-$500,000 per contract. Additionally, I
only recommend carriers with A-rated or better financial strength ratings from
AM Best. These companies have survived the Great Depression, 2008, and COVID
without missing a single payment to policyholders."

**Key facts:**

- No policyholder of an A-rated insurance company has ever lost money due to
  company failure
- State guaranty associations provide backstop protection
- Insurance company reserves are regulated and stress-tested annually
- Insurance companies survived every financial crisis in US history

---

### "I can't afford to lock up my money" / "I need liquidity"

**Response:** "I completely understand — liquidity is important, and I would
never recommend putting money somewhere you can't access it when you need it.
That's actually why the first thing we did was map out your complete financial
picture. We identified $_____ in liquid savings and emergency funds that we're
NOT touching. The amount we're discussing for the annuity is money you've
identified as not needing for at least [surrender period] years. And even within
the annuity, most contracts allow you to withdraw up to 10% per year without any
penalty. Does that address your concern?"

---

### "The fees are too high"

**Response (for fixed/FIA):** "That's a fair concern with some annuity types.
But the fixed indexed annuity I'm recommending has zero annual fees on the base
contract. The only optional fee is for the income rider, which is [X]% per year
and guarantees you lifetime income. Let me show you what that fee buys you —
[calculate the value of guaranteed lifetime income vs. the fee]. Most clients
find that the guaranteed income is worth many times more than the fee."

**Response (if asked about variable annuities):** "You're right that variable
annuities can have significant fees — often 2-3% per year. That's actually why I
recommend fixed indexed annuities instead. They provide similar upside potential
with zero base fees and lower overall costs."

---

### "I'm too young for an annuity" / "Annuities are for old people"

**Response:** "I understand why you might think that — annuities are often
associated with retirement income. But here's an interesting fact: the earlier
you start, the more powerful the guarantees become. If you start an FIA with an
income rider at age 55, that guaranteed income base can grow at 7-8% for 10
years before you start taking income. By 65, your guaranteed lifetime income
could be significantly higher than if you waited. The cost of waiting is real
and measurable."

---

### "My financial advisor told me not to buy annuities"

**Response:** "I respect that your advisor has your best interests at heart. May
I ask — is your advisor a fee-based advisor who manages your investments?
[Usually yes.] Here's something to consider: when you move money into an
annuity, your advisor stops earning fees on that money. I'm not suggesting they
have bad intentions, but there is a structural incentive for them to keep your
money under management. The question isn't whether annuities are good or bad —
it's whether having guaranteed income for life, regardless of what the market
does, gives you more peace of mind. Would it be helpful if your advisor and I
had a conversation together?"

---

### "What if rates go up after I buy?"

**Response:** "Great question. Rates could go up, but they could also go down —
nobody knows for sure. Here's what I recommend: instead of trying to time the
market, we use a laddering strategy. We might put a portion in a 3-year MYGA, a
portion in a 5-year, and a portion in a 7-year. As each one matures, you can
reinvest at whatever rate is available. This way, you're always participating in
current rates without betting everything on one moment in time."

---

### "I want to leave money to my kids/heirs"

**Response:** "That's a wonderful goal, and annuities can actually help with
that. First, most annuities pass to your beneficiaries — they're not 'use it or
lose it.' If you pass away, your beneficiary receives the remaining account
value. Second, let me show you something powerful: if we use an annuity to
guarantee your income and reduce your withdrawal rate from investments, your
investment portfolio actually has a BETTER chance of growing for your heirs.
You're spending the annuity income instead of depleting your investments. Want
me to show you the numbers?"

## Life Insurance Objections

### "I can't afford life insurance"

**Response:** "I hear you. Let me ask you this: can your family afford to be
without your income if something happens to you? The average cost of a $500,000
term policy for a healthy 40-year-old is about $25-30 per month — less than your
cell phone bill. And we can often find the money in your existing budget by
reviewing expenses you may not even realize you're paying. Would you like me to
show you how?"

---

### "I already have life insurance through work"

**Response:** "That's great that your employer provides coverage. How much is it
— usually 1-2 times your salary? [Yes.] So if you earn $80,000, you have about
$80,000-$160,000 in coverage. If something happened to you, how long would that
last your family? Maybe 2-3 years? And here's the bigger issue: what happens if
you leave that job? Group coverage ends when your employment ends. Having your
own portable policy means you're covered regardless of where you work."

---

### "I'm too old for life insurance"

**Response:** "I understand the concern. The good news is that rates have become
much more competitive for older adults, and there are products specifically
designed for estate planning and legacy purposes. At your age, life insurance
isn't about income replacement — it's about leaving a tax-free legacy, covering
final expenses, or funding a charitable gift. Let me show you some options that
might surprise you."

---

### "My spouse and I can self-insure"

**Response:** "That's a great position to be in — having enough assets to
self-insure. But consider this: if one of you passes away, Social Security
benefits drop by up to 50%. Your investment portfolio takes a hit from estate
costs. And the surviving spouse now has to cover all expenses on a reduced
income. Life insurance, especially permanent insurance, can provide a tax-free
lump sum that replaces the lost Social Security income and preserves your
estate. It's actually more capital-efficient than self-insuring."

---

## Universal Response Principles

1. **Never get defensive** — The client isn't attacking you personally
2. **Ask questions, don't lecture** — "What specifically concerns you?" is more
   powerful than a 10-minute monologue
3. **Use stories** — "I had a client in a similar situation who..." is more
   persuasive than data alone
4. **Agree first** — Finding common ground disarms resistance
5. **Know when to stop** — If the client truly isn't interested, respect that.
   Push too hard and you lose the referral too.
6. **Follow up** — "I need to think about it" plus no follow-up = lost sale.
   Always schedule the next conversation before leaving.
