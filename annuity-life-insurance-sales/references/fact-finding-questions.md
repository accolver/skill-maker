# Fact-Finding Question Bank for Insurance Sales

## How to Use This Guide

Never read these questions like a checklist in front of the client. Internalize
them and use them conversationally. The goal is a natural dialogue where the
client feels heard, not interrogated. Listen to each answer before asking the
next question — their answers should guide your follow-ups.

**Key principle:** The fact-find is not about YOU gathering data. It's about
THEM seeing their own situation clearly. When done right, the client discovers
their own problem.

---

## Opening / Rapport Building

Use these to set a comfortable tone before getting into financial details.

- "Before we dive into numbers, tell me a little about yourself. What do you
  enjoy doing?"
- "What does an ideal day in retirement look like for you?"
- "What prompted you to take this meeting today?"
- "What's the most important thing I can help you with today?"
- "Have you worked with a financial professional before? What was that
  experience like?"
- "Is there anything specific you've heard about that you'd like to discuss?"

---

## Personal & Family Information

- "Tell me about your family situation — are you married? Do you have children
  or grandchildren?"
- "How old are you and your spouse? When are your birthdays?"
- "How is your health? Your spouse's health? Any chronic conditions?"
- "Are there any family members who depend on you financially?"
- "Do you have aging parents who might need care?"
- "Where do you plan to live in retirement? Any plans to move or downsize?"

---

## Income & Employment

- "Are you currently working? What do you do?"
- "When do you plan to fully retire?"
- "What is your current annual household income?"
- "Do you have a pension? If so, what are the payout options?"
- "Have you started Social Security? If not, when do you plan to start?"
- "What is your estimated Social Security benefit? Your spouse's?"
- "Do you have any other sources of income — rental property, part-time work,
  royalties?"
- "If you retired today, what would your guaranteed monthly income be?"

---

## Assets & Savings

- "Walk me through your savings and investments. Let's start with retirement
  accounts."
- "Do you have an IRA? 401(k)? 403(b)? What are the approximate balances?"
- "Are these traditional (pre-tax) or Roth accounts?"
- "Do you have any brokerage or investment accounts outside of retirement
  plans?"
- "What about savings accounts, CDs, or money market accounts?"
- "Do you own any existing annuities? If so, what type and with which company?"
- "Do you have any cash value life insurance?"
- "Do you own real estate beyond your primary home?"
- "Are any of these accounts generating income currently?"
- "Do you have a required minimum distribution (RMD) starting soon?"

---

## Expenses & Liabilities

- "What are your monthly essential expenses — housing, utilities, food,
  insurance, healthcare?"
- "What about discretionary expenses — travel, dining out, hobbies, gifts?"
- "Do you carry a mortgage? What's the balance and monthly payment?"
- "Any other debts — car loans, credit cards, student loans?"
- "Do you anticipate any large expenses in the next 5-10 years?"
- "What do you spend on healthcare currently? Do you have a plan for Medicare?"
- "Have you thought about long-term care costs?"

---

## Risk Tolerance & Investment Experience

- "How would you describe your comfort level with market risk?"
- "When the market dropped 34% in March 2020, what did you do with your
  investments?"
- "What about during 2008? Did you sell, hold, or buy more?"
- "If your account dropped 20% tomorrow, how would you feel? What would you do?"
- "Are you more concerned about missing growth opportunities or losing what
  you've saved?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, with 1 being 'I want zero risk' and 10 being 'I'm
  comfortable with full market exposure,' where do you fall?"
- "Have you ever lost money in an investment? How did that experience affect
  you?"

---

## Retirement Goals & Vision

- "What does retirement look like for you? Paint me a picture of your ideal
  day."
- "What are the top 3 things you want to do in retirement?"
- "How much monthly income would you need to maintain your current lifestyle?"
- "How much would you need for the lifestyle you WANT?"
- "Do you plan to travel? How frequently and what kind
  (domestic/international)?"
- "Do you want to leave an inheritance? How important is that to you?"
- "Are there any charitable organizations you'd like to support?"
- "What's your biggest fear about retirement?"
- "What would make you feel financially secure?"

---

## Insurance & Protection

- "Do you have life insurance currently? How much and what type?"
- "Is it through your employer or personally owned?"
- "What happens to your life insurance when you retire?"
- "Does your spouse have life insurance?"
- "Do you have long-term care insurance?"
- "If you needed nursing home care, how would you pay for it?"
- "Do you have an estate plan — will, trust, power of attorney?"
- "Are your beneficiary designations up to date on all accounts?"
- "When was the last time you reviewed your insurance coverage?"

---

## Tax Situation

- "What tax bracket are you in currently?"
- "Do you work with a CPA or tax professional?"
- "Do you anticipate your tax bracket going up or down in retirement?"
- "Have you considered Roth conversions?"
- "Are RMDs a concern for you? Do you need the income or are they forced
  distributions?"
- "Do you have any capital gains or loss carryforwards?"
- "Are there any tax strategies you've been considering?"

---

## Decision-Making Process

- "Is your spouse involved in financial decisions? Should they be at our next
  meeting?"
- "Do you work with any other financial advisors, CPAs, or attorneys?"
- "How do you typically make financial decisions — quickly or do you like to
  research?"
- "What information would you need to feel comfortable moving forward?"
- "Is there anyone else you'd want to consult before making a decision?"
- "What's your timeline for making a decision on this?"

---

## Closing Discovery Questions (Transition to Analysis)

These questions summarize what you've learned and transition to the next
meeting.

- "Based on everything we've discussed, it sounds like your biggest concerns are
  [X, Y, Z]. Is that accurate?"
- "If I could show you a way to guarantee your essential expenses are covered
  for life, regardless of what the market does, would that be of interest?"
- "What I'd like to do is take all of this information back to my office,
  analyze it, and come back with some options tailored specifically to your
  situation. Can we schedule that meeting for [date]?"
- "Is there anything else I should know that we haven't covered?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that your current plan will get you
  through retirement? What would make it a 10?"

## Red Flags to Watch For

| Red Flag                                                | What It Means                                             | What to Do                                                                              |
| ------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Client doesn't want spouse present                      | Spouse may object or be unaware of finances               | Insist spouse be at next meeting — it's for their protection                            |
| Client is under pressure from another advisor           | May be getting competing advice                           | Don't badmouth the other advisor; focus on your unique value                            |
| Client says "I have plenty of money"                    | May be overconfident or hiding information                | Gently probe: "That's great — help me understand what 'plenty' means to you"            |
| Client is vague about assets/income                     | May not trust you yet, or may not know                    | Build more rapport before pressing; offer to review statements together                 |
| Client wants to move everything immediately             | Could be emotionally reactive (recent loss, market scare) | Slow down — ensure suitability. Never let urgency bypass due diligence                  |
| Client has existing annuity with high surrender charges | Replacement may not be suitable                           | Document why replacement is in their interest; consider waiting for surrender to expire |
