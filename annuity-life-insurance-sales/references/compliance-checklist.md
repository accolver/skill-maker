# Compliance & Regulatory Checklist for Annuity & Life Insurance Sales

## Critical Disclaimer

This guide provides general compliance guidance for licensed insurance
professionals. State regulations vary significantly. Always verify requirements
with your state's Department of Insurance and your broker-dealer/IMO compliance
department. This is NOT legal advice.

---

## Agent Licensing & Contracting Requirements

### Before You Can Sell

- [ ] Active state life and health insurance license in client's state of
      residence
- [ ] Appointed with the specific carrier whose product you're selling
- [ ] Errors & Omissions (E&O) insurance current and adequate
- [ ] Anti-Money Laundering (AML) training completed (annual requirement)
- [ ] Annuity suitability training completed (most states require this)
- [ ] Product-specific training completed (required by most carriers)
- [ ] CE (Continuing Education) credits current — check state requirements
- [ ] For variable products: Series 6 or 7 license, plus state securities
      registration
- [ ] Background check/fingerprinting clear (if applicable for new appointments)
- [ ] Non-resident licenses if selling across state lines

### Carrier Contracting Checklist

When getting appointed with a new carrier:

- [ ] Completed carrier contracting application
- [ ] W-9 tax form
- [ ] E&O insurance certificate
- [ ] State license verification
- [ ] AML training certificate
- [ ] Product training certificates
- [ ] Direct deposit/commission payment setup
- [ ] Agent agreement signed
- [ ] Compliance acknowledgement signed

## Suitability Requirements (NAIC Model Regulation)

### The Suitability Standard — Non-Negotiable

The NAIC Suitability in Annuity Transactions Model Regulation (adopted in most
states) requires that every annuity recommendation must:

1. **Be suitable** based on the client's documented financial situation
2. **Be documented** — you must record the basis for your recommendation
3. **Consider the following factors:**
   - Age
   - Annual income
   - Financial situation and needs (including debts and other obligations)
   - Financial experience
   - Financial objectives
   - Intended use of the annuity
   - Financial time horizon
   - Existing assets and financial products (including insurance)
   - Liquidity needs
   - Liquid net worth
   - Risk tolerance (including willingness to accept non-guaranteed elements)
   - Tax status

### Best Interest Standard (Updated NAIC Model)

Many states have adopted the updated "best interest" standard which adds:

- The agent must act in the client's **best interest** at the time of
  recommendation
- The agent must **not** place their own financial interest ahead of the
  client's
- Must disclose the agent's role, compensation, and material conflicts of
  interest
- Must document and substantiate the recommendation

### Suitability Documentation Required for Every Sale

- [ ] Completed financial needs analysis / fact-finder
- [ ] Client signed suitability questionnaire
- [ ] Written explanation of why the product is suitable
- [ ] Documentation of alternatives considered
- [ ] Disclosure of fees, charges, and surrender penalties
- [ ] Disclosure of agent compensation (if required by state)
- [ ] If replacing existing product: documented reason why replacement is in
      client's best interest

## Application Process Checklist

### Required Forms for Every Annuity Sale

- [ ] **Application** — Complete, accurate, signed by client (and spouse if
      applicable)
- [ ] **Suitability form** — Financial needs analysis with all required fields
- [ ] **Product disclosure** — Carrier-specific product summary/buyer's guide
- [ ] **Illustration** — Signed and dated by client, showing guaranteed and
      non-guaranteed values
- [ ] **Free-look notice** — Client acknowledges the free-look period (10-30
      days by state)
- [ ] **Privacy notice** — How client information will be used
- [ ] **Delivery receipt** — Client acknowledges receipt of policy (at delivery)

### Additional Forms for Specific Situations

**Replacement/Exchange (1035):**

- [ ] State replacement form (varies by state — some require two: one for
      existing carrier, one for new)
- [ ] Comparison statement showing old policy vs. new policy
- [ ] Client acknowledgement of surrender charges on existing contract
- [ ] Transfer/rollover authorization form
- [ ] Statement that replacement is in client's best interest

**Qualified Money (IRA/401k Rollover):**

- [ ] IRA application or transfer form
- [ ] 60-day rollover certification (if applicable)
- [ ] Direct rollover/transfer authorization
- [ ] Existing custodian's transfer paperwork
- [ ] RMD acknowledgement if client is over 73

**Trust-Owned Annuities:**

- [ ] Trust certification or full trust document
- [ ] Trustee identification and authority verification
- [ ] Trust tax ID number (EIN)

**Joint/Spousal Applications:**

- [ ] Both signatures on all required forms
- [ ] Both parties present or documented consent

## State-Specific Considerations

### High-Regulation States (Extra Requirements)

**California:**

- Suitability training every 4 years (8 hours)
- Specific disclosure requirements for senior consumers (65+)
- Enhanced replacement regulations

**New York (Regulation 60/187):**

- Best interest standard (most stringent in the country)
- Disclosure of compensation
- Comparison of proposed product vs. existing products
- Specific suitability documentation requirements
- Producer compensation transparency

**Florida:**

- 4-hour annuity suitability CE course required
- Enhanced senior protection (age 65+)
- Specific free-look provisions

**Texas:**

- Must provide buyer's guide for all annuity sales
- Suitability compliance officer designated by each insurer

### Senior-Specific Regulations

Many states have enhanced protections for clients over 65:

- Extended free-look periods (30 days vs. 10 days)
- Mandatory trusted contact person designation
- Cooling-off period requirements
- Enhanced suitability documentation
- Some states prohibit certain surrender periods for clients over a certain age

## Replacement Regulations

### When Is It a Replacement?

A **replacement** occurs when a new policy is purchased and an existing policy
is:

- Lapsed, forfeited, or surrendered
- Reduced in value by policy loan, withdrawal, or partial surrender
- Amended to reduce benefits or term
- Subject to a 1035 exchange
- Converted from one type to another

### Replacement Documentation Requirements

- [ ] Completed state replacement form
- [ ] Side-by-side comparison of old and new products
- [ ] Documentation of why replacement is in client's best interest
- [ ] Surrender charge analysis on existing product
- [ ] Tax implications documented (even in 1035 exchange)
- [ ] Notice to existing carrier (most states require)
- [ ] Client acknowledgement of all costs/penalties
- [ ] Agent attestation that replacement is suitable

### When NOT to Replace

- Existing product has substantial remaining surrender charges
- Client would lose valuable guarantees (e.g., high income rider rates no longer
  available)
- Tax consequences would be significant
- Health changes mean new coverage would be worse
- The primary benefit is to generate a new commission (churning — this is
  illegal)

## Anti-Money Laundering (AML) Compliance

### Requirements

- Complete AML training annually
- Know your customer (KYC) — verify identity
- Report suspicious activities via SAR (Suspicious Activity Report)
- Maintain transaction records
- Report cash transactions over $10,000 (Currency Transaction Report)

### Red Flags for Suspicious Activity

- Client wants to pay with large amounts of cash
- Client is evasive about source of funds
- Client structures transactions to avoid reporting thresholds
- Client is listed on OFAC sanctions list
- Third party is funding the annuity for no clear reason
- Client wants to purchase annuity and immediately surrender it

## Record Keeping Requirements

### What to Keep and How Long

| Document                           | Retention Period                  |
| ---------------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| Applications and suitability forms | Life of policy + 5-7 years        |
| Illustrations                      | Life of policy + 5-7 years        |
| Replacement forms                  | Life of policy + 5-7 years        |
| Client correspondence              | Life of policy + 3-5 years        |
| Meeting notes / fact-finders       | Life of policy + 5-7 years        |
| Training certifications            | During active licensing + 5 years |
| Commission statements              | 7 years (tax purposes)            |

**Best practice:** Keep everything for at least 7 years after the policy
terminates. Digital storage is acceptable in most states.

## Advertising & Marketing Compliance

### Rules for Agent Marketing

- [ ] All marketing materials must be approved by carrier or compliance
      department
- [ ] Cannot guarantee specific future returns on non-guaranteed products
- [ ] Must include appropriate disclaimers on all materials
- [ ] Cannot use misleading titles (e.g., "financial advisor" without proper
      registration)
- [ ] Seminar invitations must be approved before distribution
- [ ] Social media posts about products must be pre-approved
- [ ] Cannot offer inducements (gifts, rebates) to buy insurance
- [ ] Must clearly identify yourself as an insurance agent/producer
- [ ] Cannot use "no risk" or "guaranteed returns" for indexed products (the
      guarantee is 0% floor, not positive returns)

## Errors to Avoid (Career-Ending Mistakes)

| Error                                            | Consequence                                       |
| ------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Selling without proper license/appointment       | Policy voidable, fines, license revocation        |
| Skipping suitability documentation               | Fines, policy rescission, E&O claims              |
| Churning (replacing for commission)              | License revocation, criminal charges, restitution |
| Misrepresenting product features                 | E&O claims, fines, license suspension             |
| Forging client signatures                        | Criminal charges, permanent license revocation    |
| Commingling client funds                         | Criminal charges, license revocation              |
| Providing tax/legal advice without qualification | Liability exposure, regulatory action             |
| Failing to disclose surrender charges            | Client complaints, E&O claims, regulatory action  |
| Using unapproved marketing materials             | Carrier termination, regulatory fines             |
| Ignoring replacement regulations                 | Fines, policy rescission, license action          |
